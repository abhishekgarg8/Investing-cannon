# John Neff - Greatest Trades

As of 2026-06-28 (UTC), John B. Neff is deceased; he died on June 4, 2019, after a 31-year Windsor Fund career that made him one of the rare active mutual-fund managers whose record can be studied over a full market generation ([Philadelphia Inquirer](https://www.inquirer.com/business/john-neff-obituary-vanguard-windsor-funds-university-pennsylvania-endowment-fidelity-20190605.html); [CFA Institute](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/blogs/enterprising-investor/2019/in-memoriam-john-b-neff-cfa)). The greatest-trades record is unusually hard to reconstruct at security level because the most useful source, *John Neff on Investing*, is a diary-style book whose full page images were not available in this run; the opened Google Books record confirms that the book reproduces personal investment diaries and discusses the decisions behind best and worst investments, but does not expose full trade tables ([Google Books](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ)). I therefore separate first-person, primary-like evidence from book-reported and secondary figures, and I mark trade-level numbers that could not be cross-checked in primary Windsor annual reports.

## Executive Summary

Neff's best documented single stock trade is Ford, where a John Train passage quoted by Investment Masters reports that Windsor accumulated 12.3 million shares at an average price below $14, watched the stock reach $50 within roughly three years, and made almost $500 million [single-source, secondary quotation of book/Train material] ([Investment Masters](https://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2018/7/16/learning-from-john-neff)). The more representative Neff trade, however, was not a one-off home run. It was a repeatable pattern: buy dull or discredited companies at low P/E ratios when the negatives were visible, receive yield while waiting, and sell when the market stopped treating the company as damaged.

The strongest primary evidence comes from Neff's 2006 CFA Institute interview. He explicitly named Atlantic Richfield, Tandy/RadioShack, Hospital Corp., Browning-Ferris, early-1990s financial intermediaries, and beaten-down growth stocks such as Dell, Home Depot, IBM, Xerox, Seagate, Digital Equipment, and McDonald's as examples of the Windsor method at work ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). Those trades mattered because they show the shape of his edge: he was a contrarian, but not a reflexive one. He wanted a low price against a plausible fundamental recovery, not merely bad news.

Across the 31 years from 1964 to 1995, Neff's Windsor Fund compounded at about 13.7% annually versus 10.6% for the S&P 500, with a $75 million fund becoming $13.6 billion according to CFA Institute; Barron's separately reported a 5,546% cumulative total return ([CFA Institute](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/blogs/enterprising-investor/2019/in-memoriam-john-b-neff-cfa); [Barron's](https://www.barrons.com/articles/john-neff-51559786060)). Those are portfolio-level results, not proof of any one trade's P&L. They frame why the trades below deserve study.

## Source And Evidence Caveats

- Primary-like source: Neff's CFA Institute interview is the best opened source for named trade examples, process, and portfolio-level behavior. It does not provide share counts or dollar P&L for most trades.
- Book-derived source: Google Books confirms the scope and diary structure of *John Neff on Investing*, while Investment Masters, Base Hit Investing, and Stockunlock/LinkedIn provide book-derived case summaries. These are useful but must be treated as secondary until the book pages are checked.
- Missing primary records: No Neff-era Windsor annual report or portfolio schedule was recovered in this run. Where size, cost basis, or exit proceeds appear, they are explicitly labeled [single-source] or [book-reported].
- Trade definition: Several Neff successes were baskets or repeated round trips, not a single buy/sell line. I treat them as trades because Neff described them as deliberate portfolio themes.

## 1. Ford Motor Company - The Best Documented Dollar Winner

**Context & dates.** Ford had suffered through industry pessimism, import competition, and weak sentiment toward U.S. automakers in the years leading into 1984. A secondary Stockunlock summary of *John Neff on Investing* says Neff bought Ford at only about 2.5 times 1984 earnings and sold for an 85% gain by the end of 1985; Investment Masters, citing John Train's *Money Masters of Our Time*, gives the fuller book-reported version in which Neff began buying heavily in 1984 and the stock reached $50 roughly three years later ([Stockunlock/LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-john-neff-crushed-index-over-three-decades-stockunlock-ftvzc); [Investment Masters](https://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2018/7/16/learning-from-john-neff)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The Ford thesis was classic Neff: an out-of-favor cyclical with better fundamentals than the market price implied. Investment Masters' quoted Train passage says Neff emphasized Ford's low debt, large cash balance, cost culture, and better management quality versus General Motors, while Stockunlock highlights the 5% dividend as a protective yield and the low P/E as the entry point ([Investment Masters](https://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2018/7/16/learning-from-john-neff); [Stockunlock/LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-john-neff-crushed-index-over-three-decades-stockunlock-ftvzc)). This matches Neff's own stated rule that cyclical exposure required a compensating multiple, and that low-P/E candidates should have solid fundamentals and fundamental growth prospects ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Size & structure.** The size is reported as 12.3 million Ford shares accumulated at an average price below $14 [single-source, secondary quotation]. That implies a cost basis around $170 million before dividends, but the exact percentage of Windsor's assets was not verified in a primary fund schedule. A Business Insider/Stockopedia summary says Windsor was usually diversified around 1% positions but could go as high as 5% in especially attractive ideas; this is useful context, not proof that Ford hit that ceiling ([Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/john-neff-investing-screen-contrarian-thinking-mixing-low-pe-investing-and-the-total-return-ratio-2011-4)).

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** Entry was made amid popular disillusionment with U.S. autos. The trade worked because expectations were low enough that modestly better news mattered. No primary source recovered in this run gives a mark-to-market drawdown for the Ford position. The better-supported statement is that Ford was bought after a severe sentiment decline and later benefited from an improving economy and lower gasoline prices ([Stockunlock/LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-john-neff-crushed-index-over-three-decades-stockunlock-ftvzc)).

**Exit & P&L.** Investment Masters' Train quotation reports that Ford reached $50 and brought Windsor almost $500 million of profit [single-source]. Stockunlock's book summary gives a shorter holding period and an 85% gain by end-1985. These figures may refer to different sale tranches or reporting windows; absent primary annual reports, the safest conclusion is that Ford was the best documented absolute-dollar winner, but exact realized P&L remains [single-source/disputed-window] ([Investment Masters](https://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2018/7/16/learning-from-john-neff); [Stockunlock/LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-john-neff-crushed-index-over-three-decades-stockunlock-ftvzc)).

**What it teaches.** Neff's Ford trade is not a generic deep-value story. It is a lesson in paying a very low multiple for a cyclical business when balance-sheet strength, dividend yield, and management quality create a favorable asymmetry. The market did not need to fall in love with Ford; it only needed to stop pricing Ford as if the bad news had no endpoint.

**Sources.** Investment Masters/John Train quotation; Stockunlock book summary; CFA Institute interview; Business Insider/Stockopedia method summary.

## 2. Atlantic Richfield - The North Slope "Free Plus"

**Context & dates.** Neff used Atlantic Richfield as a first-person example of what he called a "free plus": a favorable development not paid for in the original analysis. In the CFA interview, he said Wellington owned Atlantic Richfield when it discovered oil on Alaska's North Slope. The U.S. Department of Energy confirms that Atlantic Richfield and Humble Oil announced the Prudhoe Bay North Slope discovery on March 13, 1968 ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [U.S. Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/management/march-13-1968-oil-discovered-alaskas-north-slope)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The original thesis was not "predict Prudhoe Bay." Neff said they knew Atlantic Richfield was looking for oil globally, but did not know it would strike such a large discovery when it did. The investment came from owning a low-P/E stock where known negatives were already reflected and where surprise good news could have an outsized impact ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Size & structure.** No source recovered in this run gives position size, purchase price, or percentage of Windsor. Treat the holding as [size unverified].

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** Neff's comment implies the stock was owned before the discovery became public. The most important path feature is not a drawdown number but the information asymmetry: the fund did not pay for a specific North Slope outcome, then received that upside when the discovery reset market expectations.

**Exit & P&L.** No realized gain figure was found. The trade is included because it was named by Neff himself as a successful example of the low-P/E method producing unexpected upside. P&L is [unverified].

**What it teaches.** The ARCO case is the cleanest example of Neff's optionality logic. Low-P/E investing, when applied to fundamentally sound companies, can create exposure to positive surprises that are effectively free at entry. The trade also warns against overfitting the research story after the fact: the discovery made the trade memorable, but it was not the purchase thesis.

**Sources.** CFA Institute interview; U.S. Department of Energy North Slope discovery record.

## 3. Tandy / RadioShack - Retail Distribution Meets Personal Computers

**Context & dates.** Neff paired Tandy with Atlantic Richfield as another "free plus" example. In his telling, Windsor owned Tandy in the 1970s as a lesser-recognized growth stock, and then the market became excited about personal computers that Tandy's RadioShack chain happened to be merchandising. The Smithsonian records that Tandy/RadioShack released the TRS-80 on August 3, 1977, and that initial internal expectations were modest before demand surprised to the upside ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Smithsonian Magazine](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smithsonian-institution/august-3-1977-the-trs-80-personal-computer-goes-on-sale-186379/)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The thesis was not that Tandy would become a personal-computer icon. Neff said the PC outcome was not part of the original analysis and that Windsor did not pay for it. The original category was "lesser-recognized growth": a company with growth potential that had not been priced like a glamour stock ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Size & structure.** Position size and fund percentage were not recovered. Treat as [size unverified].

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** The holding appears to have predated the market's excitement over personal computers. The Smithsonian article supports the external business path: the TRS-80 was one of the first consumer-available personal computers and sold far beyond cautious first-month expectations ([Smithsonian Magazine](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smithsonian-institution/august-3-1977-the-trs-80-personal-computer-goes-on-sale-186379/)). No mark-to-market drawdown was found.

**Exit & P&L.** Neff did not provide a realized dollar gain in the opened interview. P&L is [unverified], but inclusion is justified because Neff used it as a signature example of unpaid-for upside.

**What it teaches.** Tandy shows that Neff's low-P/E discipline did not exclude growth. It excluded paying glamour multiples for growth. When a modestly priced retailer had a distribution advantage in a suddenly expanding product category, Neff could own growth without underwriting heroic expectations.

**Sources.** CFA Institute interview; Smithsonian TRS-80 history.

## 4. Hospital Corp. And Browning-Ferris - Lesser-Recognized Growth After The Nifty Fifty

**Context & dates.** In the early 1970s, the market favored the Nifty Fifty at very high P/Es while Windsor's lower-P/E discipline underperformed. Neff later told CFA Institute that when those growth stocks finally became out of favor, Windsor "loaded up" on Tandy, Hospital Corp., Browning-Ferris, and others; he said Windsor then came out of the early-1970s aftermath earning around 80% compared with the market's 40% ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** Hospital Corp. and Browning-Ferris fit the "lesser-recognized growth" category: businesses in expanding fields whose stocks were no longer priced as market darlings. HCA's own history says Hospital Corporation of America was founded in 1968, introducing a new hospital-care model, while an NCBI Bookshelf chapter documents rapid investor-owned hospital expansion and HCA acquisitions in the late 1970s and early 1980s ([HCA Healthcare](https://www.hcahealthcare.com/about/our-history); [NCBI Bookshelf](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK217898/)). Browning-Ferris was a fast-growing waste-services consolidator; a company-history profile says it grew from a one-truck Houston operation into the world's second-largest waste disposal company, and Waste360 later described it as one of the founding companies of the industry ([Company-Histories.com](https://www.company-histories.com/BrowningFerris-Industries-Inc-Company-History.html); [Waste360](https://www.waste360.com/industry-insights/browning-ferris-industries-inc-)).

**Size & structure.** Neff's "loaded up" phrasing indicates meaningful exposure, but no source in this run gives individual position size or fund percentage. Size is [unverified].

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** The key drawdown was at the strategy level: Windsor was hurt in 1971-1973 while glamour stocks dominated. Neff's account says the payoff came after those expensive growth stocks corrected and neglected growth companies became available at prices compatible with Windsor's method ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Exit & P&L.** Neff's aggregate figure was roughly 80% for Windsor versus 40% for the market in the recovery period after the early-1970s weakness; exact stock-level exits and realized gains were not recovered. Treat individual P&L as [unverified] and the aggregate outperformance as first-person but rounded.

**What it teaches.** This basket is one of Neff's most important institutional lessons: a value manager can lag badly when markets reward glamour, yet the future opportunity set improves as once-loved growth stocks fall back into the low-P/E universe. Patience created the opportunity; low price plus real business growth created the payoff.

**Sources.** CFA Institute interview; HCA history; NCBI Bookshelf hospital-chain growth chapter; Company-Histories Browning-Ferris profile; Waste360 BFI profile.

## 5. Early-1990s Financial Intermediaries - Banks, Thrifts, And Insurers Under Attack

**Context & dates.** Neff described the early 1990s as a period when financial intermediaries were believed to be going bust. He said 30% or more of Windsor's portfolio was positioned in thrifts, banks, and insurance companies, and that shareholders complained the whole group would fail. Some did, but the basket eventually became rewarding ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The thesis was industry-level overreaction. Neff did not deny that parts of the group were impaired; he underwrote the difference between "some will fail" and "all are doomed." This aligns with his process of sweeping new-low lists and using Value Line's statistical yardsticks to find companies moving up the quality ladder despite market attack ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Size & structure.** This is the best-supported size figure in the set: Neff himself said 30% or more of the portfolio was in financial intermediaries. Individual names, weights, and security-level schedules were not recovered.

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** The drawdown was both market and client-pressure related. Neff emphasized shareholder complaints during the period, which matters because Windsor was an open-end mutual fund exposed to redemptions and style scrutiny, not a locked-up partnership. Penn Gazette's profile reinforces the institutional context: Neff was known for contrarian, low-P/E investing in apparently floundering companies and industries ([Penn Gazette](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).

**Exit & P&L.** No stock-level exit or P&L was recovered. Neff's first-person conclusion was that the basket "eventually" became good investments, but exact realized gains are [unverified] ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**What it teaches.** The financial-intermediary basket is the clearest example of Neff's willingness to concentrate by theme while maintaining stock-level discipline. He was not merely buying low P/Es; he was underwriting survivorship within a despised sector and accepting reputational discomfort while the thesis seasoned.

**Sources.** CFA Institute interview; Penn Gazette profile.

## 6. Dell, Home Depot, IBM, Xerox, Seagate, Digital Equipment, And McDonald's - Buying Growth After It Was Beaten Up

**Context & dates.** Neff acknowledged that Windsor sometimes owned genuine growth stocks, a fact that raised eyebrows because the fund was identified with low P/E investing. The key was entry price: he told CFA Institute that Windsor owned Dell twice at single-digit multiples and also bought Home Depot, IBM, Xerox, Seagate Technology, Digital Equipment, and McDonald's when they reached single-digit multiples after market punishment ([CFA Institute interview PDF](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The thesis was that growth did not become uninvestable simply because it had once been popular. It became investable when the price changed enough to satisfy Windsor's hurdle. Validea's model summary, based on *John Neff on Investing*, describes the method as seeking P/Es 40% to 60% below the market, sustainable EPS growth, positive free cash flow, and a superior relationship between total return and P/E paid ([Validea](https://www.validea.com/john-neff); [Interactive Brokers/Validea](https://www.interactivebrokers.com/campus/traders-insight/securities/stocks/inside-the-investment-strategy-of-john-neff/)).

**Size & structure.** Individual weights were not recovered. This should be treated as a recurring trade pattern rather than one position. Size is [unverified].

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** The drawdown came before entry: Neff waited for companies with real growth attributes to be "beat up badly enough" to meet the low-P/E hurdle. No source recovered stock-level price paths or position-level interim losses.

**Exit & P&L.** No realized gains were recovered for the named stocks. Their inclusion rests on Neff's own first-person identification of them as accepted exceptions inside a low-P/E mandate, not on P&L detail.

**What it teaches.** This basket prevents a common misreading of Neff. He was not anti-growth; he was anti-overpaying. His rule was price-relative-to-growth, not a style-box ban on good businesses. That distinction explains how a manager known for dull, cheap stocks could own Dell or Home Depot without abandoning discipline.

**Sources.** CFA Institute interview; Validea method summary; Interactive Brokers/Validea method summary.

## 7. Newmont Mining - Repeated Round Trips Rather Than Buy-And-Hold

**Context & dates.** Base Hit Investing's notes on *John Neff on Investing* report that Neff bought Newmont Mining in 1981, sold it months later after a 50% gain, bought it again in 1982, and sold again in 1983 [single-source, book-derived secondary note] ([Base Hit Investing](https://basehitinvesting.substack.com/p/john-neff-peter-lynch-portfolio-turnover)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The thesis appears to have been business and commodity-cycle momentum at a low-enough price, consistent with Neff's emphasis on earnings-power trajectory and his reluctance to hold a stock after it reached fair value. This is less well sourced than Ford or the CFA-named examples, so it should be treated as an illustrative book case until verified in the book.

**Size & structure.** No position size, percentage of fund, or purchase price was recovered. Size is [unverified].

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** The key path feature was turnover. Base Hit's reading of the book emphasizes that Neff often sold after 50% to 100% gains in a year or less, then recycled capital into the next low-P/E opportunity rather than treating the stock as a permanent holding ([Base Hit Investing](https://basehitinvesting.substack.com/p/john-neff-peter-lynch-portfolio-turnover)). No interim drawdown was found.

**Exit & P&L.** The reported first Newmont round trip generated about a 50% gain in months [single-source]. The later 1982-1983 round trip lacks a recovered gain figure.

**What it teaches.** Newmont shows Neff's portfolio as a recycling machine. He did not require decades-long compounding at the security level to compound well at the fund level. The risk is tax and timing friction; the benefit is constant redeployment from recognized value into fresh neglect.

**Sources.** Base Hit Investing book notes; CFA Institute interview for process context.

## 8. Edison Brothers - Specialty Retail As "Lesser-Recognized Growth"

**Context & dates.** Stockunlock's book summary says that in 1974 Neff bought specialty retailer Edison Brothers after the stock had declined because the company had diversified away from its core. The market did not immediately reward the company as it refocused and returned to growth, but Windsor later exited a little over a year later with a 137% gain [single-source, book-derived secondary summary] ([Stockunlock/LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/how-john-neff-crushed-index-over-three-decades-stockunlock-ftvzc)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** Edison Brothers fits Neff's "lesser-recognized growth" idea: a company whose growth had become obscured by a correctable operational issue. The attraction was not statistical cheapness alone. It was the combination of lower price, plausible recovery, and a business still capable of growth.

**Size & structure.** Stockunlock says Neff tended to have around 25% of fund assets in the "lesser-recognized growth" category, not necessarily in Edison Brothers. No individual weight was recovered, so Edison size is [unverified].

**Entry and path, including drawdown.** The entry followed a share-price decline tied to diversification concerns. No primary drawdown or purchase price was found.

**Exit & P&L.** The reported gain was 137% over a little more than one year [single-source]. This needs book-page verification before use as a definitive Canon number.

**What it teaches.** Edison Brothers is a small but important counterexample to caricatures of Neff as a low-multiple industrial buyer only. His framework could buy a retailer when the market's narrative focused on a mistake but the company's underlying growth path remained repairable.

**Sources.** Stockunlock/LinkedIn book summary; CFA Institute interview for lesser-recognized-growth category.

## Patterns Across The Trades

1. Neff paid for pessimism, not perfection. In Ford, financial intermediaries, and the Nifty Fifty aftermath, the best entries came when market language was dominated by fear or disappointment.
2. Upside often came from multiple expansion plus business normalization. The thesis rarely required a heroic forecast. It required the market to stop capitalizing temporary damage as permanent impairment.
3. Dividends mattered as psychological and mathematical ballast. Ford's reported 5% yield is one example; Neff's broader method treated yield as part of expected total return.
4. He used categories without becoming style-boxed. "Lesser-recognized growth" and beaten-up growth stocks show that Neff's low-P/E method could own growth, but only at an acceptable price.
5. His best trades were not all long-duration holds. Newmont and many book-reported cases appear to be faster round trips, while the fund's institutional edge came from repeating the process over 31 years.
6. The biggest research gap is primary trade reconstruction. Future work should locate Neff-era Windsor annual reports, *John Neff on Investing* page scans, and any John Train text pages around the Ford case before converting this file's [single-source] numbers into definitive Canon figures.
