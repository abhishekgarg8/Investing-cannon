# John Neff - Mental Models

As of 2026-06-28 (UTC), John B. Neff is deceased. This reconstruction treats Neff's model as an operating system for buying low-expectations public equities, not as a simple low-P/E screen. The strongest evidence is his CFA/AIMR interview material, the completed A-F Neff files, and institutional/press sources on Windsor, Penn, and post-Neff Windsor. The main unresolved gaps remain primary Windsor annual reports, full page-level verification of *John Neff on Investing*, and exact security-level contribution data ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Google Books, 1999](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ); [Internet Archive, 1999](https://archive.org/details/johnneffoninvest00john)).

## Named Heuristics & Frameworks

**Low-P/E game.** Neff's first filter was not a vague commitment to value; it was a concrete preference for companies whose price/earnings multiples had fallen well below the market's expectations. In the 2006 CFA Institute session, he framed stock picking around solid low-P/E companies with strong fundamentals and strong, growing industries, then added that he continued to play the low-P/E game in his own money after Windsor ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The key model is: cheapness starts the work; business adequacy earns the capital.

**Total-return-to-P/E.** Neff combined expected earnings growth and dividend yield, then compared that total return with the P/E paid. Later modelers translate this into a total-return/P-E ratio, but the original mental model is simpler: do not buy a stock just because it is cheap; buy when expected return is large relative to price and when dividends provide part of the payoff while the market is still skeptical ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Validea/Interactive Brokers, 2024](https://www.interactivebrokers.com/campus/traders-insight/securities/stocks/inside-the-investment-strategy-of-john-neff/)).

**The new-low funnel.** Neff looked where other investors had already recoiled. He described using new-low lists and weekly Value Line statistical review to find companies under pressure, then asking whether the fundamentals justified the damage ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The model is not momentum reversal. It is triage: new lows are an alert that expectations may have gone too far.

**Free plus.** Neff liked situations where the entry price did not require a specific favorable surprise, but the company had plausible optionality. Atlantic Richfield's North Slope discovery and Tandy/RadioShack's personal-computer upside were documented in the greatest-trades file as examples of upside that was not paid for at entry ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [U.S. Department of Energy, 1968/2024](https://www.energy.gov/management/march-13-1968-oil-discovered-alaskas-north-slope); [Smithsonian, 2012](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smithsonian-institution/august-3-1977-the-trs-80-personal-computer-goes-on-sale-186379/)). The checklist question is: what good thing could happen that the price does not ask me to forecast?

**Inflection-point endurance.** Neff accepted that the strategy would lag around regime turns. The Nifty Fifty cycle, 1980 oil/electronics excitement, 1987 cash position, and early-1990s financial-intermediary stress all required staying with the method while evidence was still intact ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Capital Ideas excerpt, n.d.](https://capitalideasonline.com/wordpress/inflection-points/?pdf=14944); [Jason Zweig, 2015](https://jasonzweig.com/learning-from-the-bear-market-of-1973-1974/)). This is not stubbornness; it is a pre-commitment to distinguish style pain from thesis failure.

**Sell into recognition.** Neff did not aim to own every stock forever. If the market embraced a holding, the low-expectations edge decayed. He modeled a target based on expected growth and yield, then began selling as the stock approached fair recognition rather than waiting for a heroic terminal price ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Philadelphia Inquirer, 2019](https://www.inquirer.com/business/john-neff-obituary-vanguard-windsor-funds-university-pennsylvania-endowment-fidelity-20190605.html)).

**Anchor to windward.** In personal accounts after Windsor, Neff held a concentrated equity book but paired it with roughly 30% fixed income as ballast ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The broader model is that contrarian concentration needs liquidity or stability somewhere else in the balance sheet.

## Their Decision Checklist

**1. Define the fishing pond.** Start with stocks under visible pressure: new lows, low P/E lists, out-of-favor industries, unpopular cyclicals, and companies that investors describe as dull, misunderstood, or broken. Use Value Line, filings, annual reports, analyst estimates, and street-level checks as inputs rather than as automatic signals ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).

**2. Screen for low expectations, then immediately test quality.** A candidate should have a low multiple relative to the market and its own history, but Neff demanded more: solid fundamentals, a credible industry setting, and at least moderate growth. The later Validea translation uses rough quantitative cutoffs such as low P/E relative to the market, sustained earnings growth, sales support, and free cash flow; that is useful as a screen translation but should not be treated as Neff's full judgment process ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Validea/Interactive Brokers, 2024](https://www.interactivebrokers.com/campus/traders-insight/securities/stocks/inside-the-investment-strategy-of-john-neff/)).

**3. Build the total-return case.** Estimate conservative earnings growth, add dividend yield, and compare that total with the P/E paid. Reject companies where all the return depends on a distant narrative, a heroic multiple, or management-adjusted earnings. Neff's rejection of EBITDA shortcuts matters here: interest, taxes, and depreciation were real costs, so accounting quality is part of valuation ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**4. Ask whether the market is wrong for behavioral or structural reasons.** Neff wanted temporary neglect, overgeneralized fear, or disappointment that had gone too far. He was less interested in cheapness caused by permanent impairment. The early-1990s financial-intermediary basket is the template: the market feared the entire group, while Neff underwrote which banks, thrifts, and insurers could survive ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**5. Size by conviction and common-factor risk.** The precise Neff-era Windsor position table remains unrecovered, but evidence supports meaningful concentration: he described 30% or more of Windsor in financial intermediaries during the early 1990s, while his post-Windsor personal equity account held only 8 to 10 stocks with fixed-income ballast ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). For a modern checklist, single-name sizing should be lower when the thesis is cyclical, levered, or dependent on a macro turn; group sizing should be capped by the investor's ability to withstand a common drawdown.

**6. Keep liquidity for periods when valuation disappears.** In 1987, Neff held roughly 20% liquidity because he could not find enough reasonably priced stocks; the position hurt relative returns before the crash, then gave him optionality. At Penn, later reporting credited the institution's reluctance to overpay for alternatives and temporary Treasury holdings with preserving liquidity during the 2008 crisis ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Philadelphia Inquirer, 2009](https://www.inquirer.com/philly/business/20090814_PhillyDeals__Penn_fund_lost__but_other_Ivies_lost_more.html)).

**7. Re-underwrite on a calendar, not on hope.** If the growth rate and earnings case are not materializing after roughly a year, Neff's rule was to revisit the analysis and be willing to exit. This is the value-trap breaker. A cheap stock that does not progress is not made safer by repeating the original thesis louder ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**8. Sell when the crowd catches up.** If the thesis works and the stock rerates, sell patiently into strength. Neff's process began selling before the full theoretical target because a large mutual fund could not exit instantly, and because the low-P/E edge fades as the stock becomes admired ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

## Failure Modes Of The Model

**Cheap-for-a-reason traps.** The model fails when low P/E reflects a permanently impaired business, accounting quality problem, overlevered balance sheet, or secular decline. Neff's antidote was fundamental analysis and a sell rule if earnings and growth did not materialize, but the Canon still lacks the complete Windsor loss ledger needed to know how often the rule failed in practice ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D); [Google Books, 1999](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ)).

**Style-cycle pain mistaken for error.** Windsor lost 25% in 1973 and 16.8% in 1974 before large rebounds, and Neff's low-P/E style lagged during Nifty Fifty and 1980 theme markets ([Jason Zweig, 2015](https://jasonzweig.com/learning-from-the-bear-market-of-1973-1974/); [Capital Ideas excerpt, n.d.](https://capitalideasonline.com/wordpress/inflection-points/?pdf=14944)). The mental challenge is that real thesis failure and temporary style pain feel the same in the account statement.

**Open-end fund pressure.** Windsor shareholders could complain or redeem while the portfolio looked wrong. Neff's Penn experience shows similar governance discomfort: trustees could see a portfolio full of unloved companies and wonder whether the manager had lost touch ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)). The model requires client education, reporting discipline, and liquidity management, not just security analysis.

**Common-factor concentration.** The financial-intermediary basket worked in Neff's telling, but a 30%+ sector allocation creates a real possibility that one macro shock overwhelms stock selection. Modern Windsor prospectus language is not Neff-era evidence, but it correctly warns that value stocks can underperform and that active selection can cause benchmark-relative losses ([Vanguard prospectus, 2026](https://www.vanguard.com/pub/Pdf/sp22.pdf)).

**Screen overfitting.** Modern Neff-based screens are useful for first-pass idea generation, but they flatten a judgment-heavy process into ratios. The danger is buying every low-P/E, dividend-paying name without asking whether the market's skepticism is temporary or deserved ([Validea/Interactive Brokers, 2024](https://www.interactivebrokers.com/campus/traders-insight/securities/stocks/inside-the-investment-strategy-of-john-neff/)).

**Manager-specific transferability.** Post-Neff Windsor is the warning label. In 1998, after Neff had retired, the fund returned 0.81% versus 28.58% for the S&P 500 under successor management, according to the Wall Street Journal ([Wall Street Journal, 1999](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB920852456762443500)). That does not reduce Neff's record; it shows that the fund wrapper and value label were not the edge.

## Transferability: What An Individual Investor Can And Cannot Replicate

**Replicable.** An individual can replicate the discipline of starting with low expectations, demanding business adequacy, using dividend yield and conservative growth as the return base, rejecting adjusted-earnings shortcuts, keeping a thesis calendar, and selling when recognition removes the bargain. Individuals may even have an advantage in small and mid-cap stocks because they do not need to deploy Windsor-scale capital. Neff's own post-Windsor account suggests that a smaller portfolio can be more concentrated if the investor balances it with cash or fixed income ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

**Partly replicable.** A modern investor can build a Neff-style screen, but should treat it as a work queue rather than a portfolio. The screen should be followed by balance-sheet analysis, revenue and earnings durability checks, management quality, capital allocation, and a written reason why the market's low expectation is excessive. The FAJ review's description of Neff's book as memoir, principles treatise, and exhaustive Windsor report is a useful reminder: the method lived in market-cycle memory as much as in formulas ([Financial Analysts Journal review, 2000](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/research/financial-analysts-journal/2000/john-neff-on-investing-a-review)).

**Hard to replicate.** Most individuals cannot replicate Wellington's research infrastructure, Neff's career-long access to managements, the legitimacy to hold an unpopular portfolio in front of clients, or the institutional trust he earned at Vanguard and Penn. They also cannot recreate the exact 1964-1995 opportunity set, when low-P/E public equities, dividends, commission structures, and mutual-fund competition differed from today's market.

**Not replicable yet from the source record.** The Canon cannot fully replicate Neff's security-level rules until the Neff-era Windsor annual reports and *John Neff on Investing* pages are checked. The book is confirmed to contain personal investment diary excerpts and best/worst decision material, but the accessible online records are still metadata or excerpt carriers rather than complete primary pages ([Google Books, 1999](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ); [Internet Archive, 1999](https://archive.org/details/johnneffoninvest00john)).

## A Neff-Style Personal Checklist

1. Is the stock cheap because expectations are low, or cheap because the business is deteriorating permanently?
2. Does expected earnings growth plus dividend yield justify the P/E paid without heroic assumptions?
3. Are debt, accounting quality, and capital intensity honestly reflected, or is EBITDA doing too much work?
4. What is the "free plus" that I am not paying for?
5. If this takes a year longer than expected, do I have the liquidity and temperament to hold?
6. What evidence would prove the thesis wrong, and when will I check it?
7. If the stock rerates, what price or valuation range turns the bargain into an ordinary holding?
8. Is my position size small enough that a sector-level mistake will not force a bad sale?

The model's essence is low expectations plus business adequacy plus repeatable sell discipline. Neff's edge was not that he found beautiful businesses before everyone else. It was that he could buy businesses that looked embarrassing, prove that they were good enough, wait while the market stayed skeptical, and leave before admiration turned the price into the next person's problem.
