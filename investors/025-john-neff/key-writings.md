# John Neff - Key Writings

As of 2026-06-28 (UTC), John B. Neff is deceased; this Task F pass found no new source-backed personal legal or SEC enforcement development beyond the negative checks already recorded in Tasks A-E. The main research limitation is access: Neff's core book is visible through bibliographic pages and excerpt/review carriers, but the Internet Archive copy is access-restricted and this run did not obtain full page images. Treat exact book quotations, page numbers, and stock-level diary claims as pending physical-copy or controlled-digital-lending verification ([Internet Archive, 1999](https://archive.org/details/johnneffoninvest00john); [Google Books, 1999](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ)).

## Source Handling Notes

For Neff, "works by him" is a small corpus: one book, one formal AIMR/CFA interview lineage, a later CFA reprint/session transcript, and scattered first-person interview or seminar carriers. The book is the central work, but it must be handled carefully because many online summaries quote it without page images. This file ranks sources by proximity to Neff's own words, not by popularity. It uses review/catalog pages for structure, the CFA/AIMR PDF for exact first-person process, and secondary review sources only where they clearly identify what the book contains ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://storage.googleapis.com/ni_library_storage/Investors/Neff-On-Low-PE-Investing-2006.pdf?mc_cid=c396e533c2&mc_eid=c2c0b87462); [Financial Analysts Journal review, 2000](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/research/financial-analysts-journal/2000/john-neff-on-investing-a-review)).

## Works By John Neff And Near-Primary Materials

### 1. John Neff on Investing - John Neff with S. L. Mintz (Wiley, 1999)

**Central thesis.** Neff's book is the canonical statement of his low-P/E method: buy fundamentally adequate companies when market expectations are low, insist that expected earnings growth plus dividend yield more than compensates for the multiple paid, and recycle capital when recognition removes the excess return. Google Books says the book sets out Neff's low-P/E principles, explains strategies and investment decisions, and reproduces personal investment diary excerpts that reveal some best and worst decisions ([Google Books, 1999](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ)). Open Library and AbeBooks repeat the book-jacket framing that Neff shares the methods behind a roughly 57-fold Windsor-era result, while also warning that the edition record and outperformance counts vary across carriers ([Open Library, 2008 edition record](https://openlibrary.org/books/OL29170234M/John_Neff_on_Investing); [AbeBooks, n.d.](https://www.abebooks.com/9780471197171/John-Neff-Investing-0471197173/plp)).

**Best chapters / sections.** The table of contents available through an eBay bibliographic listing divides the book into three practical blocks: "My Road to Windsor," "Enduring Principles," and "A Market Journal," followed by appendices and index. The most important sections for Canon work are "Elements of Style," "The Bargain Basement," "Care and Maintenance of a Low P/E Portfolio," and the market-journal chapters "The Silly Season," "Four Yards and a Cloud of Dust," "The Right Stuff," "Good Guys Persevere," and "Deja Vu" ([eBay bibliographic listing, n.d.](https://www.ebay.com/p/872245)). The structure matters because it makes the book more than a memoir: it is biography, method manual, and case diary in one.

**Key ideas, paraphrased.**

1. Low P/E is a starting discipline, not a substitute for business analysis. Neff wanted cheapness plus adequate growth, dividends, and a strong business case, not statistically cheap weak companies ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://storage.googleapis.com/ni_library_storage/Investors/Neff-On-Low-PE-Investing-2006.pdf?mc_cid=c396e533c2&mc_eid=c2c0b87462)).
2. The "total return ratio" - expected growth plus dividend yield divided by the P/E paid - was the compact way Windsor compared expected reward with price ([Validea, 2026](https://www.validea.com/john-neff)).
3. Dividends were not cosmetic. They provided part of return, evidence of cash discipline, and a way to be paid while waiting for recognition ([Morningstar Australia, 2026](https://www.morningstar.com.au/personal-finance/john-neff-buying-stocks-on-sale)).
4. A low-P/E investor should seek "free plus" upside: positive surprises not included in the entry price, such as a corporate development, restructuring, or industry turn ([Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).
5. Concentration is acceptable when evidence is strong. Review carriers and Neff's CFA/AIMR discussion indicate Windsor often held about 60 stocks with the top 10 near 40% of assets, which is diversified by hedge-fund standards but concentrated for a giant mutual fund ([TheStreet, 1999](https://www.thestreet.com/opinion/bookshelf-john-neff-on-investing-814203); [Acquirer's Multiple excerpt carrier, 2016](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2016/12/john-neff-why-the-low-pe-strategy-works/)).
6. Sell discipline is built into the style. Neff bought unpopular stocks to sell them after recognition; the book-review and interview carriers repeatedly emphasize selling into strength rather than falling in love with holdings ([TheStreet, 1999](https://www.thestreet.com/opinion/bookshelf-john-neff-on-investing-814203); [Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
7. The style requires historical memory. Investment Masters' book notes argue that one of the book's rare strengths is the quarter-by-quarter historical context through the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s, not merely a timeless screen ([Investment Masters, 2018](https://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2018/7/16/learning-from-john-neff)).
8. Neff's record does not remove the need for caveats. Return figures, the 22/23 outperformance-year discrepancy, and stock-level P&L need primary Windsor annual reports or book pages before the Canon uses exact precision ([Barron's, 2019](https://www.barrons.com/articles/john-neff-51559786060); [CFA Institute, 2019](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/blogs/enterprising-investor/2019/in-memoriam-john-b-neff-cfa)).

**Best use for the Canon.** This is the first source to read for Neff's self-understanding and should anchor Tasks G and H after page-level verification. Its best use is reconstructing decision process, trade categories, sell rules, and market-cycle memory. Its weakest use, until pages are checked, is exact quotation or exact individual trade economics.

### 2. A Conversation with Legendary Value Investor John B. Neff, CFA - AIMR Conference Proceedings (2004)

**Central thesis.** This four-page AIMR proceeding is the most compact first-person operating manual recovered in this run. It presents Neff in Q&A form with Fred H. Speece Jr., CFA, and focuses on equity analysis, low-P/E investing, portfolio concentration, sell discipline, research staffing, and lessons from Windsor ([AIMR PDF carrier, 2004](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5197d643e4b041b39c92abcb/t/5198f87fe4b0f56ba1d95e55/1368979583089/DOC006.PDF)).

**Best sections.** The strongest sections are the discussion of total return versus P/E, the NYNEX example, Windsor's approximate 60-stock structure with a concentrated top 10, and the research-process answer describing a small team that pursued low-P/E ideas deeply ([Acquirer's Multiple excerpt carrier, 2016](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2016/12/john-neff-why-the-low-pe-strategy-works/)).

**Key ideas, paraphrased.**

1. Neff distinguished low-P/E from generic "value" because P/E is a concrete, measurable starting point.
2. He defined total return as earnings growth plus dividend yield, then compared that with the P/E paid.
3. He used examples such as NYNEX to show how yield, growth, and rerating potential were modeled together.
4. Windsor's research process was small-team and intensive rather than bureaucracy-heavy.
5. Portfolio concentration was a deliberate consequence of attractive expected returns, not neglect of risk.
6. Selling began before a holding reached the full theoretical appreciation target, especially for large positions.
7. The most transferable lesson is not a static screen but the discipline to keep reassessing whether the business is deteriorating.

**Best use for the Canon.** Use this as the first operational checklist source. It is shorter than the book and easier to audit. Because several online versions are PDF carriers rather than the original CFA page, later work should preserve the bibliographic details and DOI when a primary CFA/AIMR landing page is available.

### 3. On Low-P/E Investing - CFA Institute / Global Perspectives on Investment Management (2006)

**Central thesis.** This nine-page CFA Institute reprint is the cleanest opened first-person source in the Neff folder. It covers the same core territory as the AIMR conversation but adds useful first-person detail on post-retirement concentration, fixed-income ballast, new-low sourcing, Value Line use, cyclicals, financial intermediaries, EBITDA skepticism, and the "one lesson" summary ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://storage.googleapis.com/ni_library_storage/Investors/Neff-On-Low-PE-Investing-2006.pdf?mc_cid=c396e533c2&mc_eid=c2c0b87462)).

**Best sections.** The early pages are essential because Neff states the durable principles in operational form: low P/E, solid fundamentals, strong industries, roughly 7% or better fundamental growth, compensating multiples for cyclicals, new-low screens, and weekly statistical review ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://storage.googleapis.com/ni_library_storage/Investors/Neff-On-Low-PE-Investing-2006.pdf?mc_cid=c396e533c2&mc_eid=c2c0b87462)). Later pages are useful for mistakes and mental models because they discuss Nifty Fifty underperformance, 1980, 1987 cash, financial intermediaries, and the practical risk of weak fundamentals.

**Key ideas, paraphrased.**

1. Stock picking can still work if the investor seeks strong fundamentals at low expectations.
2. A low P/E must be paired with growth and industry quality; cheap cyclicals need special compensation.
3. New lows and Value Line statistics were idea sources, not automatic buy signals.
4. In Neff's personal post-Windsor account, concentration was acceptable because fixed income supplied balance.
5. Debt penalized the P/E; EBITDA was suspect because interest, taxes, and depreciation are real costs.
6. Value traps require reassessment if the expected thesis fails to progress.
7. The investor must be lonely without being stubborn: keep the thesis if evidence holds, change it if fundamentals decay.

**Best use for the Canon.** This should remain the Task B/G/H backbone because it is source-visible, concise, and clearly in Neff's own voice. It also lets later tasks quote sparingly without relying on quote aggregators.

### 4. 2006 Financial Analysts Seminar write-ups - Motley Fool Parts 1-3

**Central thesis.** These are not works authored by Neff, but they are near-primary write-ups of a 2006 Financial Analysts Seminar presentation hosted by CFA Institute. They preserve contemporaneous practitioner notes on all-cap flexibility, sell discipline, value traps, curbstone research, free-plus upside, and hedge-fund fee criticism ([Motley Fool Part 1, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/20/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-1.aspx); [Motley Fool Part 2, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/dividends-income/2006/07/21/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-2.aspx); [Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).

**Best sections.** Part 2 is most useful for sell discipline and value traps; Part 3 is best for sourcing and "free plus"; Part 1 is useful for the context of style-box pressure and the 1987 cash stance. Because these are reporter summaries, they should not outrank the CFA PDF for exact wording.

**Key ideas, paraphrased.**

1. Reevaluate a thesis if time passes without fundamental progress.
2. Avoid turning a low-P/E thesis into a permanent excuse for a value trap.
3. Research can include street-level observation, not just formal financial statements.
4. Low-P/E investing works best when it leaves unpaid-for upside.
5. Neff's disdain for excessive hedge-fund fees was part of his fiduciary culture.

### 5. Barron's Roundtable, Kiplinger's, Money, Institutional Investor, and New York Times interview corpus - mostly unrecovered

**Central thesis.** Neff was a long-running Barron's Roundtable participant and appeared in several named press sources. The Philadelphia Inquirer reports that he served on Barron's annual stock-picking panel from 1976 to 2007 ([Philadelphia Inquirer, 2019](https://www.inquirer.com/business/john-neff-obituary-vanguard-windsor-funds-university-pennsylvania-endowment-fidelity-20190605.html)). Novel Investor and Encyclopedia.com identify source families including Barron's, Kiplinger's, Institutional Investor, Money, and The New York Times, but most original articles were not recovered in this run ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/); [Encyclopedia.com, n.d.](https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/educational-magazines/neff-john-b-1931)).

**Best use for the Canon.** Treat this corpus as a source-hunting map, not as verified quotation authority. The likely value is high: these articles can show how Neff explained the same process in real time, especially around cyclicals, bear markets, technology booms, and late-career warnings. Until originals are opened, do not upgrade quote-carrier snippets into verified Canon quotes.

### 6. Book excerpts and excerpt carriers - Inflection Points, Low-P/E Strategy Works, and trade summaries

**Central thesis.** Several sites reproduce or paraphrase chunks of the book. The "Inflection Points" PDF carrier is useful for style-cycle performance context; Acquirer's Multiple reproduces a section on low-P/E mechanics; Morningstar Australia uses a Citibank passage and Ford example to explain how Neff treated ugly stocks as potentially beautiful; Investment Masters and other book-note carriers summarize diary examples ([Capital Ideas excerpt, n.d.](https://capitalideasonline.com/wordpress/inflection-points/?pdf=14944); [Acquirer's Multiple, 2016](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2016/12/john-neff-why-the-low-pe-strategy-works/); [Morningstar Australia, 2026](https://www.morningstar.com.au/personal-finance/john-neff-buying-stocks-on-sale); [Investment Masters, 2018](https://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2018/7/16/learning-from-john-neff)).

**Best use for the Canon.** Use these as locators and triangulation aids. They are especially useful for identifying chapters and examples to verify in the book: Citibank, Ford, NYNEX, Gulf Oil, Newmont, Edison Brothers, "inflection points," and the total-return ratio. They should not be treated as primary substitutes for a copy of the book.

## Reading Order For Future Canon Work

1. Start with *John Neff on Investing*, but read it with a note sheet organized by method, trade examples, mistakes, and quote candidates. Verify each cited page before upgrading any book-derived claim.
2. Read the CFA/AIMR conversation next. Convert it into an operational checklist: idea source, business filter, valuation target, position size, sell rule, and reassessment trigger.
3. Use the 2006 CFA reprint to reconcile any wording or examples that appear in the Motley Fool seminar notes.
4. Read the Penn Gazette profile and Inquirer obituary to understand institutional temperament: Penn governance, trustee discomfort, cash/Treasury patience, and Barron's Roundtable role.
5. Read Fridson's FAJ review and TheStreet review as review lenses: they highlight how professional readers understood the book at publication.
6. Only then use Validea/AAII-style screens. They are helpful translations, but they can flatten Neff into a factor recipe if read too early.
