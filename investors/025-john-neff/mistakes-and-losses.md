# John Neff - Mistakes and Losses

As of 2026-06-28 (UTC), John B. Neff is deceased, and this run found no source-backed personal SEC enforcement action or active legal proceeding involving him. The evidence for Neff's mistakes is unusually uneven: the public record is strong on style-cycle underperformance and weak on position-level realized losses, because Neff-era Windsor annual reports and full page scans of *John Neff on Investing* were not recovered in this run. This file therefore distinguishes documented drawdowns and underperformance from still-unverified individual stock mistakes.

## Executive Summary

Neff's central mistake pattern was not fraud, leverage, or a single catastrophic stock. It was the repeated willingness to look wrong in public while low-P/E value was out of favor. That posture was essential to his long-term excess return, but it created real interim pain: Windsor was "absolutely" hurt during the 1971-1973 Nifty Fifty period, lagged in 1980 when oil and electronics themes dominated, fell behind in the first three quarters of 1987 after building a large liquidity position, and endured shareholder complaints in the early 1990s when more than 30% of the portfolio sat in financial intermediaries that the market feared would fail ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

The most quantifiable drawdown came in the 1973-1974 bear market. Jason Zweig, republishing a Wall Street Journal column, reports that Vanguard Windsor lost 25% in 1973 and another 16.8% in 1974, before gaining 54.5% in 1975 and 46.4% in 1976 ([Jason Zweig, 2015](https://jasonzweig.com/learning-from-the-bear-market-of-1973-1974/)). A book excerpt carrier for *John Neff on Investing* gives a different but related relative framing: Windsor's cumulative underperformance during 1971-1973 was almost 26 percentage points, followed by outperformance of more than 63 percentage points during 1974-1976 ([Capital Ideas excerpt, n.d.](https://capitalideasonline.com/wordpress/inflection-points/?pdf=14944)). These are not contradictions; one is absolute fund return during the bear market, the other is relative performance over the style cycle.

Neff's record also contains softer mistakes: high client discomfort during sector concentration, a process that could reject hot growth winners too long before their valuations compressed, limited transparency for future researchers, and a dependence on a rare manager temperament that proved hard to reproduce after he left. The 1998 Windsor slump under successor Charles Freeman is not a Neff loss, but it is an important transferability warning: Institutional Investor reports Windsor gained less than 1% versus 28.6% for the S&P 500, hurt by a large Citicorp position, and the Wall Street Journal reported a 0.81% 1998 return versus 28.58% for the S&P 500 ([Institutional Investor, 2002](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgidyngwpw34bmkndhc/home/going-its-own-way); [Wall Street Journal, 1999](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB920852456762443500)).

## Major Losses and Error Episodes

### 1. The Nifty Fifty and 1973-1974 Bear Market

The largest documented pain period was the early-1970s growth-stock era and bear market. In the CFA Institute interview, Neff said the Nifty Fifty traded at very high P/E multiples before those multiples collapsed, and that Windsor was hurt badly in 1971-1973 before the same former growth favorites became cheap enough to buy as "lesser-recognized growth" ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The Capital Ideas excerpt of Neff's book quantifies the relative damage as almost 26 percentage points of cumulative underperformance from 1971 through 1973, then a more than 63-point relative recovery from 1974 through 1976 ([Capital Ideas excerpt, n.d.](https://capitalideasonline.com/wordpress/inflection-points/?pdf=14944)).

The absolute losses were harsh. Zweig reports Windsor lost 25% in 1973 and then another 16.8% in 1974, before the 1975-1976 rebound generated a large portion of Neff's cumulative outperformance ([Jason Zweig, 2015](https://jasonzweig.com/learning-from-the-bear-market-of-1973-1974/)). This was not a near-death in the sense of forced liquidation, but it was the kind of two-year drawdown that could have broken a public mutual-fund manager's resolve or client base. The behavioral root cause was principled refusal to buy fashionable growth, but the practical mistake risk was real: being right on valuation too early is indistinguishable from being wrong for clients who can redeem.

What changed afterward was not the philosophy but the opportunity set. Neff said Windsor loaded up on former growth companies such as Tandy, Hospital Corp., and Browning-Ferris after they fell into the low-P/E universe ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The lesson is double-edged: discipline paid, but only because the portfolio had enough liquidity, client patience, and psychological stamina to survive the drawdown.

### 2. The 1980 Oil and Electronics Mania

Neff described 1980 as another period when Windsor did poorly because the market was excited about oil and electronics themes. In his telling, oil was expected to keep rising and electronic stocks were hot; Windsor refused to join the groupthink, lagged, and then recovered when those themes were punished in 1981 ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The book-excerpt carrier quantifies the relative miss as 9.8 percentage points of underperformance in 1980, followed by more than 21.7 points of outperformance in 1981 ([Capital Ideas excerpt, n.d.](https://capitalideasonline.com/wordpress/inflection-points/?pdf=14944)).

This was an error of omission only if one judges by short-term benchmark-relative returns. Neff avoided expensive themes that later reversed, so the final result vindicated the discipline. The mistake lesson is different: a value manager can be structurally underexposed to a real trend before that trend becomes a bubble. If the bubble lasts long enough, career risk becomes part of portfolio risk.

### 3. The 1987 Liquidity Position

In 1987, Neff built a 20% liquidity position because equity valuations had risen to roughly 22-23 times earnings and he could not find enough reasonably priced stocks. That cash caused Windsor to fall behind during the first three quarters, before the October crash and 1988 recovery allowed the fund to more than recoup the underperformance ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

This episode was a good decision with bad interim optics. The root cause was valuation discipline, but the visible failure mode was opportunity cost: holding cash in a rising market makes a manager look timid until the reason for the cash appears. The Inquirer later described Treasuries and liquidity as a recurring Neff tactic when few bargains were available, and credited similar caution with helping Penn avoid some illiquidity damage in 2008 after Neff's direct role had declined ([Philadelphia Inquirer, 2019](https://www.inquirer.com/business/john-neff-obituary-vanguard-windsor-funds-university-pennsylvania-endowment-fidelity-20190605.html)). The process lesson was not "always hold cash"; it was "hold cash only when valuation evidence is strong enough to withstand client impatience."

### 4. Early-1990s Financial Intermediaries

The early-1990s financial-intermediary bet was Neff's most explicit client-pressure episode. He said 30% or more of Windsor was in thrifts, banks, and insurers when the market feared the group was going bust. Shareholders complained that all of them would fail. Neff acknowledged that some did fail, but argued that the group eventually became rewarding ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

The mistake risk here was concentration plus common-factor exposure. A 30%+ sector allocation can be rational if the market has overgeneralized bad news, but it also means the portfolio is vulnerable to one macro or credit regime. Modern Vanguard Windsor documents are not Neff-era evidence, but they are a useful reminder that large funds face risks from concentration, ownership limits, regulatory limits, and adverse market conditions ([Vanguard Windsor prospectus, 2026](https://www.vanguard.com/pub/Pdf/p022.pdf)). The missing evidence is important: this run did not find the individual bank/thrift/insurer names, position sizes, or realized P&L, so the episode should remain flagged until Neff-era annual reports are located.

### 5. Penn Endowment Transition Friction

Penn was not a Windsor shareholder account, but it shows how Neff's style looked when transplanted into an institutional endowment. The Pennsylvania Gazette reports that Neff changed Penn's portfolio quickly after taking over, and that in the first year, a poor year for his type of stocks, Penn did better than Windsor mainly because Penn still held some legacy positions he had not yet sold ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)). The same profile records trustee discomfort with the look of the portfolio, including a complaint that he bought too many shoe-related stocks, which Neff treated as an exaggerated but directionally understandable reaction to buying unloved companies ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).

This was not a major loss in the final record; Penn credited Neff with a dramatic endowment turnaround. The mistake lesson is governance-related. A style that looks strange even when right needs unusually clear communication with trustees and clients. Neff's plain-spoken reporting style helped, but the portfolio's appearance still created political friction.

### 6. Post-Neff Windsor and the Transferability Problem

The late-1990s Windsor slump should not be charged to Neff's own track record, because he retired at the end of 1995. It still belongs in the mistakes file because it reveals the danger of treating "Windsor value" as a system that anyone could run the same way. Institutional Investor reports that in 1998, when successor management was in place, Windsor badly lagged the market, gained less than 1%, and suffered from a large Citicorp position that fell sharply from its high ([Institutional Investor, 2002](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgidyngwpw34bmkndhc/home/going-its-own-way)). The Wall Street Journal similarly reported that Windsor returned only 0.81% in 1998 versus 28.58% for the S&P 500, with Wellington's fees hurt by the bad spell ([Wall Street Journal, 1999](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB920852456762443500)).

The root cause was not simply "value stopped working." It was manager-specific judgment, scale, public-fund client pressure, and the difficulty of distinguishing a cheap stock from a value trap during a growth-led market. Neff's record proves that active value skill can exist; the successor slump warns that the brand, factor, and fund wrapper are not the skill.

## What Neff Said About Mistakes

Neff's public language around mistakes emphasized process endurance rather than apology. In the CFA interview, he framed bad spells as recurring inflection points: performance can suffer around them, but the manager must keep the discipline if the underlying evidence still supports it ([CFA Institute interview, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The Inquirer preserved a related sell-discipline warning from 1995: when bragging feels tempting, selling may be prudent ([Philadelphia Inquirer, 2019](https://www.inquirer.com/business/john-neff-obituary-vanguard-windsor-funds-university-pennsylvania-endowment-fidelity-20190605.html)).

The book itself remains a key unresolved source. Google Books and AbeBooks both state that *John Neff on Investing* reproduces diary material and covers some of Neff's best and worst investment decisions, but full page-level access was not available in this run ([Google Books, 1999](https://books.google.com/books/about/John_Neff_on_Investing.html?id=5-5cCgAAQBAJ); [AbeBooks, n.d.](https://www.abebooks.com/9780471197171/John-Neff-Investing-0471197173/plp)). Until those pages are verified, any stock-level "worst investment" list should remain provisional.

## Behavioral Root Causes

1. **Style purity under benchmark pressure.** Neff's refusal to chase glamour was the source of long-term alpha and the source of repeated interim underperformance.
2. **Concentration in hated sectors.** The early-1990s financial-intermediary position shows that a correct contrarian thesis can still create severe client discomfort and common-factor risk.
3. **Valuation discipline that can look like timidity.** The 1987 cash position was sensible in hindsight but looked costly while the market kept rising.
4. **Transferability overconfidence.** Later Windsor history shows that low-P/E rules alone did not reproduce Neff's judgment.
5. **Evidence opacity.** Because public annual reports and exact trade ledgers remain incomplete, the Canon should avoid false precision about losses, individual position P&L, and year-by-year attribution.

## Process Changes Made After

The public record does not show a dramatic process overhaul. Neff's main response to painful periods was to refine and recommit to the same core process: buy low-P/E stocks with defensible fundamentals, use dividend yield and cash as ballast, reject excessive leverage and weak prospects, and sell when recognition removes the return advantage ([Morningstar Australia, 2026](https://www.morningstar.com.au/personal-finance/john-neff-buying-stocks-on-sale); [Morningstar India, 2019](https://www.morningstar.in/posts/53267/investing-style-low-pe-investor.aspx)). The process did evolve in application: he bought former growth stocks after their multiples compressed, used liquidity when bargains were scarce, and accepted sector concentration only when the market's fear looked overgeneralized.

Neff also seems to have drawn ethical and fiduciary lessons from the mutual-fund business. In a 1996 Washington Post article on personal trading by fund managers, he criticized heavy personal trading as distracting and potentially harmful to fund decision-making ([Washington Post, 1996](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/1996/06/30/mutual-mystery-funds-codes-of-conduct/8ad3d2d8-ac71-42d3-96f3-876f85146e31/)). That is not a trading loss, but it is relevant to mistakes: Neff saw behavioral distraction as a risk control issue.

## Legal, Regulatory, and Criticism Check

This run searched for John B. Neff, Windsor Fund, Wellington, SEC enforcement, and lawsuit combinations. It found biography/proxy sources and common-name false positives, but no source-backed personal enforcement matter involving John B. Neff. The strongest primary filing opened was Amkor's 2004 proxy, which identifies Neff as a director, former Windsor/Gemini II portfolio manager, former Wellington senior vice president/managing partner, and an independent audit committee financial expert; it did not disclose a personal enforcement issue in the opened biography section ([SEC Amkor proxy, 2004](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1047127/0000893220-04-001339.txt)). This is a negative search finding, not a guarantee; later tasks should re-run the check.

Criticism of Neff is therefore mostly investment-process criticism: style-cycle lag, sector concentration, public-fund client pressure, lack of surviving primary trade ledgers, and weak replicability. Barron's and CFA Institute both document the exceptional long record, but those same headline results can obscure the drawdowns and the fact that a meaningful part of excess return came from rebounding after ugly periods ([Barron's, 2019](https://www.barrons.com/articles/john-neff-51559786060); [CFA Institute, 2019](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/blogs/enterprising-investor/2019/in-memoriam-john-b-neff-cfa)).
