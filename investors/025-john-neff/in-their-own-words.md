# John Neff - In Their Own Words

As of 2026-06-28 (UTC), John B. Neff is deceased; multiple institutional and obituary sources put his death on June 4, 2019, at age 87 ([CFA Institute, 2019](https://rpc.cfainstitute.org/blogs/enterprising-investor/2019/in-memoriam-john-b-neff-cfa); [Penn Almanac, 2019](https://almanac.upenn.edu/articles/john-b.-neff-emeritus-trustee); [Dignity Memorial, 2019](https://www.dignitymemorial.com/obituaries/paoli-pa/john-neff-8734677)). This file uses source-visible wording only. Because several popular Neff quotes circulate through quote aggregators, the main quote set below favors the 2006 CFA Institute session, the 1998 Pennsylvania Gazette profile, the 1996 Washington Post article, and contemporaneous notes from the 2006 Financial Analysts Seminar. Short quote snippets are intentionally kept brief; book-attributed lines not located in a primary page image are listed in the attribution watchlist rather than treated as fully verified.

## Quote Handling Notes

Neff's own voice is direct, dry, and operational. The safest opened first-person source is CFA Institute's 2006 nine-page session, which preserves Q&A wording from the Financial Analysts Seminar ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)). The Pennsylvania Gazette profile is also unusually useful because it quotes Neff discussing Penn's endowment, contrarianism, and what a low-P/E portfolio looks like to outsiders ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)). The Motley Fool's three-part 2006 series is secondary, but it was written from Neff's Financial Analysts Seminar appearance and captures several distinctive terms that match the CFA session's themes ([Motley Fool Part 1, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/20/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-1.aspx); [Motley Fool Part 2, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/dividends-income/2006/07/21/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-2.aspx); [Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).

## Verified Short Quotes By Theme

### Low-P/E Discipline And Valuation

1. "low-P/E game" - Neff used this plain phrase for the strategy he continued after Windsor, though in a concentrated personal-account form ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).
2. "on the squash" - his shorthand for looking at stocks making new lows, where discomfort begins the research funnel ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).
3. "market attack" - Neff warned investors not to fear a stock under attack if the business and price were still adequate ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).
4. "decent companies" - the key qualifier in his low-P/E method; he was not buying statistical cheapness alone ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP%E2%80%93Investing.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)) [URL encoding warning: opened source used `Low%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf`; retain the canonical opened PDF link elsewhere if this encoded dash fails].
5. "the crux of it" - how he described the low-P/E equity strategy when asked to pick one important lesson ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).
6. "legitimate business expenses" - his reason for distrusting EBITDA as an evaluation shortcut; interest, taxes, and depreciation counted ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).
7. "anchor to windward" - his nautical phrase for holding fixed income beside concentrated post-Windsor equities ([CFA Institute PDF, 2006](https://static1.1.sqspcdn.com/static/f/172986/1283427/1200961476880/John%2BNeff%2Band%2BLow%2BP-E%2Binvesting.pdf?token=7jFoO8zeFqMbWytb0ovDOXHz9BI%3D)).

### Contrarian Temperament

8. "out of favor" - Neff's plain description of the stocks Penn was buying when the portfolio looked uncomfortable ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
9. "misunderstood" - his preferred description for many holdings that outsiders thought were simply ugly ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
10. "woebegone" - the colorful word attached to companies the market had left behind ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
11. "relatively unloved" - another compact description of the low-expectations universe he preferred ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
12. "pricker of the bubble" - his phrase for the useful part of contrarianism, provided it served shareholders ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
13. "argue with the stock market" - Neff's better career use for the argumentative streak his mother noticed ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
14. "fair maiden" - his storybook image for the payoff when the market eventually recognized a low-P/E case ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).
15. "lonely" - the emotional condition of buying while others are selling; Neff treated it as part of the job ([Penn Gazette, 1998](https://thepenngazette.com/an-eye-for-value/)).

### Buying, Selling, And Risk

16. "buy into weakness" - a concise description of his entry discipline, with averaging down only when room remained ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
17. "hip pocket" - his phrase for leaving capital available when building positions under pressure ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
18. "sell into strength" - the flip side of buying weakness; recognition was a reason to harvest, not brag ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
19. "bought to be sold" - Neff's reminder that Windsor positions were not sacred permanent holdings ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
20. "poor short-term performance" - the risk he thought investors should accept when owning overlooked stocks ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
21. "panic runs amok" - the market condition in which his cash and discipline could be redeployed ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
22. "fat, dumb, and happy" - his warning about investors getting too comfortable with prevailing trends ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
23. "abject desperation" - his description of how far the Street can swing after excessive enthusiasm ([Novel Investor, 2022](https://novelinvestor.com/wise-words-from-john-neff/)).
24. "stick to your guns" - a compact statement of endurance around market inflection points; opened as a source-visible quote carrier tied to the CFA materials ([Wealth Awesome, 2026](https://www.wealthawesome.com/investor-wisdom/investor/john-neff)).

### Fiduciary Standards And Management Judgment

25. "shareholder comes first" - Neff's fiduciary rule in a Washington Post discussion of mutual-fund personal trading ([Washington Post, 1996](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/1996/06/30/mutual-mystery-funds-codes-of-conduct/8ad3d2d8-ac71-42d3-96f3-876f85146e31/)).
26. "psychologically involving" - his reason frequent personal trading could impair fund decision-making ([Washington Post, 1996](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/1996/06/30/mutual-mystery-funds-codes-of-conduct/8ad3d2d8-ac71-42d3-96f3-876f85146e31/)).
27. "tracking error" - the consultant phrase the Motley Fool report says Neff sent packing, a useful clue to his anti-style-box temperament ([Motley Fool Part 1, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/20/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-1.aspx)).
28. "never smart enough" - his self-deprecating explanation for refusing to be boxed into one capitalization segment ([Motley Fool Part 1, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/20/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-1.aspx)).
29. "curbstone opinion" - a phrase for the street-level observation that complemented reading filings and Value Line ([Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).
30. "free plus" - Neff's term for unexpected upside not paid for at entry, including buyouts or favorable surprises ([Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).
31. "way too greedy" - his criticism of hedge-fund compensation economics ([Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).
32. "too much off the top" - the companion phrase for incentive fees that reduce investor returns ([Motley Fool Part 3, 2006](https://www.fool.com/investing/value/2006/07/24/john-neff-legendary-investor-part-3.aspx)).

## Synthesis Of Neff's Voice

Neff's diction is strikingly unpolished in the best way: "squash," "hip pocket," "fair maiden," "hanky-panky," and "anchor to windward" belong to a practitioner explaining how work really feels, not to a consultant packaging a factor model. The content of the quotes matches the track record: buy where expectations are low, insist on business adequacy, leave room to average down, sell after recognition, and do not confuse short-term loneliness with analytical error.

The most transferable part of his language is the insistence on total return and price. The least transferable part is the institutional nerve behind it. A mutual-fund manager can say "poor short-term performance" is acceptable, but shareholders can still redeem. A trustee can say a portfolio should not look comfortable, but an endowment board still has politics. Neff's own words therefore make him less like a low-P/E screen and more like a behavioral system: valuation gave him candidates, but temperament, fiduciary culture, and sell discipline made the candidates compound.
