## Life & Career Timeline

Tepper grew up in Pittsburgh's Stanton Heights neighborhood, the middle child of a public-school teacher and an accountant. New York Magazine's 2010 profile describes an early affinity for numbers, sports statistics, wisecracking, and risk; he began buying penny stocks in high school and worked his way through college before entering finance ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). Carnegie Mellon later summarized the formal path: BA in economics from the University of Pittsburgh in 1978, graduate business degree from CMU in 1982, then a finance career that made him one of the school's signature alumni ([CMU bio, 2025](https://www.cmu.edu/sites/default/files/2025-05/bio-tepper-david.pdf)).

After business school, Tepper worked in corporate treasury and credit roles before joining Goldman Sachs's high-yield group. The important apprenticeship was the distressed and junk-bond desk: Goldman was late to high yield, the 1987 crash and late-1980s credit stress created broken securities, and Tepper's job trained him to look at capital structures under duress. New York Magazine reports that he helped Goldman by buying underlying bonds of damaged financial institutions and expected partnership, but his personality and internal politics worked against him; after being passed over, he left in 1992 ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

In 1993, Tepper started Appaloosa with backing from Jack Walton after trading from a desk lent by Michael Price. Appaloosa began as a distressed-debt and high-yield boutique. Its early edge was not a "quality compounder" mindset; it was willingness to buy legally senior or recovery-sensitive claims when conventional investors were fleeing. The strategy produced feast-or-famine years: the Russia 1998 loss-and-recovery cycle, the 2002 junk-bond collapse followed by a 2003 rebound, and later crisis-era bank securities ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/); [Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

The 2009 financial-crisis trade made Tepper a public investing celebrity. Bloomberg Markets reported that Appaloosa bought banks, preferred shares, debt, and AIG-linked securities while nationalization fears were intense; the flagship fund returned 117.3% for the nine months ended September 30, 2009, and the firm gained $6.5B through that date after a 2008 drawdown ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). New York Magazine later framed the same episode as a bet that the U.S. government would follow through on bank support rather than wipe out private investors ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

The late-career arc is a move away from outside-capital hedge-fund scale toward personal-capital flexibility. Institutional Investor reported in 2019 that Appaloosa planned to return outside capital and eventually become a family office, after years of returning capital to keep the opportunity set manageable ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually)). Separately, Tepper bought the Carolina Panthers for a reported $2.275B in 2018, after previously holding a minority stake in the Pittsburgh Steelers ([NFL.com, 2018](https://www.nfl.com/news/carolina-panthers-finalize-terms-to-sell-franchise-to-david-tep-0ap3000000933033); [Panthers, 2018](https://www.panthers.com/news/panthers-reach-sales-agreement-with-david-tepper-20662986)).

Philanthropy became part of the public biography. Carnegie Mellon reported a $55M 2004 naming gift to the business school and a later $67M gift toward the Tepper Quadrangle, bringing his CMU commitments above $125M by 2013 ([CMU, 2004](https://www.cmu.edu/cmnews/extra/040319_dtepper.html); [CMU, 2013](https://www.cmu.edu/news/stories/archives/2013/november/nov14_teppergift.html)). Charlotte FC's 2026 philanthropy release reported that TIME named Tepper to its 2026 philanthropy list and that he had contributed more than $529M to three giving vehicles the prior year, according to the Chronicle of Philanthropy ([Charlotte FC, 2026](https://www.charlottefootballclub.com/news/david-tepper-named-to-the-2026-time100-philanthropy-list-of-the-100-most-influential-leaders-in-philanthropy)).

## Vehicles & Structure

The investable Tepper record belongs primarily to Appaloosa, not to a public operating company. Bloomberg Markets identified four funds and separate accounts in 2009: Appaloosa Investment, Palomino Fund Ltd., and offshore/onshore Thoroughbred vehicles, with $12B under management across funds and accounts at that time ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). The exact vehicle-by-vehicle record is not fully public, and later press accounts often blend flagship, firmwide, and personal-capital outcomes.

The current public legal structure visible in SEC filings is Appaloosa LP and related entities. The Q1 2026 Form 13F lists Appaloosa LP as the institutional investment manager, Michael L. Palmer as CFO/signatory, 31 reportable long entries, and $5,933,477,986 of 13F value for the quarter ended March 31, 2026 ([SEC 13F cover, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/primary_doc.xml)). The holdings table shows a modern long book led by Amazon, Micron, Alphabet, Uber, Taiwan Semiconductor, Alibaba, Vistra, Nvidia, and Whirlpool, but that is only the SEC-reportable long equity sleeve ([SEC 13F information table, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)).

The 2026 Whirlpool Schedule 13D is useful because it shows how Tepper sits atop the reporting chain in a live activist situation. It identifies Appaloosa LP, Azteca Partners LLC, Palomino Master Ltd., Appaloosa Capital Inc., and David A. Tepper as reporting persons, with Tepper signing as president of Appaloosa Capital Inc. and as an individual. It reports 3,190,207 Whirlpool shares, or 4.9%, on the filing date, and the legal-proceedings disclosure checkbox for Tepper and the reporting persons is not checked ([SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml)).

The capital base changed materially after 2018. Institutional Investor reported Appaloosa at $11.6B of AUM at year-end 2018, down from $16.5B roughly a year earlier, with the decline driven by capital returns, taxes, Tepper's Panthers purchase, and some redemptions rather than solely by investment losses ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswhgrmp6rwew6061728/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-sees-steep-decline-in-assets)). The same outlet reported that Appaloosa had returned capital in eight of the prior nine years and preferred to maximize returns on Tepper's own capital rather than asset-gathering fees ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually)).

## Track Record Detail With Caveats

The headline record is extraordinary, but it needs precise labels. Institutional Investor reported in 2019 that Appaloosa had compounded at more than 25% annually net of all fees since inception in 1993; New York Magazine reported 30% net annualized over the first 17 years; Bloomberg Markets reported 30.7% average return to investors for the flagship in its 2010 profile package ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/); [Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). These numbers are directionally consistent, but they are not a public audited monthly return series, so later tasks should keep the [secondary-source] caveat unless original investor letters or audited performance schedules are found.

The open record is strongest around drawdowns followed by explosive rebounds. Institutional Investor reported Appaloosa Investment I up 67% in 2001, down 25% in 2002, and up 149% in 2003, with Conseco and Marconi among the winners ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)). New York Magazine independently describes the same 2002-2003 pattern as a junk-bond collapse followed by a rebound in bankrupt-company bonds ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

The best-documented single cycle is 2008-2009. Bloomberg Markets reported that Appaloosa Investment LP I fell 26.7% in 2008, then returned 117.3% for the nine months ended September 30, 2009; firmwide gains were $6.5B through that date. New York Magazine later reported Appaloosa made more than $7.5B in the 2009 panic-to-recovery trade, while other sources use roughly $6.5B-$7B depending on period and measurement. The conservative profile statement is that Appaloosa's crisis trade made about $6.5B-$7.5B firmwide in 2009 and turned a prior-year drawdown into one of the hedge-fund industry's defining wins ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

There are also caveats. In 2010, the SEC found that Appaloosa Management violated Rule 105 in connection with short sales before a Wells Fargo public offering, ordered disgorgement, interest, a civil penalty, and compliance undertakings; Appaloosa settled without admitting or denying the findings except jurisdiction ([SEC order, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)). In Washington Mutual, a bankruptcy-court opinion found colorable claims around trading while receiving restructuring information, but the opened record does not show a final liability finding against Tepper personally; Appaloosa denied wrongdoing in contemporaneous reporting ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf); [Business Insider, 2011](https://www.businessinsider.com/david-tepper-insider-trading-wamu-2011-2)). In 2024-2026, Appaloosa-linked entities pursued Credit Suisse AT1 litigation; Bloomberg Law reported in March 2026 that securities-fraud claims could proceed while racketeering claims were insufficiently pleaded, which is a live litigation milestone, not a merits finding ([Swissinfo/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/appaloosa-sues-credit-suisse-over-%2417-billion-at1-bond-wipeout/76316361); [Bloomberg Law, 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/ubs-stuck-with-securities-claims-over-credit-suisse-bond-wipeout)).

## Why They Matter

Tepper belongs in the Canon because he is one of the clearest examples of credit-trained crisis investing at public-market scale. He did not build his record by buying great businesses and waiting. He built it by asking whether a broken security's implied disaster was too severe, whether a legal or policy mechanism could change the distribution of outcomes, and which part of the capital structure offered the best asymmetry.

The practical contribution is threefold. First, he shows that a distressed investor can cross asset classes without abandoning a core method: downside first, catalyst second, sizing third. Second, he shows that temperament can be an edge if it is paired with evidence. Buying bank securities in early 2009 required social and emotional tolerance for looking wrong, but the trade worked because it was tied to Treasury terms and conversion math, not just bravado. Third, he demonstrates that capital structure matters as much as stock selection. Appaloosa could buy common, preferred, bank debt, distressed bonds, or later activist equity depending on where panic was most mispriced.

The caution is equally important. Tepper's method can confuse policy incentive with policy guarantee; Russia 1998 and Credit Suisse AT1s are reminders that governments and regulators can change outcomes in ways spreadsheets do not control. The public cannot safely copy Appaloosa's 13F because the filing omits shorts, credit, hedges, cash, derivative overlays, cost basis, and intraperiod trading. The transferable lesson is process, not cloning.
