# David Tepper - Key Writings

As of 2026-06-25T20:52:00Z, no book-length investing work, public Appaloosa annual-letter archive, or authorized collection of David Tepper writings was found in open sources. This file therefore treats "key writings" as the public record Tepper and Appaloosa actually left: signed activist letters, legal filings, university speeches and statements, long interviews/transcript carriers, and serious reporter reconstructions. That is a narrower and less elegant corpus than Buffett letters or Oaktree memos, but it is the right evidence base for a private distressed-credit hedge-fund manager.

## Evidence and Access Notes

Tepper is a living investor whose main vehicle has been private. The strongest first-party materials found are the 2018 Carnegie Mellon commencement transcript, the 2026 Whirlpool letter signed by Tepper, a 2015 TerraForm Power Schedule 13D exhibit letter, CMU gift statements, and SEC filings that identify Appaloosa structures and positions. The best near-primary investing interviews are CNBC appearances, but full official CNBC transcripts were not found; where transcript carriers are used, this file labels them as such. The best works about Tepper are long-form profiles and Institutional Investor/Bloomberg pieces that report Appaloosa performance, crisis-trade mechanics, capital returns, and legal/process risks.

## Works by Tepper or Appaloosa

### 1. Appaloosa letter to Whirlpool board, 2026

**Central thesis.** This is the cleanest current first-party Appaloosa investment text. In a letter published by PR Newswire and signed by David A. Tepper, Appaloosa argues that Whirlpool's equity issuance imposed unnecessary dilution, that management failed to exploit strategic opportunities created by tariffs, and that the board should focus on shareholder value rather than management entrenchment ([Appaloosa/PR Newswire, 2026](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/appaloosa-sends-letter-to-whirlpool-board-of-directors-302697107.html)).

**Key ideas.** The letter is an activist version of Tepper's distressed playbook: identify a broken capital-allocation decision, link it to a measurable cost of capital, make the governance failure legible to other owners, and press for strategic alternatives. It is not a generic complaint about a low stock price; it alleges that a specific financing decision damaged existing shareholders and that strategic inaction reflects incentives rather than necessity.

**Best sections.** The most useful section is the body of the letter after Appaloosa frames the equity issuance, especially the argument that tariff policy created a window for partnerships or combinations with foreign manufacturers. The closing fiduciary-duty paragraph is also important because it shows Tepper's public activist tone: direct, confrontational, and board-centered.

**Use with caveats.** The letter is adversarial advocacy, not neutral analysis. It should be read beside the March 2026 Schedule 13D, which shows the reporting-person chain, beneficial ownership, source-of-funds language, and Tepper's signature as reporting person ([SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml)).

### 2. Appaloosa letter to TerraForm Power CEO, 2015

**Central thesis.** Filed as Exhibit D to a Schedule 13D amendment, this December 2015 letter argues that TerraForm Power needed independent leadership and arm's-length governance from SunEdison to restore market confidence and protect the company's renewable-asset mandate ([SEC Schedule 13D/A exhibit, 2015](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1599947/000090571815000963/appaloosa_13dadec72015.htm)).

**Key ideas.** The TerraForm letter is the earlier public template for the Whirlpool campaign. Appaloosa emphasizes governance mechanics, related-party risk, board independence, and the need to honor the economic promise embedded in a public vehicle. It shows Tepper's activist voice before the later Whirlpool fight: less headline-grabbing than the 2026 letter, but similarly focused on incentive alignment and capital-market trust.

**Best sections.** The exhibit letter itself begins near the end of the filing and is the core text. The surrounding Schedule 13D signatures matter because they show Tepper signing across Appaloosa Investment, Palomino, Thoroughbred, Appaloosa Management, Appaloosa Partners, and personally, which helps map the firm's public activist structure.

**Use with caveats.** This is still a campaign document. It reveals Appaloosa's stated governance logic, not a final proof that the trade worked or that every allegation was correct.

### 3. Carnegie Mellon commencement address, 2018

**Central thesis.** The official CMU transcript is Tepper's best long-form first-person public speech. It is not an investing lecture; it is a temperament document. Tepper frames life as adversity, identity, family, work, and blunt honesty rather than polish. The remarks are useful because they expose the psychological style behind Appaloosa: irreverence, anti-pretension, risk tolerance, and identification with outsiders ([CMU commencement transcript, 2018](https://www.cmu.edu/news/stories/archives/2018/march/2018CMUCommencementSpeech-DavidTepper.pdf)).

**Key ideas.** The speech stresses personal history, resilience, gratitude to parents, and the value of real experience over ceremonial perfection. It also reinforces a recurring Tepper theme from profiles and interviews: he distrusts ornate presentation and prefers facts, effort, and directness. For investors, the relevance is temperament rather than technique.

**Best sections.** The opening, where he discards the idea of a professional polished speech, is the clearest guide to his public voice. The adversity section, where he describes challenges and triumphs, is the closest thing to a personal operating philosophy.

**Use with caveats.** Do not overread the speech as investment process. It belongs in a key-writings file because it is first-person and revealing, not because it teaches distressed-credit valuation.

### 4. Carnegie Mellon gift statements, 2004 and 2013

**Central thesis.** Tepper's two major CMU gift announcements show his public explanation of analytical training and entrepreneurship. In 2004, CMU quoted him saying the school's education shaped his ability to analyze investment options; in 2013, he emphasized a vision of CMU as an entrepreneurial hub ([CMU, 2004](https://www.cmu.edu/cmnews/extra/040319_dtepper.html); [CMU, 2013](https://www.cmu.edu/news/stories/archives/2013/november/nov14_teppergift.html)).

**Key ideas.** These statements matter because they tie Tepper's self-understanding to analytical education, interdisciplinary problem solving, and practical entrepreneurship. They also explain why CMU is not merely philanthropy in his biography; it is part of how he narrates his edge.

**Best sections.** The 2004 statement is best for the analytical-investing link. The 2013 statement is best for his broader institutional and entrepreneurial vision.

**Use with caveats.** University gift announcements are polished institutional communications. They are primary for what Tepper publicly chose to say, but not a source for investment returns.

### 5. CNBC Squawk Box interview, 2010 (transcript carrier)

**Central thesis.** The 2010 CNBC interview, preserved in an unofficial transcript carrier, is Tepper's most important public investing interview. He discusses Appaloosa's record, the 2009 bank trade, his willingness to move across asset classes, and the post-crisis policy setup often summarized as "don't fight the Fed" ([A Letter a Day transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010)).

**Key ideas.** The interview presents Appaloosa as opportunistic rather than style-boxed: credit roots, but broad asset-class freedom. Tepper describes a long-run record that was exceptional but uneven; the phrase "inconsistently consistent" captures the rhythm of big drawdowns and rebounds. The policy framework is scenario-based: if the economy improves, risk assets work; if it weakens, policy easing can support them.

**Best sections.** The opening record discussion, the cross-asset universe discussion, and the Fed/QE discussion are the most important. They should be used carefully because the transcript is not hosted by CNBC and exact wording should be treated as carrier evidence.

**Use with caveats.** This source is highly useful but not ideal. Future agents should replace it with an official CNBC transcript or archived video transcript if one becomes available.

### 6. CNBC China interview, 2024, and CNBC/Fed interview, 2025

**Central thesis.** Tepper's 2024 and 2025 CNBC appearances, available through secondary recaps and transcript carriers, show the late-career Appaloosa framework in public: policy inflection plus valuation, with position limits relaxed when he sees an unusual setup. The 2024 China interview argued that Chinese stimulus and buyback support made Chinese equities attractive; the 2025 interview framed U.S. equities as expensive but still supported by Fed easing ([Markets Insider/Business Insider, 2024](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-buys-chinese-stocks-country-launches-fiscal-stimulus-bazooka-2024-9); [MarketWatch, 2025](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/billionaire-david-tepper-is-feeling-miserable-about-stocks-but-wont-fight-the-fed-74d96d45); [A Letter a Day transcript carrier, 2025](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-298-david-tepper-2025)).

**Key ideas.** These interviews show Tepper still using the crisis-investor template after Appaloosa's family-office shift: price, policy, liquidity, and willingness to be uncomfortable. In China, the emphasis is on large official easing, buybacks, and low multiples; in the U.S., the tension is between valuation discomfort and not fighting easier money.

**Best sections.** The 2024 China recap is best for policy-plus-price reasoning; the 2025 transcript carrier is best as an interview index, because it flags topics including tariffs, industrial policy, Nvidia, China, valuation, credit spreads, sizing, and liquidity.

**Use with caveats.** These are interview recaps/transcript carriers, not investor letters. Use them for public reasoning, not position-level P&L. Cross-check with 13F filings where the claim concerns public long holdings; for example, Appaloosa's Q1 2026 13F shows the later public long book and the remaining China exposure, but 13F omits shorts, derivatives, cash, credit, and intraperiod trades ([SEC 13F cover, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/primary_doc.xml); [SEC 13F information table, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)).

### 7. SEC filings as Appaloosa public documents

**Central thesis.** SEC filings are not essays, but for Tepper they are essential primary texts. They reveal the public slice of a private investor's structure, holdings, activist posture, signatures, and legal boundaries. The 2026 13F cover page and information table disclose the report date, Appaloosa LP manager identity, 31 reportable entries, and a $5.93 billion listed long-equity value; the Whirlpool 13D maps beneficial ownership and the reporting-person chain ([SEC 13F cover, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/primary_doc.xml); [SEC 13F information table, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml); [SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml)).

**Key ideas.** The filings teach humility. Appaloosa's public equity book can be studied, but it is not the full portfolio. The investor should distinguish manager identity, reporting period, long-only U.S.-listed positions, and legal ownership from the actual economic exposure of a private fund or family office.

**Best sections.** For 13Fs, the cover page and information table together are the useful pair. For 13Ds, Item 2 (identity/background), Item 3 (source of funds), Item 4 (purpose), and exhibits are the key reading order.

**Use with caveats.** Never clone the 13F as if it were the strategy. It is delayed, partial, long-only, and silent on cost basis and hedging.

## Best Works About Tepper, Ranked

### 1. Richard Teitelbaum, "Bullish at the Brink," Bloomberg Markets, 2010

This is the best single secondary source on Tepper's 2009 crisis trade. It reports the flagship Appaloosa Investment LP I 117.3% return through September 2009, firmwide gains of $6.5 billion through September 30, the 2008 drawdown, the bank-preferred conversion logic, and capital-structure instruments across common, preferred, and junior debt ([Bloomberg Markets via Opalesque PDF, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

Why it ranks first: it ties numbers to mechanics. Many Tepper profiles celebrate the trade; this one explains why the Treasury terms changed the odds and how Appaloosa expressed the bet.

### 2. Jessica Pressler, "Ready to Be Rich," New York Magazine, 2010

This is the best narrative profile. It covers Tepper's background, Goldman exit, Appaloosa launch, personality, 1998 Russia loss-and-recovery, 2002-2003 distressed rebound, 2009 bank trade, public hero worship, and SEC penalty context ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

Why it ranks second: it captures temperament better than any other source. It is also valuable because it is not purely admiring; it includes confidence, profanity, gender/culture cues, legal/regulatory context, and the risk that bravado can become process risk.

### 3. Institutional Investor, "The Bucks Stop Here," 2004

This is the best early Appaloosa performance source. It reports Appaloosa Investment I's 67% gain in 2001, 25% loss in 2002, and 149% net rebound in 2003, plus early asset and capital-return context ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

Why it ranks third: it predates the 2009 fame and therefore shows Tepper as a distressed-credit investor before the bank-trade legend hardened.

### 4. Institutional Investor, family-office transition profile, 2019

This piece explains the late-career structure: capital returns, Appaloosa's planned family-office conversion, and the more-than-25% net compounding claim since 1993 ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually)).

Why it ranks fourth: it is the best bridge between classic Appaloosa and the opaque modern personal-capital vehicle. It also reinforces that capacity management is part of Tepper's edge.

### 5. Institutional Investor Rich List, 2026

The 2026 Rich List is useful for a current check on Appaloosa's owner-capital era. It reports Tepper's 2025 gain, Alibaba's role, the size of the public long portfolio, and the fact that the bulk of the firm's capital belongs to Tepper ([Institutional Investor, 2026](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/hedge-funds/rich-list-institutional-investors-25th-annual-ranking-highest-earning-hedge)).

Why it ranks fifth: it is current and connected to realized investor outcomes, but it is a ranking summary rather than a deep process source.

### 6. SEC Rule 105 order, 2010

The SEC order is the best primary legal source for Appaloosa's Wells Fargo offering violation. It states the Rule 105 framework, the SEC's findings, and the sanctions; Appaloosa consented without admitting or denying findings except jurisdiction ([SEC order, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)).

Why it belongs: Tepper research cannot be only performance and personality. The legal record is part of the process-quality file, especially for distressed and event-driven investors operating near offerings and restructurings.

### 7. Washington Mutual bankruptcy opinion and Jones Day analysis, 2011

The WaMu materials are the best source cluster for distressed-investor information-boundary risk. The bankruptcy court found colorable claims around temporary-insider status for settlement noteholders, while Jones Day explains the equitable-disallowance and insider-trading implications in plainer language ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf); [Jones Day, 2011](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2011/12/wamu-confirmation-denied-interest-rates-equitable-disallowance-and-insider-trading)).

Why it belongs: it is a warning source. It does not prove securities-law liability against Tepper personally, but it shows the legal complexity Appaloosa's strategy can encounter.

### 8. AT1 litigation sources, 2024-2026

Swissinfo/Bloomberg reports Appaloosa-linked plaintiffs' Credit Suisse AT1 lawsuit, and Bloomberg Law reports the March 2026 threshold ruling allowing securities-fraud claims to proceed while rejecting insufficient racketeering pleadings ([Swissinfo/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/appaloosa-sues-credit-suisse-over-%2417-billion-at1-bond-wipeout/76316361); [Bloomberg Law, 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/ubs-stuck-with-securities-claims-over-credit-suisse-bond-wipeout)).

Why it belongs: it is current, legal, and directly tied to Appaloosa's core question: what happens when the investor reads the capital structure one way and the state/regulator resolves it another way?

### 9. MarketWatch and Business Insider recaps of CNBC interviews, 2024-2025

These pieces are best used as secondary access points to recent Tepper reasoning on China stimulus, U.S. valuation, Fed cuts, liquidity, and position sizing ([Markets Insider/Business Insider, 2024](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-buys-chinese-stocks-country-launches-fiscal-stimulus-bazooka-2024-9); [MarketWatch, 2025](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/billionaire-david-tepper-is-feeling-miserable-about-stocks-but-wont-fight-the-fed-74d96d45)).

Why they rank lower: they are not full transcripts and should be replaced by official CNBC transcripts if available. They remain useful because they preserve recent public reasoning and can be cross-checked against 13F filings.

## Reading Path for Future Agents

Start with Bloomberg Markets 2010, New York Magazine 2010, and Institutional Investor 2004 to understand the classic Appaloosa record. Then read the 2010 CNBC transcript carrier to hear Tepper's own post-crisis framework. Next read the 2026 Whirlpool letter, the 2015 TerraForm letter, and the related 13D filings to understand the activist/governance expression of the same method. Finish with the SEC Rule 105 order, WaMu court/Jones Day sources, and AT1 litigation sources to keep the file non-hagiographic. Use 13Fs only as current public-position evidence, never as a full portfolio map.
