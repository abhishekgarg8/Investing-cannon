## Evidence Notes and Ranking Method

The strongest evidence is for Appaloosa's 2009 financials trade and the 2002-2003 distressed-credit rebound. Those trades are covered by multiple independent sources and have fund-level return figures, even though the underlying investor letters and full position ledgers are not public. Later trades have cleaner SEC position data but weaker realized-P&L evidence because Appaloosa is now largely a family-office-style vehicle and 13F reports reveal only listed long positions after quarter-end.

Ranking below uses: absolute P&L, percentage return, evidence quality, difficulty of the path, and transferability of the lesson. Figures that are not independently triangulated are labeled single-source or press-reported.

## 1. The Single Best Trade: 2009 U.S. Financials Panic

**Context and dates.** The trade began in late 2008 and accelerated in February-March 2009, when investors feared nationalization or wipeout across U.S. banks and insurers. Bloomberg Markets reported that Appaloosa bought financial-company securities after Treasury's bank-support framework changed the nationalization odds, and that Bank of America and Citigroup shares rebounded 330% and 223%, respectively, from February 28 through September 2009 ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). The Guardian's December 2009 account independently described the same bet as a wager that Citigroup and Bank of America would survive without nationalization ([Guardian, 2009](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2009/dec/22/hedge-fund-financial-crisis-us-banks)).

**Thesis and discovery.** Tepper's key read was that policy documents and conversion terms made outright nationalization less likely than market prices implied. Bloomberg reported that the preferred-share conversion prices embedded in the government's bank support were above then-market common stock prices, making the common look severely underpriced if the government intended private-market recovery rather than wipeout ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

**Size and structure.** This was a capital-structure trade, not just a common-stock punt. Bloomberg says Appaloosa owned common and preferred shares plus junior subordinated debt across Bank of America, Citigroup, Fifth Third, SunTrust, AIG, Washington Mutual, and Wachovia-related securities, with peak exposure of about 30% of Appaloosa assets across financial companies ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). Institutional Investor later summarized Tepper's method as moving around bonds, preferreds, bank debt, and equity to find the best value within the capital structure ([Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgea9afk8qds2jxjlds/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-management-among-first-quarter-hedge-fund-leaders)).

**Entry and path.** Entry occurred while prices and confidence were still falling. Business Insider wrote in December 2009 that Tepper instructed traders to buy when nationalization fears were intense and that he was told at one point he was nearly alone among major buyers ([Business Insider, 2009](https://www.businessinsider.com/hedge-fund-manager-rubs-brass-balls-makes-7-billion-2009-12)). The path included the prior year's damage: Appaloosa Investment I had dropped 26.7% in 2008 before the 2009 snapback ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

**Exit and P&L.** The headline figures differ by measurement date. Bloomberg reported Appaloosa Investment I up 117.3% for the nine months ended September 30, 2009, and the firm up $6.5 billion through that date. Business Insider and The Guardian reported roughly $7 billion of 2009 profits by December, with Tepper's personal take estimated around $2.5 billion in those contemporaneous accounts ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf); [Business Insider, 2009](https://www.businessinsider.com/hedge-fund-manager-rubs-brass-balls-makes-7-billion-2009-12); [Guardian, 2009](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2009/dec/22/hedge-fund-financial-crisis-us-banks)). Institutional Investor later put Tepper's 2009 personal earnings near $4 billion including gains on his own capital and fees, while reporting Appaloosa Investment I's 2009 net return as 133% and Palomino's as 129% ([Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgea9afk8qds2jxjlds/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-management-among-first-quarter-hedge-fund-leaders)). The best conservative statement is: Appaloosa made roughly $6.5-$7 billion in 2009 from the financials-led crisis trade, with the flagship fund returning about 117%-133% depending on source and measurement period.

**What it teaches.** Tepper's greatest trade was not simply contrarian courage. It combined credit analysis, policy interpretation, capital-structure flexibility, and the willingness to size heavily when the downside scenario became less likely than the market price implied.

## 2. AIG, WaMu, and Wachovia: The Crisis Trade's Credit Sleeves

**Context and dates.** These were part of the same 2008-2009 financials complex, but they deserve separate treatment because they show Tepper working below the common stock. Bloomberg says Appaloosa bought Washington Mutual and Wachovia bonds and preferreds in late 2008 at about 20 cents on the dollar, then bought AIG debt in early 2009 at about 10 cents on the dollar ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The thesis was that securities above common equity could recover sharply if acquirers, government support, or restructuring outcomes kept the institutions alive. Wachovia's sale to Wells Fargo and JPMorgan's acquisition of WaMu assets improved recovery expectations. AIG debt similarly offered a security where survival, not a clean business turnaround, was the main underwriting question.

**Size and structure.** Exact position size by security is not public. Bloomberg gives the best structure detail: preferreds, bonds, and debt bought at distressed cents-on-the-dollar prices, within a broader financials allocation that peaked near 30% of assets ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

**Entry and path.** These entries happened amid an unstable policy environment. WaMu had failed, Wachovia required a rescue transaction, and AIG had received extraordinary government support. The drawdown risk was not a mark-to-model inconvenience; a wrong legal or policy outcome could have meant deep impairment.

**Exit and P&L.** Bloomberg reports Wachovia preferreds doubled after the Wells Fargo transaction and AIG bonds bought around 10 cents traded near 61 cents by December 2009. Security-level dollar profit is not disclosed, so this is a high-confidence multi-bagger price path but not a fully auditable P&L line ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

**What it teaches.** The credit sleeves show why Tepper is dangerous in a panic: he can choose the part of the capital structure where market fear is maximal but survival upside is still large.

## 3. 2002-2003 Bankruptcy Debt Rebound: Conseco, Marconi, Enron, WorldCom

**Context and dates.** The junk-bond market broke badly in 2002. New York Magazine reports Appaloosa lost 25% that year, then rebounded the next year when bankrupt-company debt Tepper had bought appreciated about 150% ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). Institutional Investor independently reports Appaloosa Investment I fell 25% in 2002 and returned 149% in 2003, net of fees ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The trade was a classic distressed-credit bottom. Tepper bought debt in companies whose headlines were toxic but whose bankruptcy recoveries could be materially better than market prices implied. Institutional Investor names Conseco bonds and Marconi debt as the biggest winners. New York Magazine adds Enron, WorldCom, and Conseco as then-largest bankruptcies whose debt appreciated after emergence or restructuring progress ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

**Size and structure.** Exact position sizes are not public. The best size proxy is fund-level: Appaloosa had about $2.8 billion under management at year-end 2003 after returning about $1.3 billion to investors, including more than $800 million at year-end, according to Institutional Investor ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

**Entry and path.** Tepper entered during the 2002 collapse, not after the turn was visible. The path required enduring a -25% fund year. The reward came in 2003 as distressed and junk markets recovered; the Wall Street Journal reported in August 2003 that Appaloosa was up 76% for the year and highlighted Marconi, where Appaloosa owned a reported 14% equity stake after the restructuring path ([Wall Street Journal, 2003](https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB106124546292139900)).

**Exit and P&L.** New York Magazine says the 2003 rebound drove a 148% fund return and that Tepper personally took home about $500 million, then his personal best ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). Institutional Investor's 149% fund-return figure corroborates the scale, though not the exact personal-payout math ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

**What it teaches.** The trade was not about forecasting a normal economy. It was about buying claims whose legal and recovery values had become detached from panic prices, while preserving enough capital and investor patience to survive the bad year before the good one.

## 4. 2001 California Utilities: PG&E and Edison-Linked Distress

**Context and dates.** During the California power crisis, utility credits and equities were distressed by political, regulatory, and liquidity uncertainty. Institutional Investor reports Appaloosa Investment I surged 67% in 2001, then fell 25% in 2002 ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The published trade detail is thinner here. Hedge Fund Alpha and a recycled early profile report that Tepper bought PG&E and Edison-linked securities when the companies were near bankruptcy and their credit ratings had fallen to junk, expecting that essential-service politics and regulatory action would prevent a total wipeout ([Hedge Fund Alpha, 2014](https://hedgefundalpha.com/david-teppers-bio-quotes-videos-recent-buys-news-resource-page/?srsltid=AfmBOoref1bOei8Rh5hSseQvhsFmjuWtVh8VMrbrZNGZfsH4CmeVd_Fn); [Moneymunch, 2011](https://moneymunch.com/david-tepper/)). Because these sources appear to share lineage, the security-level details should be treated as single-source until an investor letter, 13F, or contemporaneous newspaper report is found.

**Size and structure.** Not disclosed. The account says Tepper bought millions of shares in the low teens and sold in the mid-twenties, but this is not independently verified and should remain marked single-source ([Hedge Fund Alpha, 2014](https://hedgefundalpha.com/david-teppers-bio-quotes-videos-recent-buys-news-resource-page/?srsltid=AfmBOoref1bOei8Rh5hSseQvhsFmjuWtVh8VMrbrZNGZfsH4CmeVd_Fn)).

**Entry and path.** The trade required buying when bankruptcy and political intervention were live risks. Unlike the 2009 bank trade, where federal support became the explicit catalyst, the utility trade depended on a state/regulatory solution around essential-service providers.

**Exit and P&L.** Fund-level 2001 return is corroborated by Institutional Investor at 67%. Security-level P&L is unverified beyond the repeated secondary account ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)).

**What it teaches.** This was an early form of Tepper's policy-backstop trade: buy when the market prices failure, but only where the social or political cost of failure makes support plausible.

## 5. Russia 1998: Worst Trade, Then Recovery Discipline

**Context and dates.** In 1998, Tepper bought Russian debt before Russia devalued and defaulted. New York Magazine reports the default cost the fund hundreds of millions, but that Tepper kept buying cheaper bonds and the fund later rose 60% ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). In a 2010 CNBC transcript carrier, Tepper identified Russia 1998 as his worst trade and said the error was assuming Russia might devalue but should not default after receiving IMF support ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The thesis was policy and sovereign rationality: Russia had incentives and international support not to default. The flaw was assuming that a sovereign would behave as the spreadsheet implied.

**Size and structure.** Exact security sizes are not public. The loss was large enough to dominate the fund year and force liquidity decisions in other emerging-market and high-yield holdings, according to Tepper's CNBC recollection and New York Magazine's reporting ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

**Entry and path.** This was the ugliest path among the trades in this file. Tepper was too long and could not exit the Russian securities at acceptable prices. The later recovery came from adding or holding when prices were lower, but the initial underwriting error was real.

**Exit and P&L.** The available sources support a loss followed by a strong rebound, not a clean audited P&L. Treat the 60% recovery figure as profile-reported and the exact hundreds-of-millions loss as single-source. It belongs in a greatest-trades file only because it became part of Tepper's later crisis muscle memory; it also belongs in the mistakes file.

**What it teaches.** Policy support is not policy control. This trade foreshadows the 2009 success while warning that sovereign and legal outcomes can break even a sophisticated credit thesis.

## 6. 2010 QE2 / Risk-Asset Policy Trade

**Context and dates.** After the 2009 financials win, Tepper publicly argued in September 2010 that Federal Reserve policy made the distribution of risk assets favorable if the economy weakened, while a normal recovery would also support equities. The transcript used here is an unofficial carrier of a CNBC Squawk Box interview and should be treated as a transcript-carrier source, not an official CNBC transcript ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The thesis was the "policy put": either the economy improved, which helped earnings and risk assets, or the Fed eased, which lifted asset prices. This was not the same as saying every asset was cheap; it was a macro scenario tree with both branches favoring non-Treasury risk.

**Size and structure.** Public sources do not provide a full book. Institutional Investor reported in April 2010 that Appaloosa's main funds were already up 14.5% net in the first quarter, and that Tepper had bought collateralized mortgage-backed securities early in the year while also selling some credit-related securities and equities ([Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgea9afk8qds2jxjlds/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-management-among-first-quarter-hedge-fund-leaders)).

**Entry and path.** This was a less distressed entry than 2009, but still psychologically difficult because the debate centered on deflation, double-dip recession, inflation, and the limits of quantitative easing. The trade's public expression helped create the market label often called the "Tepper rally," but sourceable position-level entry is limited.

**Exit and P&L.** The best open evidence supports a profitable 2010 for Appaloosa, but the exact QE2 trade P&L is not public. Treat this as a successful policy-cycle trade, not an auditable single-position score.

**What it teaches.** Tepper's edge can migrate from distressed balance sheets to central-bank reaction functions, but the method is the same: map the scenarios, find the branch where market pricing is too pessimistic, and own the security basket with the best asymmetry.

## 7. China Stimulus Equity Basket: Alibaba, PDD, JD, Baidu, FXI, KWEB

**Context and dates.** In September 2024, Tepper argued publicly that Chinese policy had shifted enough to warrant buying Chinese equities. Markets Insider reported his bullish comments after stimulus measures and buyback encouragement, and his view that valuations were low relative to growth ([Markets Insider, 2024](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-buys-chinese-stocks-country-launches-fiscal-stimulus-bazooka-2024-9)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The trade echoed 2009 and 2010: policy change plus depressed valuation. The difference is jurisdictional and governance risk. Chinese equities had been cheap for reasons that included property stress, geopolitics, ADR risk, and policy unpredictability.

**Size and structure.** Here the filings are much cleaner. Appaloosa's Q3 2024 13F showed 10.0 million Alibaba ADS worth about $1.06 billion, 5.30 million PDD ADS worth about $714.6 million, 7.3 million JD.com ADS worth $292.0 million, 1.425 million Baidu ADS worth $150.0 million, 5.835 million FXI ETF shares worth $185.4 million, and 3.765 million KWEB shares worth $128.1 million ([SEC Q3 2024 13F information table](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645624000004/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)). Q4 2024 showed the China exposure still large, including 11.84 million Alibaba ADS and 10.47 million JD.com ADS ([SEC Q4 2024 13F information table](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645625000001/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)).

**Entry and path.** Public comments came after a sharp China stimulus rally. MarketWatch later reported that Appaloosa increased China exposure into Q4 2024 as the initial rally faded, consistent with Tepper saying he would buy pullbacks ([MarketWatch, 2025](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/billionaire-david-tepper-built-on-his-everything-china-bet-as-rally-faded-0c5dada5)).

**Exit and P&L.** By Q1 2026, Appaloosa still held China exposure but had reduced several positions: Alibaba was down to 3.465 million ADS, JD.com to 1.305 million ADS, PDD to 900,000 ADS, and KWEB to 1.08 million shares ([SEC Q1 2026 13F information table](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)). Because 13F reports quarter-end long holdings, not cost basis, short exposure, derivatives, or intraperiod trades, realized P&L cannot be computed from public filings alone.

**What it teaches.** The China trade shows the modern Tepper playbook still favors policy inflection plus cheap assets, but the evidence standard is different: filings prove exposure, not profitability.

## 8. Whirlpool Activist Campaign: Open Trade, Not Yet a Completed Great Trade

**Context and dates.** On March 3, 2026, Appaloosa filed a Schedule 13D for Whirlpool after sending a February 25 letter criticizing Whirlpool's equity issuance, tariff strategy, and management entrenchment concerns ([SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml); [Appaloosa letter, 2026](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/appaloosa-sends-letter-to-whirlpool-board-of-directors-302697107.html)).

**Thesis and discovery.** The thesis was capital allocation and governance. Appaloosa argued Whirlpool had issued equity at a high cost, failed to exploit strategic options tied to tariffs, and needed to act more clearly for shareholder value.

**Size and structure.** The 13D reported 3,735,655 shares, or about 5.8%, on the event date, and 3,190,207 shares, or about 4.9%, as of filing date. It reported total cost of $265.1 million for the shares the reporting persons could be deemed to beneficially own as of filing date, with shares held through Azteca and Palomino and control attributed through Appaloosa Capital Inc. and Tepper ([SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml)). Q1 2026 13F later showed 1.95 million Whirlpool shares valued at $105.1 million at quarter-end ([SEC Q1 2026 13F information table](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)).

**Entry and path.** The campaign was launched publicly after a contested equity issuance. The 13D says the reporting persons had no intention, as filed, to pursue a control transaction or contested director election, but reserved the right to change plans.

**Exit and P&L.** No completed exit or campaign outcome is public as of this file's date. This should be monitored by later tasks; it is not yet a greatest trade by results.

**What it teaches.** Tepper's distressed instincts can express as activism when the perceived mispricing is governance and capital allocation rather than bankruptcy recovery.

## Legal and Process Risk Check

The main trade-adjacent legal caveat is Washington Mutual. In 2011, the Delaware bankruptcy court found that equity holders had stated a colorable claim for equitable disallowance based on alleged insider trading by settlement noteholders, including Appaloosa-related parties in the broader noteholder group, and stayed prosecution pending mediation ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court opinion, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf); [Jones Day, 2011](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2011/12/wamu-confirmation-denied-interest-rates-equitable-disallowance-and-insider-trading)). This was not a final securities-law liability finding against Tepper personally. A later Washington Mutual SEC filing states that the standing-motion portions of the September opinion/order were vacated in connection with plan resolution, and Appaloosa denied wrongdoing in contemporaneous reporting ([Washington Mutual SEC 8-K, 2012](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/933136/000090951812000087/jg02-2712_8ke23.htm); [Business Insider, 2011](https://www.businessinsider.com/david-tepper-insider-trading-wamu-2011-2)).
