## Named Heuristics & Frameworks

### 1. Policy plus price, not policy alone

Tepper's most durable mental model is to ask whether policy, liquidity, and price are all pointing in the same direction. He does not simply buy because central banks or governments are supportive. He buys when the market price still implies a disaster that a credible policy mechanism has made less likely. In 2009, the key evidence was not optimism about the economy; it was the Treasury bank-support framework and conversion math that made full nationalization less likely than market prices implied. Bloomberg Markets reported that Appaloosa invested a peak 30% of assets across financial-company capital structures and bought bank and insurer securities at distressed prices as those policy signals changed the odds ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

This same model appears in later macro trades. In 2010, Tepper's public QE framework was essentially a two-branch decision tree: if the economy recovered, earnings and risk assets could work; if it did not, the Fed could support assets with further easing. The accessible transcript is a carrier rather than an official CNBC transcript, so it should be used cautiously, but it matches the broader record of his policy-reaction style ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010)). In 2024, the China thesis again used policy plus price: Fed easing gave China room to ease, Chinese authorities announced rate cuts, liquidity support, and buyback encouragement, and large Chinese equities were still trading at depressed multiples ([Markets Insider, 2024](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-buys-chinese-stocks-country-launches-fiscal-stimulus-bazooka-2024-9)).

### 2. Find the best security in the capital structure

Tepper is often described as a distressed investor, but the operative rule is more specific: once the broad situation is attractive, search the full capital structure for the best risk/reward. Institutional Investor described him as an eclectic investor who may own equities but searches across bonds, preferreds, bank debt, and stock for the best value, sometimes using capital-structure arbitrage ([Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgea9afk8qds2jxjlds/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-management-among-first-quarter-hedge-fund-leaders)). In 2009 this meant common shares, preferreds, junior subordinated debt, and AIG debt, not just one headline bank stock ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

The model is useful because distress rarely misprices every layer equally. Sometimes common equity has the convexity, sometimes preferred stock has the best conversion math, sometimes bank debt or distressed bonds offer survival upside without common-stock dilution risk. The required question is: which claim is being mispriced relative to the legal and policy path?

### 3. Distinguish drawdown from impairment

Tepper's career repeatedly separates mark-to-market pain from thesis impairment. Appaloosa lost heavily in Russia in 1998 before later recovering; Appaloosa Investment I reportedly fell 25% in 2002 and rose 149% in 2003; the flagship fell 26.7% in 2008 and then produced a triple-digit 2009 rebound depending on vehicle and period ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/); [Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here); [Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). Institutional Investor later summarized this as a pattern of severe interim losses followed by large gains when the thesis paid off ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually)).

The model is not "never sell." It is: before treating price pain as a sell signal, ask whether the core facts have deteriorated, the legal path has worsened, financing has changed, client capital is unstable, or the catalyst has disappeared. If price has fallen while the thesis has improved, the drawdown may be the opportunity. If price has fallen because the legal or policy premise has broken, the drawdown is information.

### 4. Capacity is an edge

Appaloosa's capital-return discipline is part of the investing model. Distressed and event-driven trades decay when too much capital chases them, and they become dangerous when client withdrawals force sales at bad marks. Institutional Investor reported that Appaloosa repeatedly returned capital, had a long-standing policy of keeping size aligned with returns rather than management fees, and by 2019 intended eventually to become a family office ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswhgrmp6rwew6061728/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-sees-steep-decline-in-assets); [Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually)).

For the model, capacity discipline does two things. It lets Appaloosa concentrate when price and evidence align, and it reduces the risk that a correct but early trade is liquidated by fund structure. This is one reason public 13F cloning is misleading: the visible long-equity book does not show cash, credit, shorts, derivatives, cost basis, or liquidity reserves.

### 5. Legal process is part of underwriting

Tepper's model works in places where law, regulation, and restructuring mechanics determine outcomes. That means legal process is not background noise. The Wells Fargo Rule 105 settlement shows capital-markets compliance as a hard operating constraint: the SEC found that Appaloosa violated Rule 105 around a public offering and required disgorgement, penalty, and compliance undertakings including written policies, training, trade review, senior oversight, and certification ([SEC, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)).

WaMu shows a different legal risk: information status inside bankruptcy negotiations. The Delaware Bankruptcy Court's 2011 opinion identified Appaloosa among the settlement noteholders in the bankruptcy background and addressed colorable equitable-disallowance and insider-trading allegations against noteholders, though later plan materials vacated the standing-motion portions of that opinion and the opened record does not show final liability against Tepper personally ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf); [Washington Mutual SEC filing, 2012](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/933136/000090951812000087/jg02-2712_8ke23.htm)). The practical rule is: when a trade's edge comes from being near a restructuring, information controls are part of the position.

### 6. Do not fight the liquidity regime

Tepper's public comments are often simplified into "don't fight the Fed," but the real model is regime-sensitive. In 2022, coordinated central-bank tightening made him bearish; in 2025, rate cuts made him reluctant to exit equities even while he disliked multiples; in 2024, Fed easing helped underpin the China-stimulus thesis ([Markets Insider, 2022](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-short-stock-market-dont-fight-fed-interest-rates-2022-12); [MarketWatch, 2025](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/billionaire-david-tepper-is-feeling-miserable-about-stocks-but-wont-fight-the-fed-74d96d45); [Markets Insider, 2024](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-buys-chinese-stocks-country-launches-fiscal-stimulus-bazooka-2024-9)).

This is not a permanent bullish rule. It is a discipline for aligning exposure with the marginal direction of liquidity, while still asking whether valuation, exit liquidity, and inflation/currency risk make the support fragile.

## Reconstructed Decision Checklist

### Screens: where Tepper looks first

1. **Forced-selling zones.** Bankruptcies, distressed debt, crisis financials, and sectors where mandates or fear push holders out. The 2002-2003 distressed-credit rebound and 2009 bank trade are the clearest examples ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here); [Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

2. **Policy inflection zones.** Central-bank easing, rescue programs, fiscal stimulus, regulatory changes, or official-sector backstops where the market has not yet repriced the new regime. China 2024 and the 2010 QE framework belong here ([Markets Insider, 2024](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/david-tepper-buys-chinese-stocks-country-launches-fiscal-stimulus-bazooka-2024-9); [CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010)).

3. **Capital-allocation or governance breaks.** Whirlpool is the current example: Appaloosa criticized equity issuance, strategy, and management incentives, then filed a Schedule 13D showing a material stake and a $265.1 million reported cost for shares beneficially owned as of the filing date ([Appaloosa letter, 2026](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/appaloosa-sends-letter-to-whirlpool-board-of-directors-302697107.html); [SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml)).

4. **Visible but incomplete public long books.** Current 13F filings show where Appaloosa has U.S.-listed long exposure, but the screen is only a clue. The Q1 2026 13F lists 31 reportable holdings and shows Amazon, Alibaba, Alphabet, and other long positions, but it omits shorts, credit, derivatives, cash, non-U.S. securities, and intraperiod trading ([SEC 13F cover, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/primary_doc.xml); [SEC 13F table, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)).

### Underwriting sequence

1. **Define the disaster priced in.** Is the market pricing nationalization, default, liquidity freeze, dilution, permanent earnings impairment, or regulatory wipeout?

2. **Find the mechanism that changes odds.** For 2009, it was Treasury's bank-support architecture and conversion math; for China 2024, it was coordinated monetary/fiscal language plus buyback support; for Whirlpool, it is pressure on governance and capital allocation.

3. **Choose the best instrument.** Compare common, preferred, debt, bank loans, ETFs, options, or activist equity by downside, convexity, legal priority, and liquidity. Do not assume common stock is the right expression.

4. **Stress legal and regulatory path.** Ask whether bankruptcy information, offering rules, bank-resolution powers, or sovereign discretion can override the spreadsheet. Russia 1998 and Credit Suisse AT1s are the warnings; Bloomberg Law reported in March 2026 that Appaloosa could proceed with securities-fraud claims over Credit Suisse AT1 bonds, while racketeering claims were insufficiently pleaded ([Bloomberg Law, 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/ubs-stuck-with-securities-claims-over-credit-suisse-bond-wipeout); [Swissinfo/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/appaloosa-sues-credit-suisse-over-%2417-billion-at1-bond-wipeout/76316361)).

5. **Decide if pain is survivable.** A trade that can fall 25% before working requires patient capital, liquid reserves, and no forced-selling mechanism.

### Sizing rules

Tepper sizes large only when evidence, price, and catalyst line up. The 2009 financials exposure reportedly peaked around 30% of assets across securities, while Institutional Investor described a typical willingness to allocate 30%-40% of assets to equities when the macro and capital-structure view warrant it ([Bloomberg Markets, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf); [Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgea9afk8qds2jxjlds/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-management-among-first-quarter-hedge-fund-leaders)). The implied sizing rule is not maximum concentration. It is graduated aggression:

- starter position when the asset is cheap but the catalyst is uncertain;
- larger position when policy/legal documents improve the scenario tree;
- largest position only when the market is still pricing a disaster after the evidence changes;
- reduce or avoid scale when exit liquidity, legal status, or client capital can force a bad sale.

### Sell rules

Tepper's public record suggests four sell rules:

1. **Sell or trim when the panic discount closes.** The trade was made because price implied disaster; once price implies normalization, expected value falls.
2. **Sell when policy support is fully priced or starts creating a new risk.** Tepper's 2025 comments show that easing can support markets while also creating danger if it goes too far ([MarketWatch, 2025](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/billionaire-david-tepper-is-feeling-miserable-about-stocks-but-wont-fight-the-fed-74d96d45)).
3. **Sell when the legal premise changes.** Sovereign default, bank-resolution powers, or information restrictions can turn a cheap claim into a legally impaired one.
4. **Sell or shrink when capacity dulls edge.** Capital returns and the family-office transition are portfolio-level sell rules: if there are not enough exceptional opportunities, return or idle capital rather than manufacture trades ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswhgrmp6rwew6061728/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-sees-steep-decline-in-assets)).

### Risk limits

The reconstructed risk limits are:

- avoid structural leverage that can force liquidation before a thesis matures; public comments and secondary accounts suggest Appaloosa emphasized position risk more than mechanical leverage, though this is not audited leverage disclosure ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010));
- preserve cash and liquidity when visibility is low, even if that causes underexposure during rebounds ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswhgrmp6rwew6061728/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-sees-steep-decline-in-assets));
- separate trading from material nonpublic information in restructurings;
- treat public-offering and restricted-period rules as hard constraints, not back-office details ([SEC, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf));
- assume sovereigns and regulators can behave discontinuously in crises.

## Failure Modes of the Model

### Policy-backstop overconfidence

The model works when policy support is credible, explicit, and enforceable enough to change outcomes. It fails when the investor assumes officials will act rationally but cannot force them to do so. Russia 1998 is the central caution: Tepper expected Russia might devalue but not default after IMF support; the country did both, and the trade became his clearest self-identified mistake in accessible accounts ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

### Legal hierarchy illusion

Credit investors can mistake contractual priority for realized recovery. AT1 bonds, bank rescues, and bankruptcy negotiations show that emergency authority and court process can rewrite economics before investors can exit. Tepper's model therefore needs a second legal screen: who can change the rules, under what statute, in what venue, and on what timetable?

### Being early with impatient capital

Tepper's record rewards being early only because Appaloosa could survive. The 2002 and 2008 drawdowns turned into great trades, but a similar path with hot client capital, margin debt, or concentrated personal leverage could end in forced liquidation. The model is fragile when copied by investors who cannot hold through the valley.

### 13F-copycat error

Public investors can see Appaloosa's U.S.-listed long holdings but not the full book. The Q1 2026 13F is useful evidence of public longs, but it does not show credit claims, hedges, shorts, cash, derivatives, non-U.S. securities, cost basis, or Tepper's exit plan ([SEC 13F cover, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/primary_doc.xml)). Copying a 13F turns a multi-instrument process into delayed stock picking.

### Temperament drift into combativeness

Tepper's bluntness is part of the edge in panic: it helps him ignore consensus fear. It is also a governance risk. The Whirlpool letter is analytically focused on dilution and capital allocation, but its language is openly confrontational and signed by Tepper ([Appaloosa letter, 2026](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/appaloosa-sends-letter-to-whirlpool-board-of-directors-302697107.html)). Activism can unlock value, but public conflict can also harden counterparties and narrow exit paths.

### Late-career opportunity-cost risk

Capital discipline can become underexposure. Institutional Investor reported that Appaloosa ran large cash balances and was underexposed to equities around 2018-2019, even as markets rebounded ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswhgrmp6rwew6061728/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-sees-steep-decline-in-assets)). That may be rational for personal capital, but it is still a cost of protecting optionality.

## Transferability: What Individuals Can and Cannot Replicate

### Replicable

Individual investors can copy the sequence of thinking:

1. **Start with the downside.** Define exactly what disaster the price implies.
2. **Demand a catalyst.** Cheap is not enough; look for policy, restructuring, governance, or liquidity change.
3. **Think across claims.** Even if individuals cannot buy bank loans or distressed bonds easily, they can ask whether common equity is actually the best expression.
4. **Respect liquidity regimes.** A Fed or central-bank shift changes the opportunity set, but it does not eliminate valuation and exit risk.
5. **Separate price pain from thesis impairment.** Create a checklist before the drawdown: what facts would make the thesis wrong?
6. **Avoid copying delayed disclosures.** Use 13Fs as source material, not instructions.
7. **Build legal humility.** If a trade depends on bankruptcy, bank resolution, sovereign action, or public offerings, know the rulebook or pass.

### Hard to replicate

Most of Tepper's true edge is institutional. Individuals generally cannot replicate Appaloosa's access to distressed-credit markets, trading lines, restructuring counsel, capital-structure instruments, analyst network, counterparty relationships, and ability to buy across common, preferred, bonds, bank debt, and derivatives. They also cannot see Appaloosa's full book or financing terms.

The capital base is also hard to replicate. Tepper can run personal and family-office-style capital with fewer redemption pressures. An individual with salary risk, concentrated net worth, or limited liquidity should not interpret his drawdown tolerance as permission to average down indefinitely.

Finally, the legal and compliance apparatus is non-transferable. Appaloosa's Rule 105 settlement and WaMu process risk show that the strategy lives near legal boundaries. Individuals should not enter trades whose edge depends on privileged restructuring information, offering mechanics, or emergency resolution powers they cannot evaluate.

### Practical distilled model

The safest individual adaptation is:

- avoid leverage;
- use public equities or ETFs only when the thesis does not require hidden capital-structure knowledge;
- size small until the catalyst is visible;
- predefine a legal/policy failure point;
- treat a panic discount as a temporary spread, not a forever holding;
- exit when normalization is priced.

The conclusion is not "be as aggressive as Tepper." It is: when forced selling, policy change, and price line up, the market can briefly offer unusually asymmetric trades. Tepper's skill is knowing when that setup is real, where in the capital structure it is most mispriced, and whether the fund structure can survive the path.
