# David Tepper - Mistakes and Losses

As of 2026-06-25T14:25:50Z, David Tepper is a living, active investor whose public footprint is now best observed through Appaloosa's SEC filings, selected activist situations, and litigation tied to distressed financial instruments rather than through a continuously marketed outside-capital hedge fund. This file covers Task D: the major losses, errors of omission, legal/process near-misses, behavioral roots, and process changes visible from public records. Because Appaloosa is private and does not publish an audited investor-letter archive, fund-level return figures below rely on strong secondary reporting unless otherwise noted.

## Major losses, errors of omission, and near-death moments

### 1. Russia 1998: policy support did not equal enforceable protection

The cleanest self-identified mistake in the record is Russia. New York Magazine reported that in 1998 Tepper bought Russian debt on the assumption that the Russian government would not default; when Russia defaulted and the ruble collapsed, the episode cost Appaloosa hundreds of millions of dollars. Tepper kept buying into the collapse and the fund later rose about 60 percent, but the initial premise was wrong: official-sector incentives and international support did not prevent default ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

An unofficial transcript carrier of Tepper's 2010 CNBC appearance also captures him discussing Russia as the worst trade in the context of past drawdowns and recovery. Because the accessible transcript is a repost rather than CNBC's official transcript, exact wording should remain caveated, but the substance lines up with the New York Magazine account and with the later Appaloosa pattern of using big mark-to-market losses as a setup for rebounds rather than as automatic stop-loss events ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010)).

The mistake was not merely that Russia defaulted. It was that a distressed sovereign trade was analyzed too much like a mispriced credit and not enough like a political option with discontinuous downside. The lesson later matters because Tepper's 2009 bank trade also depended on government behavior. The difference was that, in 2009, he had a specific Treasury term sheet and conversion math to underwrite; in Russia, he was underwriting a sovereign promise and macro rationality.

### 2. 2002 credit collapse: being early can be indistinguishable from being wrong

Institutional Investor reported that Appaloosa Investment I rose 67 percent in 2001, fell 25 percent in 2002, and then rebounded 149 percent in 2003, net of fees. The 2003 winners included bonds of Conseco and debt of Marconi, both classic stressed-credit situations ([Institutional Investor, 2004](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgjr6f5taemnllr6br4/home/the-bucks-stop-here)). New York Magazine describes the same cycle in broader terms: junk bonds collapsed, Tepper lost 25 percent, and the following year bonds purchased in bankrupt companies surged ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)).

This is not a simple investment mistake, because the end result was a spectacular recovery. It is a path-risk warning. A 25 percent drawdown in an illiquid credit strategy can become fatal if investors redeem, financing disappears, or the manager is forced to sell. Tepper's edge was the ability to remain in the game long enough for the post-distress recovery. That does not erase the error mode: he was willing to be very early in cyclical distress, and early distressed buying looks irrational until liquidity and confidence return.

The near-death lesson is that high expected value does not eliminate financing risk. The same trade can be brilliant for permanent or patient capital and disastrous for capital that can leave at the bottom. Appaloosa's later habit of returning capital and resisting asset bloat belongs partly in this category: it reduced the chance that a correct but early trade would be killed by fund structure.

### 3. 2008 financial crisis drawdown before the 2009 bank windfall

The celebrated 2009 bank trade was preceded by a real loss. Bloomberg Markets reported that Appaloosa Investment LP I returned 117.3 percent for the nine months ended September 30, 2009, after a 26.7 percent drop in 2008; the firm gained $6.5 billion through September 30, 2009, across funds and accounts ([Bloomberg Markets via Opalesque PDF, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)).

The mistake/near-miss was not buying bank securities after the Treasury's Capital Assistance Program term sheet. That was the great trade. The risk was entering the crisis arena while the market could still plausibly price nationalization, dilution, and bank failures. A 26.7 percent annual drawdown in the flagship before the 2009 rebound means the trade family had a meaningful mark-to-market and survival component.

The public record suggests Tepper's process improved inside the crisis. Bloomberg's account emphasizes that the February 25, 2009 Treasury white paper and term sheet changed the underwriting. Tepper interpreted the conversion prices and policy language as evidence that the government did not intend to wipe out common equity holders in the major banks ([Bloomberg Markets via Opalesque PDF, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). In other words, the 2008 loss became manageable because the later sizing was tied to a concrete policy document, not just to the idea that the government would eventually do the sensible thing.

### 4. Wells Fargo Rule 105: a small-dollar but high-signal compliance failure

The SEC's July 2, 2010 administrative order is the most important official legal source in Tepper's mistake file. The Commission found that Appaloosa Management sold short 1,034,896 shares of Wells Fargo during the Rule 105 restricted period before Wells Fargo's November 2008 follow-on offering, then bought 125,000 shares in the offering at $27.00 per share. The SEC found disgorgeable profits of $842,500 and ordered Appaloosa to pay disgorgement, prejudgment interest of $40,773.34, and a civil penalty of $421,250. Appaloosa settled without admitting or denying the findings, except as to jurisdiction ([SEC Release No. 62447, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)).

This was not a portfolio-threatening loss. It was a process failure with reputational value. The order is especially useful because it names the corrective controls: Appaloosa undertook to adopt and maintain Rule 105 compliance policies, train relevant employees, require review of public-offering trades, designate a senior employee for oversight, and certify the new policies to the SEC ([SEC Release No. 62447, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)).

New York Magazine later reported Tepper's investor-letter position that Appaloosa had not knowingly manipulated the stock price, while noting the roughly $1.3 million payment ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). The critical point for the Canon is not intent; the official lesson is that fast-moving capital-markets trades require mechanical restricted-list and offering-participation controls. A brilliant crisis-trade shop can still fail at the plumbing.

### 5. Washington Mutual: information-wall risk in distressed negotiations

The Washington Mutual bankruptcy fight was not a final liability finding against Tepper or Appaloosa, but it is a major process-risk case for distressed investors. In September 2011, the Delaware Bankruptcy Court found that the equity committee had stated colorable claims that settlement noteholders received material nonpublic information, could be temporary insiders, and engaged in insider trading under classical and misappropriation theories. The court granted but stayed the equity committee's standing motion pending mediation ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court opinion, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf)).

The opinion's facts are nuanced. It notes, for example, that Appaloosa and Centerbridge received information in connection with separate negotiations and then engaged in opposite trades; elsewhere it notes that Appaloosa voluntarily restricted trading after receiving certain information while Centerbridge continued to trade ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court opinion, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf)). Business Insider reported Appaloosa's contemporaneous denial, including its statement that the allegations were demonstrably false and defamatory ([Business Insider, 2011](https://www.businessinsider.com/david-tepper-insider-trading-wamu-2011-2)).

Jones Day's legal analysis is the cleanest practical takeaway: distressed investors that trade claims in bankruptcy should treat participation in settlement negotiations as a potential source of material nonpublic information and should consider trading restrictions or ethical walls ([Jones Day, 2011](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2011/12/wamu-confirmation-denied-interest-rates-equitable-disallowance-and-insider-trading)). The final 2012 confirmation materials show the case moved forward under a Seventh Amended Plan after the prior confirmation disputes and mediation process ([Washington Mutual SEC 8-K exhibit, 2012](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/933136/000090951812000087/jg02-2712_8ke23.htm)).

The WaMu lesson is therefore not "Tepper was found liable." He was not, on the opened record. The lesson is that Appaloosa's edge in distressed credit required being close to negotiations, and closeness itself created legal and process hazards.

### 6. Credit Suisse AT1 bonds: loss recovery is not the same as loss prevention

A current legal-development caveat belongs in the mistake file because it involves Appaloosa-managed vehicles pursuing recovery on a distressed-financials loss. In April 2024, Swissinfo, republishing Bloomberg, reported that Appaloosa LP, Palomino Master Ltd., and Azteca Partners LLC sued over Credit Suisse Additional Tier 1 bonds that were written to zero in the UBS rescue. The report says the plaintiffs alleged significant losses after buying AT1 bonds shortly before the wipeout and claimed Credit Suisse misled investors about its health ([Swissinfo/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/appaloosa-sues-credit-suisse-over-%2417-billion-at1-bond-wipeout/76316361)).

As of March 30, 2026, Bloomberg Law reported that Appaloosa and two managed/advised funds could proceed with securities-fraud claims over the AT1 bonds, while the court said the firms had not sufficiently pleaded New Jersey racketeering claims. This is not a ruling that fraud occurred or that Appaloosa will recover; it is a live litigation status update showing that the securities-law claim survived a threshold challenge ([Bloomberg Law, 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/ubs-stuck-with-securities-claims-over-credit-suisse-bond-wipeout)).

For Tepper's process record, the AT1 episode is a modern cousin of Russia and 2009 financials: a bank-capital trade can depend on emergency-state action, regulator discretion, instrument hierarchy, and post-crisis litigation. The possible mistake is not that Appaloosa is litigating; suing may be rational recovery work. The risk is that a security whose contractual hierarchy appears analyzable can still be rewritten by emergency public authority before a distressed investor can exit.

### 7. 2018-2019 underexposure, shrinkage, and the cost of risk control

Institutional Investor reported that Appaloosa's assets fell to $11.6 billion at year-end 2018, down from $14.8 billion at the end of August and $16.5 billion about a year earlier. The same account says performance did not drive the whole decline, but Appaloosa was slightly in the red in 2018, ran large cash balances, and was underexposed to the stock market as equities rebounded in early 2019 ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswhgrmp6rwew6061728/portfolio/david-teppers-appaloosa-sees-steep-decline-in-assets)).

This is a different kind of mistake: omission and opportunity cost. Tepper built a career on buying panic, yet by 2018-2019 Appaloosa was smaller, more conservative, and increasingly shaped by Tepper's personal wealth, taxes, sports-team ownership, and eventual family-office transition. Reuters reported in May 2019 that Tepper planned to return outside capital and turn Appaloosa into a family office; the same report cited Appaloosa's more-than-quarter-century average annual gain of about 25 percent and $13 billion of capital ([Reuters via Investing.com, 2019](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/hedge-fund-manager-david-tepper-plans-to-send-investors-money-back-1877205)).

This should not be overread. Returning capital is a strength if the opportunity set cannot absorb size. But it creates a tension: a manager who protects the franchise by shrinking may also miss broad beta rebounds. In the Canon taxonomy, this is a high-quality investor's late-career tradeoff between protecting permanent personal capital and maximizing outside-capital compounding.

### 8. Open live risks: China and Whirlpool are not yet mistakes

Two current Appaloosa exposures should be monitored but not scored as mistakes yet. Appaloosa publicly embraced Chinese equities after the 2024 stimulus turn, and 13F filings later showed meaningful listed exposure to Chinese ADRs and ETFs. But 13F filings show U.S.-listed long holdings at quarter-end, not total exposure, cost basis, hedges, or realized P&L. The Q1 2026 Appaloosa information table is useful for current portfolio visibility but cannot prove the trade's economics ([SEC Q1 2026 Form 13F information table](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1656456/000165645626000002/xslForm13F_X02/Form13FInfoTable.xml)).

Whirlpool is also live. In February 2026 Appaloosa sent a public letter criticizing Whirlpool's capital allocation and board oversight; in March 2026 it filed a Schedule 13D. The 13D is useful for current reporting-person and beneficial-ownership context, while the letter shows Appaloosa's governance critique. Neither source tells us whether the campaign will become a win, a loss, or a distraction ([Appaloosa Whirlpool letter, 2026](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/appaloosa-sends-letter-to-whirlpool-board-of-directors-302697107.html); [SEC Schedule 13D, 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/106640/000149315226008749/xslSCHEDULE_13D_X01/primary_doc.xml)).

## What Tepper and Appaloosa said about the mistakes

Tepper's public style is unusually blunt, but the available record still has gaps. For Russia, the accessible sources indicate he framed it as a major or worst trade, while also emphasizing the later recovery after buying more distressed bonds ([CNBC transcript carrier, 2010](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-101-david-tepper-2010); [New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). The framing is important: he did not present every large drawdown as a process failure. He separated being wrong on the first leg from having the capital and conviction to exploit the second leg.

For the 2008-2009 financials episode, Tepper's self-account was that the trade became clear once the government documents made nationalization less likely and showed conversion values above market prices. Bloomberg captured him treating the Treasury term sheet as the key evidence, not as a vague macro hunch ([Bloomberg Markets via Opalesque PDF, 2010](https://www.opalesque.com/files/BloombergRichestHedgeFunds.pdf)). That account should be read alongside the 26.7 percent 2008 drawdown: Tepper's story is not "never lose money." It is "survive the panic, then size when the evidence becomes asymmetric."

For Wells Fargo Rule 105, Appaloosa's legal posture was settlement without admitting or denying the SEC findings, while agreeing to cease-and-desist, censure, monetary payments, and compliance undertakings ([SEC Release No. 62447, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)). New York Magazine reported Tepper denying knowing manipulation in a letter to investors ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). The practical message is that Appaloosa disputed intent but accepted the remedial framework.

For WaMu, Appaloosa denied the allegations in strong terms, and the opened court record did not produce a final insider-trading liability finding against Tepper or Appaloosa. But the court and legal commentary made the process risk explicit: negotiation access plus trading can become a dangerous combination even for sophisticated distressed investors ([Business Insider, 2011](https://www.businessinsider.com/david-tepper-insider-trading-wamu-2011-2); [Jones Day, 2011](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2011/12/wamu-confirmation-denied-interest-rates-equitable-disallowance-and-insider-trading)).

For Credit Suisse AT1s, the public posture is litigation rather than a postmortem. Swissinfo/Bloomberg reported the plaintiffs alleged Credit Suisse misled them about its health when they bought the notes, and Bloomberg Law later reported that securities-fraud claims were allowed to proceed while racketeering claims were not sufficiently pleaded ([Swissinfo/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/appaloosa-sues-credit-suisse-over-%2417-billion-at1-bond-wipeout/76316361); [Bloomberg Law, 2026](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/litigation/ubs-stuck-with-securities-claims-over-credit-suisse-bond-wipeout)). Until the case is resolved, it should be treated as a disputed loss-recovery effort, not as an adjudicated mistake.

## Behavioral root causes

### Policy-backstop overconfidence

The Russia and 2009 bank episodes are mirror images. In both, Tepper asked whether official actors would rationally avoid a catastrophic outcome. Russia showed the danger of assuming the answer is yes. The 2009 bank trade worked because policy support became observable in documents and conversion terms. The root behavior is not naivete; it is a credit trader's habit of translating institutional incentives into price. That habit is powerful when the rules are explicit and hazardous when the rules are political, fluid, or unenforceable.

### Comfort with severe interim pain

Appaloosa's record includes repeated rebounds after drawdowns: Russia, 2002-2003 distressed credit, and 2008-2009 financials. The behavioral strength is obvious: Tepper does not seem to treat price pain alone as invalidating evidence. The failure mode is equally obvious: a manager who can tolerate pain may underweight the chance that liquidity, clients, or legal constraints force action before the thesis matures.

### Boundary-pushing temperament

New York Magazine's profile repeatedly presents Tepper as profane, combative, irreverent, and willing to say what others will not ([New York Magazine, 2010](https://nymag.com/news/features/establishments/68513/)). That temperament fits distressed investing, where the money is often made by entering ugly situations. It also fits the risk profile of legal and process near-misses: capital-markets rules, information barriers, and bankruptcy negotiations reward discipline more than bravado.

### Legal hierarchy and regulator risk

Tepper's best trades often come from reading capital structures under stress. The error mode is that legal priority is not always the same as realized recovery. Russia shows sovereign-payment risk, WaMu shows negotiation-information risk, and Credit Suisse AT1s show that bank-resolution powers can override what a distressed investor believes the loss waterfall should produce. The practical behavioral risk is treating legal architecture as more fixed than it is during crisis.

### Scale and life-cycle drift

The 2018-2019 period highlights a less dramatic root cause: success itself changes the game. Tepper's personal wealth, Carolina Panthers ownership, returned capital, and family-office transition changed the opportunity set and incentives. A manager running mostly personal capital can rationally avoid marginal risks that outside investors would prefer him to take. The error of omission is not laziness; it is incentive drift after extraordinary success.

### Public-copycat opacity

A final root issue is evidentiary. The public sees 13F snapshots and occasional TV comments, but not Appaloosa's shorts, derivatives, credit positions, basis, or risk limits. That opacity can create a false sense of lesson transfer. Copying a Tepper headline idea without his entry price, sizing, and exit flexibility is itself a mistake.

## Process changes made after

### More explicit catalyst underwriting

The clearest process improvement after Russia is the difference between a hoped-for policy backstop and a documented one. In 2009, the Treasury's Capital Assistance Program materials and conversion math were the catalyst. The transferable process rule is: when a trade depends on government behavior, underwrite the actual legal/economic mechanism, not just the policymaker's incentive to avoid disaster.

### Capacity discipline and capital returns

Appaloosa repeatedly returned capital and eventually moved toward a family-office model. Institutional Investor reported that the firm had returned capital in eight of the prior nine years as of 2019, and Reuters reported the plan to return outside capital and invest Tepper's personal fortune ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswguw6e4as640zbx8g0/corner-office/david-tepper-will-convert-appaloosa-to-a-family-office-eventually); [Reuters via Investing.com, 2019](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/hedge-fund-manager-david-tepper-plans-to-send-investors-money-back-1877205)). This is a process response to drawdown and liquidity risk: keep assets small enough that bargains can matter and investors do not force sales at the worst time.

### Compliance controls after Wells Fargo

The SEC order lists the control changes Appaloosa undertook after the Wells Fargo Rule 105 violation: written policies, annual review, training, public-offering trade identification and review, senior oversight, and certification to the Commission ([SEC Release No. 62447, 2010](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2010/34-62447.pdf)). That is a concrete process change, not an inferred one.

### Trading restrictions and ethical walls in distressed negotiations

The WaMu opinion and Jones Day analysis make the distressed-investor lesson explicit: a creditor receiving material nonpublic information in negotiations should restrict trading or establish an ethical wall. The court itself stated that creditors at the negotiating table can avoid the problem by restricting trading or building an ethical wall ([Delaware Bankruptcy Court opinion, 2011](https://www.deb.uscourts.gov/sites/deb/files/opinions/mwf2011091308-12229_0.pdf); [Jones Day, 2011](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2011/12/wamu-confirmation-denied-interest-rates-equitable-disallowance-and-insider-trading)). For Appaloosa, the practical lesson is that distressed edge must be separated from trading discretion when information status changes.

### Contract-plus-resolution review for bank-capital instruments

The Credit Suisse AT1 case adds a modern checklist item: bank-capital instruments need both contract review and emergency-resolution review. A legal priority claim is not enough if the regulator has crisis powers that can rewrite economics before courts can respond. In practice, the process needs scenario analysis for write-down triggers, governing law, forum, sovereign-immunity defenses, and expected recovery timing, not just spread-to-par math.

### Distinguishing drawdown from impairment

Tepper's best process habit may be refusing to equate mark-to-market loss with permanent impairment. But the mature version needs a checklist: Is the thesis still true? Is the catalyst closer? Is liquidity sufficient? Are clients stable? Has any legal or information-boundary constraint changed? The Russia and 2002 cases show the upside of staying power; Wells Fargo, WaMu, and Credit Suisse AT1s show that not all risks are market risks.
