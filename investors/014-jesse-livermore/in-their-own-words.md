# Jesse Livermore - In Their Own Words

As of 2026-06-23T12:27:37Z, the cleanest direct Livermore voice located for this task is his 1940 book, *How to Trade in Stocks: The Livermore Formula for Combining Time Element and Price*. That source was published by Duell, Sloan & Pearce shortly before his death and presents itself as Livermore's own late-career explanation of his trading rules, record-keeping method, pivotal-point system, and mistakes ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

The famous 1923 *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* is different evidence. Project Gutenberg identifies Edwin Lefevre as author, and the text speaks through Larry Livingston, a Livermore-inspired fictionalized operator rather than through Jesse Livermore's signed memoir voice ([Project Gutenberg metadata](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/60979); [Project Gutenberg full text](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/60979/60979-h/60979-h.htm)). This file therefore treats *Reminiscences* as a literary/psychological source and attribution trap: useful for what later traders think "Livermore" sounds like, but not enough by itself to quote Livermore directly.

Copyright note for future agents: the quote bank below uses short source-visible phrase handles and paraphrases rather than long extracts from the 1940 book. The purpose is to preserve exact attribution, source year, and location cues without over-quoting a still-copyrighted trading text.

## Quote Bank: Direct Livermore Phrase Handles

These entries are intentionally compact. Each gives a short phrase visible in Livermore's 1940 text, then a paraphrased takeaway. Use the cited source before expanding any quote in a future task.

### Speculation As A Business

1. "game of speculation" - Livermore opens by treating speculation as absorbing but dangerous work, not entertainment ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
2. "I don't know" - His answer to people seeking easy market money is blunt humility, not a tip ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
3. "time element" - He frames his edge as the combination of price with waiting for the right time ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
4. "own records" - He insists that durable speculation requires personal market records, not borrowed conviction ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
5. "own thinking" - The same opening chapter rejects delegation of judgment; the trader must reason independently ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
6. "business" - Livermore says speculation should be approached as a learned business, not a gamble ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

### Market Confirmation And Opinion

7. "nothing new" - His late-life rule begins from recurring patterns in securities and commodities markets ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
8. "should not speculate" - He explicitly rejects constant market participation; inactivity is part of the method ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
9. "anticipating coming movements" - Speculation is prediction, but only after a definite evidentiary basis is formed ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
10. "psychological effect" - News matters because of its market impact on participants, not because it sounds bullish or bearish ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
11. "don't trust" - He warns against acting on opinion before market action confirms it ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
12. "Markets are never wrong" - The market's action outranks the speculator's opinion when the two conflict ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

### Waiting, Timing, And Pivotal Points

13. "have patience" - Livermore tells the trader to wait until the stock proves itself by action ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
14. "psychological time" - The desired entry is the moment when the market's force is already strong enough to carry through ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
15. "human weaknesses" - He links impatience and over-participation to ordinary human frailty, not lack of intelligence ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
16. "rules to guide" - Successful speculation is not mere guessing; it requires rules that control action ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
17. "stale" - Patience is not passivity; stale action after a profit can itself become a warning ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
18. "careless" - After success, the danger shifts to overconfidence and excessive ambition ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
19. "do not argue" - He wants the trader aligned with the condition in front of him, not debating it ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
20. "Pivotal Points" - His core technical vocabulary is the price area where action confirms a new move ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
21. "danger signal" - A failed move after a pivotal point is not noise; it is evidence to respect ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
22. "careful timing" - The cotton chapter turns a missed profit into a lesson on impatience and premature entry ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

### Loss Control And Capital Discipline

23. "Never average losses" - His most direct risk rule forbids adding to an initial losing trade ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
24. "losses never do" - Profits can compound, but losses require active control before they threaten the account ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
25. "guard his capital" - Livermore applies capital protection to investors and speculators alike ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
26. "safe deposit" - After a successful deal, he recommends physically removing part of the profit from Wall Street exposure ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
27. "paper profits" - His Palm Beach anecdote distinguishes mark-to-market gains from cash withdrawn from the brokerage account ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

### Leaders, Records, And Scope Control

28. "follow the leader" - He wants the trader focused on leading stocks/groups instead of a scattered market list ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
29. "mentally flexible" - Leadership changes; the trader must update the list of market leaders rather than preserve old favorites ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
30. "records began" - Livermore presents record-keeping as the mechanism that made patterns visible to him ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
31. "Key Price" - His market key combines two stocks in a group to reduce false signals from a single issue ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).
32. "big opportunities" - The commodity examples show him scanning across markets but waiting for rare pivotal opportunities ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

## Attribution Watchlist: Famous Lines Not Treated As Direct Livermore

The following sayings are important because traders often quote them as "Livermore." In this file they remain separated from Livermore's signed 1940 voice unless a future agent locates stronger direct evidence.

- The "sitting tight" maxim belongs, in the opened source, to Lefevre's Larry Livingston narrative in *Reminiscences*, not to a signed Livermore memoir ([Project Gutenberg full text, 1923](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/60979/60979-h/60979-h.htm)).
- The "be right and sit tight" idea is also in the Larry Livingston/Old Partridge episode; use it as a literary expression of patience, not a direct Livermore quotation ([Project Gutenberg full text, 1923](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/60979/60979-h/60979-h.htm)).
- The line that the market does not beat people, but they beat themselves, appears in the same literary passage and should not be promoted to direct Livermore without independent corroboration ([Project Gutenberg full text, 1923](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/60979/60979-h/60979-h.htm)).
- Bucket-shop origin stories in *Reminiscences* match the Livermore legend broadly, but the narrative voice remains Larry Livingston; cross-check any factual detail against TIME, court, newspaper, or archival sources before using it as biography ([Project Gutenberg full text, 1923](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/60979/60979-h/60979-h.htm); [Investopedia bucket-shop background](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bucketshop.asp)).
- TIME's 1934 quotation from Livermore's lawyers about repeated fortunes is a lawyer statement, not Livermore speaking; it is useful for public reputation and bankruptcy context only ([TIME, "Fourth Down," 1934](https://content.time.com/time/subscriber/article/0,33009,747199-2,00.html)).

## Annotated Primary And Near-Primary Materials Index

### *How to Trade in Stocks* (1940)

Status: direct, signed Livermore source; highest-value source for this task. It supplies his late-career explanation of speculation as a business, the time element, pivotal points, the market key, leader-following, cash withdrawal after profits, and loss discipline ([How to Trade in Stocks, 1940](https://www.buysidedigest.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/How-to-Trade-in-Stocks-Jesse-Livermore.pdf)).

Takeaway: use this as the primary quotation source, but keep context attached. It is a 1940 retrospective by a trader whose own record included repeated bankruptcies, so the source is both a rulebook and an implicit self-indictment: he could state the rules more consistently than he lived them.

### *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* (1923)

Status: literary near-primary source, not a direct Livermore-authored memoir. Project Gutenberg's record lists Edwin Lefevre as author and provides the public-domain text ([Project Gutenberg metadata](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/60979); [Project Gutenberg full text](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/60979/60979-h/60979-h.htm)).

Takeaway: useful for tape-reading psychology, bucket-shop lore, patience, and the later trader canon's mental image of Livermore. Do not quote Larry Livingston as Jesse Livermore unless another primary source independently confirms the line.

### TIME, "Business & Finance: Cotton" (1929)

Status: contemporary magazine profile during Livermore's commodity-speculator fame ([TIME, "Cotton," 1929](https://time.com/archive/6743139/business-finance-cotton-2/)).

Takeaway: useful as near-primary public-reputation evidence. The opened TIME archive route exposes limited text through a TollBit wrapper, so future agents should prefer library databases, scans, or the TIME Vault page if direct quotations are needed.

### TIME, "Business: Fourth Down" (1934)

Status: contemporary bankruptcy profile. It anchors the public balance-sheet figure already used in the profile task: liabilities of $2,259,212.48 against assets of $184,900; the page also summarizes earlier trade episodes and public reputation ([TIME, page 1](https://content.time.com/time/subscriber/article/0,33009,747199,00.html); [TIME, page 2](https://content.time.com/time/subscriber/article/0,33009,747199-2,00.html)).

Takeaway: strong near-primary context, weak direct-quote source. It records what lawyers said and what TIME reported; it does not replace court dockets or Livermore's own records.

### TIME, "Business: Boy Plunger" (1940)

Status: near-contemporaneous death profile, published after Livermore's suicide ([TIME, "Boy Plunger," 1940](https://time.com/archive/6764404/business-boy-plunger/)).

Takeaway: useful for career arc, late public perception, and the non-investment reality behind the legend. Avoid extracting trading rules from it unless directly quoted and independently checked.

### TIME, "Business & Finance: Wheat" (1925)

Status: contemporary wheat-market article for the commodity episode that Livermore later revisited in *How to Trade in Stocks* ([TIME, "Wheat," 1925](https://time.com/archive/6818758/business-finance-wheat-7/)).

Takeaway: use to contextualize the wheat chapter and the market-wide conjecture around large traders; not a clean direct-voice source.

### The New Yorker, "A Corner in Piggly Wiggly" (1959)

Status: retrospective historical account of the Clarence Saunders/Piggly Wiggly corner, with Livermore in the old Wall Street operator ecosystem ([The New Yorker, 1959](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1959/06/06/a-corner-in-piggly-wiggly)).

Takeaway: important for transferability caveats. It shows the market-manipulation/corner environment around Livermore's era, not timeless investing craft.

### Official Regulatory Context

Status: SEC and Investor.gov pages are not Livermore sources, but they are essential guardrails for reading a pre-SEC operator. The SEC page lists major securities laws and the Investor.gov page explains that Congress passed the Securities Act of 1933 and Securities Exchange Act of 1934 after the 1929 crash, creating the SEC ([SEC statutes](https://www.sec.gov/rules-regulations/statutes-regulations); [Investor.gov role of the SEC](https://www.investor.gov/introduction-investing/investing-basics/role-sec)).

Takeaway: the more a Livermore quotation sounds like operator-era manipulation, corners, or tape-driven opacity, the more it needs a modern-regulatory caveat.

### Secondary Orientation Sources

Federal Reserve History's Panic of 1907 essay supplies macrostructure for the 1907 crisis and the pre-Fed, pre-SEC environment ([Federal Reserve History](https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/panic-of-1907)). Britannica's 1929 crash page and Investopedia's Livermore profile are useful quick checks for dates and reported public claims, but they should remain below primary/near-primary sources in the citation hierarchy ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/stock-market-crash-of-1929); [Investopedia Livermore](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/j/jesse-l-livermore.asp)). Business Insider and Reformed Broker are tertiary/practitioner context only; do not use them to certify exact numbers or quotes ([Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/the-life-of-jesse-livermore-2015-7); [Reformed Broker](https://thereformedbroker.com/2017/04/18/jesse-and-the-quake/)).

## What The Direct Voice Says

Livermore's own 1940 voice is less swaggering than the folklore. He presents speculation as hard, repetitive work: records, time, price, patience, and self-control. The direct book does not mainly say "trust your genius." It says to wait until market action confirms the idea, use records to identify pivotal points, cut losses, avoid overtrading, follow current leaders, and remove some cash from Wall Street after success.

The strongest live lesson is the contradiction between rule and biography. Livermore could write a disciplined manual in 1940, yet earlier tasks in this folder document repeated bankruptcy, disastrous lapses around tips/averaging/overtrading, and eventual personal tragedy. The quote bank should therefore be read as a map of the rules he wanted to obey, not as proof that he obeyed them. This is especially important for the no-averaging and capital-withdrawal passages: they are among his cleanest rules precisely because his life shows the cost of violating them.

The second lesson is source hygiene. The cultural Livermore is a composite of the 1940 book, Lefevre's Larry Livingston, TIME profiles, later biographies, and trader quote collections. The Canon should preserve that composite as an object of study, but it should not collapse it into direct speech.
