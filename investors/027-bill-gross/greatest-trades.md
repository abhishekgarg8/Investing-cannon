# Bill Gross - Greatest Trades

As of 2026-06-29T04:13:40Z. This was a stale retry of T0213. Bill Gross is alive and still publishing; this file treats his best trades as fixed-income trade clusters because PIMCO did not publish a security-level ledger for most Gross-era positions.

## Evidence cautions and ranking method

Gross's greatest trades rarely look like a stock-picker's one-ticket multibagger. They were usually portfolio-level choices across duration, curve, mortgage, credit, and volatility exposures, often implemented by a PIMCO team under Gross's investment-committee leadership. The cleanest hard numbers are therefore fund returns, position-size clues, one-day or period P&L reported in contemporaneous journalism, and regulator records. Where exact trade-level P&L is unavailable, this file labels the figure as portfolio-level, secondary, or single-source.

The single best trade is the 2007-2009 mortgage-crisis positioning: avoiding subprime and high-yield exposure while loading the Total Return Fund with agency mortgage debt before the government put Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac into conservatorship. It combined thesis, size, timing, direct P&L evidence, and franchise impact better than any other Gross trade.

## 1. 2007-2009 agency MBS and Fannie/Freddie rescue - best trade

**Context and dates.** By 2005-2006, PIMCO was worried that housing credit was unstable. Gross later told Barry Ritholtz that PIMCO became sensitive to instability in housing, sent 10 credit analysts around the country to test mortgage-underwriting conditions, and removed subprime mortgages and high-yield bonds from portfolios before the crisis ([Ritholtz transcript, 2022](https://ritholtz.com/2022/04/transcript-bill-gross/)). Gross's 2022 retrospective says the 2007-2009 period was especially reflective of the original total-return concept because PIMCO avoided subprime mortgages and then saw firm assets roughly double in the following years ([Gross, 2022](https://williamhgross.com/50-basis-deposit-no-return/)).

**Thesis and how he found it.** The thesis was not simply "housing is bad." Gross separated private-label/subprime mortgage credit from agency-backed mortgage debt. The field research pointed away from subprime credit. The agency trade rested on a different belief: that Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac's agency debt and mortgage-backed securities had enough systemic importance that the U.S. government would support them. In May 2008, contemporaneous reporting said Gross had tripled mortgage-debt exposure to more than 60% of PIMCO Total Return, mainly in agency debt rather than subprime paper, because of the government's implicit support of Freddie and Fannie ([MarketWatch/FT summary, 2008](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/pimcos-gross-scoops-up-mortgage-debt-ft); [Business Insider/FT summary, 2008](https://www.businessinsider.com/2008/5/pimco-s-gross-loads-boat-with-mortgage-debt-says-treasuries-a-lay-up-short)).

**Size and structure.** The key size figure is the reported more-than-60% mortgage-debt allocation in the Total Return Fund by May 2008, up from roughly 20% a year earlier; the fund itself was about $132 billion in the Telegraph/HousingWire account and much larger than most bond peers ([HousingWire/Telegraph summary, 2008](https://www.housingwire.com/articles/we-have-winner-and-his-name-bill-gross/)). The exposure was primarily agency debt/MBS rather than equity in Fannie or Freddie; that distinction mattered because common and preferred shareholders were badly impaired while agency bondholders benefited from government support.

**Entry and path.** The entry was uncomfortable. Agency spreads had widened because markets doubted the GSEs' capital and implicit guarantee. Gross was public in calling for federal support, which created a conflict-of-interest critique: he was arguing for policy that would help securities PIMCO owned. HousingWire captured that criticism and quoted Gross acknowledging that the forecast was self-serving ([HousingWire, 2008](https://www.housingwire.com/articles/we-have-winner-and-his-name-bill-gross/)). The policy path broke in PIMCO's favor. FHFA says it placed Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac into conservatorship on September 6, 2008, after severe housing-market deterioration left the enterprises unable to fulfill their missions without intervention ([FHFA, 2008/updated](https://www.fhfa.gov/conservatorship/history)). Treasury says the preferred stock purchase agreements committed it to invest as needed to keep each GSE net-worth positive, initially up to $100 billion for each ([U.S. Treasury, 2021](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sm1236)).

**Exit and P&L.** The sharpest P&L number is secondary but contemporaneous: the Fannie/Freddie takeover reportedly gave Gross and the fund a $1.7 billion one-day gain, about a 1.3% fund move, after the explicit government backstop lifted agency debt ([HousingWire/Telegraph summary, 2008](https://www.housingwire.com/articles/we-have-winner-and-his-name-bill-gross/)). Reuters Graphics later reported that the fund returned over 9% in 2007, ranked first in its peer group, and returned 4.82% in 2008 while competitors averaged a 4.69% loss; it also said the fund took more than $65 billion of net inflows during 2008-2009 ([Reuters Graphics, 2012](https://graphics.thomsonreuters.com/12/02/BillGross.pdf)). Another 2013 account put Total Return's 2008 gain at 3.54%, beating peer bond funds by more than eight percentage points ([The Fiscal Times, 2013](https://www.thefiscaltimes.com/Columns/2013/09/09/Bill-Gross-Can-Bond-King-Stop-Bleeding)). The discrepancy likely reflects share class or measurement-period differences; the outperformance direction is consistent.

**What it teaches.** Gross's best trade was a credit-quality discrimination trade: avoid the toxic part of a mortgage system while buying the systemically protected part. It also shows the moral hazard and policy-dependence of crisis investing. The edge was analytical and political-economic, but the optics were difficult because PIMCO benefited from a public rescue it openly supported.

## 2. 2000-2002 recession/rate-cut complex - Treasuries, TIPS, eurodollars, corporates

**Context and dates.** In 2000, Gross and other PIMCO managers concluded that the tech bubble and the Fed's prior tightening campaign were setting up recession. Fortune reported that as early as May 2000 he was calling for recession and repositioning PIMCO portfolios toward government securities and federally backed mortgages ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**Thesis and how he found it.** Gross's thesis was classic total-return fixed income: slowing growth and disinflation would make duration, high-quality spread, and rate-sensitive instruments outperform equities and lower-quality credit. This aligned with the process he later formalized as a three-to-five-year secular outlook plus structural tilts in duration, curve, credit, and volatility ([Gross, 2005](https://williamhgross.com/consistent-alpha-generation-through-structure/)).

**Size and structure.** Fortune gives the most granular trade map: PIMCO sold about $10 billion of corporate bonds and bought a similar amount of government securities and federally backed mortgages; then it put billions more into Treasuries; a $20 billion Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities stake became a rate-cut expression; after September 11, PIMCO accumulated about $5 billion of higher-yielding investment-grade corporates such as AT&T, Ford, and GMAC; and Gross bought 100,000 eurodollar futures contracts around the December 2001 Fed meeting ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**Entry and path.** The entry path was staged. First PIMCO de-risked away from corporates before the slowdown. Then it added duration and TIPS as the Fed eased. After the post-September 11 flight to quality widened corporate spreads, PIMCO bought higher-quality corporates while avoiding Enron-like risk. The eurodollar futures position was a direct bet that the market was overpricing a quick Fed reversal; Fortune says the contract price implied a much higher federal funds rate one year out than Gross thought plausible ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**Exit and P&L.** Fortune attributes roughly $500 million to the early 2000 government/federally backed mortgage rotation, about $1.5 billion to the $20 billion TIPS stake, roughly $200 million to the AT&T/Ford/GMAC corporate-debt rally, and about $100 million to the 100,000 eurodollar contracts after the December 2001 Fed cut. These figures are single-source and PIMCO-wide, not audited fund-level P&L, but they are the best opened trade-level numbers for Gross's pre-crisis peak ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**What it teaches.** This was Gross's playbook in miniature: macro regime call first, then multiple fixed-income instruments to express it. The lesson is not one perfect bond pick. It is sequencing: reduce credit before recession, add duration and inflation-protected securities when rate cuts are likely, then buy stronger corporates after spreads compensate for fear.

## 3. Early total-return bond trading, 1975-1976 - proving bonds could be traded

**Context and dates.** Gross's foundational trade was to challenge buy-and-hold bond culture in the 1970s. In the inflationary bond bear market, he argued that trading bonds could reduce risk and capture volatility rather than merely locking in low coupons. Fortune reports that Pacific Mutual started him with $15 million and that four years later he was managing $40 million; in 1975 he gained 17.6%, followed by nearly 18% in 1976 ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**Thesis and how he found it.** The thesis was that duration, inflation expectations, and yield changes could be managed actively. Gross later described the early-1980s total-return insight as starting from very high Treasury yields: with 30-year yields near 15% and durations around six to seven years, investors could receive income and still have room for capital gains if rates fell ([Gross, 2024](https://williamhgross.com/they-just-wanna-sell-you-a-bond-fund/)).

**Size and structure.** The initial account was small by later PIMCO standards, but strategically huge: a $15 million trial portfolio became proof of concept for active fixed-income management. The structure was not one bond; it was trading duration and quality across an insurer's bond portfolio.

**Entry and path.** The path included brutal macro conditions. Passive bondholders were suffering from inflation and rising yields. Gross's claim was that active trading allowed the portfolio to avoid sinking bonds and move toward higher yields. The later bond bull market turned that into a decades-long franchise, but the first success came before PIMCO had the scale, brand, or dealer leverage it later enjoyed.

**Exit and P&L.** The exact holdings and security-level exits are not public. The reported 17.6% and nearly 18% gains in 1975 and 1976 are therefore portfolio outcomes, not a single trade ledger ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**What it teaches.** This is the origin trade. Gross made his later agency-MBS, TIPS, eurodollar, and credit trades possible by first proving that bonds were not static vault assets. The transferability warning is that this worked best when starting yields were high; Gross himself later argued that the same total-return concept became much harder after yields approached zero ([Gross, 2022](https://williamhgross.com/50-basis-deposit-no-return/)).

## 4. 2001 emerging-market credit dislocation - Argentina avoidance, Mexico/Brazil buying

**Context and dates.** This was more a PIMCO platform trade than a Gross-only trade. Fortune reported that Mohamed El-Erian and Curtis Mewbourne generated a 28% return in PIMCO Emerging Markets in 2001, helped by shorting or exiting Argentina debt before crisis conditions became obvious and then buying debt in Mexico and Brazil when investors dumped Latin American bonds broadly ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**Thesis and how they found it.** The thesis was cross-country credit discrimination: Argentina was near default, but contagion had cheapened stronger Latin credits. This sat within Gross's larger framework of using specialist desks inside PIMCO while the central investment committee coordinated risk. Gross later credited PIMCO's specialists, including high-yield, mortgage, global, futures, convexity, and yield-curve people, for ideas that flowed into broader portfolios ([Ritholtz transcript, 2022](https://ritholtz.com/2022/04/transcript-bill-gross/)).

**Size and structure.** Fortune says the PIMCO duo bought about $1.5 billion of Mexico and Brazil debt after the Argentina panic. Position sizes as a percent of the emerging-market fund or Total Return Fund were not recovered.

**Entry and path.** Entry occurred after contagion selling widened spreads across Latin America. The drawdown endured is not quantified in the opened sources, but the trade required buying while investors were fleeing the region after Argentina risk became visible.

**Exit and P&L.** The 28% fund return in 2001 is the best performance figure opened; exact Mexico/Brazil security-level P&L is not available. This belongs in the Gross file because it shows how the PIMCO machine generated trade ideas under the investment culture Gross built, but attribution should be to El-Erian/Mewbourne and the PIMCO platform rather than to Gross alone ([Fortune, 2002](https://fortune.com/article/bill-gross-pimco-bond-king/)).

**What it teaches.** Gross's edge was institutional. Some of the greatest trades under his umbrella came from specialists, not from the person at the center of the horseshoe desk. The model worked when a macro framework left room for bottom-up experts to buy mispriced credit after forced selling.

## 5. 2012 PIMCO Total Return ETF odd-lot non-agency MBS - profitable but tainted

**Context and dates.** PIMCO launched the actively managed PIMCO Total Return ETF, later ticker BOND, on February 29, 2012. Its registration statement said the fund sought maximum total return consistent with preservation of capital and prudent investment management, and that William H. Gross managed the fund from inception ([SEC prospectus, 2011](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1450011/000119312511103136/d485apos.htm)).

**Thesis and how it was found.** The trading thesis was that small non-agency MBS odd lots could be bought below the pricing vendor's institutional round-lot marks, creating immediate performance lift when marked at those higher levels. Economically, this was a microstructure/basis trade in thinly traded mortgage bonds.

**Size and structure.** The SEC order says BOND grew from $102 million at inception to more than $1.7 billion by June 30, 2012. It says PIMCO bought roughly 156 non-agency MBS odd-lot positions for a total of $37 million during the relevant period; 43 were later found not to have a reasonable basis for valuation at the vendor mark ([SEC order, 2016](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2016/ia-4577.pdf)).

**Entry and path.** Internal notes cited by the SEC show the strategy was intended to help early performance. The SEC order says BOND outperformed TRF and the Barclays Aggregate from inception through June 30, 2012: BOND returned 6.27%, versus 2.83% for TRF and 1.50% for the index ([SEC order, 2016](https://www.sec.gov/files/litigation/admin/2016/ia-4577.pdf)).

**Exit and P&L.** This was not a clean victory. The SEC later said PIMCO misled investors about BOND's early performance source, failed to disclose that the odd-lot performance was not sustainable as the ETF grew, overstated NAV by as much as $0.31 for a subset of shares, and agreed to disgorgement, interest, and an $18.3 million penalty without admitting or denying the findings ([SEC press release, 2016](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2016-252)). The measurable gain was early ETF outperformance and asset gathering; the cost was regulatory sanction and reputational damage.

**What it teaches.** This is a greatest-trade candidate only if the word "greatest" includes cautionary precision. It shows how Gross/PIMCO could find tiny fixed-income pricing gaps. It also shows that alpha which cannot be disclosed plainly is not durable public-fund alpha.

## 6. 2012 Total Return rebound after the 2011 Treasury error

**Context and dates.** Gross's 2011 Treasury underweight was a mistake covered in the D file. The rebound mattered because it showed that the process could recover from a public macro miss. The Fiscal Times reported that in 2012 PIMCO Total Return gained 9.12%, more than double the Barclays Aggregate's 4.21% gain ([The Fiscal Times, 2013](https://www.thefiscaltimes.com/Columns/2013/09/09/Bill-Gross-Can-Bond-King-Stop-Bleeding)).

**Thesis and how he found it.** The reconstructed thesis was to re-engage risk and inflation-sensitive exposures after the 2011 mea culpa while keeping a long-term view that developed-market debt and monetary policy would keep fixed-income opportunity alive. The same Fiscal Times account says that by April 2013 Gross had a 12% TIPS stake and emerging-market fixed-income exposure; those later hurt in the taper tantrum, but they also reflect the risk sources behind the 2012 rebound ([The Fiscal Times, 2013](https://www.thefiscaltimes.com/Columns/2013/09/09/Bill-Gross-Can-Bond-King-Stop-Bleeding)).

**Size and structure.** The opened sources do not provide the full 2012 exposure table. The 12% TIPS stake is an April 2013 snapshot, not proof of the exact 2012 average position. Therefore this trade is included as a portfolio-level rebound rather than a discrete security trade.

**Entry and path.** The drawdown was reputational and performance-based: Gross had to change course after the 2011 Treasury mistake. In 2013 the same set of macro bets became painful as the Fed taper scare hit TIPS and emerging-market debt, so the rebound had a short half-life.

**Exit and P&L.** The clearest figure is the 9.12% 2012 fund return versus 4.21% for the Barclays Aggregate. Exact absolute P&L is not disclosed in the opened sources.

**What it teaches.** A great fixed-income manager can recover from a bad macro call, but recovery trades may carry the seeds of the next drawdown. Gross's 2012 rebound and 2013 pain should be studied together, not separately.

## 7. 2024 meme-stock option selling - post-retirement small trade

**Context and dates.** After retiring from active fund management, Gross kept trading personal capital. In May 2024, as GameStop and AMC surged again, Bloomberg reporting republished by Financial Advisor said Gross sold options on both meme stocks rather than buying the rally ([Financial Advisor/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.fa-mag.com/news/bill-gross-sells-gamestop--amc-options-to-cash-in-on-meme-mania-78107.html)).

**Thesis and how he found it.** The thesis was volatility selling: meme-stock implied volatility was so high that a short straddle could make money if the stock stayed inside a wide range. This is consistent with Gross's own 2022 statement that selling volatility helped PIMCO, but only in small, diversified size ([Gross, 2022](https://williamhgross.com/winning/)).

**Size and structure.** The reported GameStop trade was a $40 call-and-put straddle with $22 of combined premium, profitable if the stock closed between $18 and $62 at expiration and best near $40. He used a similar $10 straddle on AMC. The article also says he had recently exited a Trump Media options position ([Financial Advisor/Bloomberg, 2024](https://www.fa-mag.com/news/bill-gross-sells-gamestop--amc-options-to-cash-in-on-meme-mania-78107.html)).

**Entry and path.** Entry came after two-day surges of 179% in GameStop and 135% in AMC, according to the same Bloomberg report. The path risk was obvious: short straddles lose if volatility keeps exploding.

**Exit and P&L.** The 2024 article does not provide final P&L for these specific straddles. Earlier media reports said Gross had made millions shorting GameStop in 2021, but the exact 2021 source was not reopened in this run, so this file does not treat that dollar figure as verified. The 2024 trade is included because its structure is unusually explicit.

**What it teaches.** Gross's post-retirement trading shows the continuity of his model: sell overpriced volatility, know the range, and size it so path risk cannot ruin the portfolio. It is not comparable to PIMCO-era public-fund trades because it used personal capital and much smaller scale.

## Cross-trade lessons

1. Gross's largest wins came from identifying which fixed-income risks were actually protected, compensated, or mispriced: agency MBS in 2008, duration/TIPS in 2000-2002, and odd-lot mortgage bonds in 2012.
2. His best trades were rarely pure forecasts. They combined macro regime, structural premium, and implementation.
3. Position size was the edge and the risk. A 60% agency-mortgage allocation made the 2008 gain meaningful; the same public-fund scale later made 2013-2014 outflows painful.
4. PIMCO's team deserves explicit attribution. Gross led the culture and investment committee, but mortgage, global, high-yield, futures, and convexity specialists generated important trade ideas.
5. The cleanest transferable lesson is not to copy the security list. It is to decompose each bond trade into duration, credit, convexity, liquidity, policy dependency, and client-flow risk before sizing it.
