## Method and Ranking Notes

Bill Miller's best trades are hard to reduce to tidy entry-and-exit tickets. The public record gives strong evidence about holdings, portfolio philosophy, fund-level returns, and some personal-investment anecdotes, but it rarely discloses full cost basis, realized gains, or position-level profit and loss. This file therefore ranks trades by a combination of documented economic impact, repeatability as an expression of Miller's method, and influence on his record. Whenever a security-level P&L is not public, the trade is marked as not reconstructable rather than estimated.

The single best trade is Amazon. It combines all of Miller's defining traits: willingness to own a "growth" business as value, long holding period, tolerance for deep volatility, and personal capital concentration. Bitcoin may have produced the most explosive personal mark-to-market return from early lots, but the evidence is interview-based and does not provide a full ledger. Amazon is better documented across Miller letters, fund reports, and long-form interviews.

## 1. Amazon - Best Single Trade

### Context and Dates

Amazon became a signature Miller holding because it sat outside the usual value-investing comfort zone. In his 2006 Q4 commentary, Miller used Amazon as an example of a business investors misunderstood because they extrapolated then-current low operating margins too far into the future; he connected the holding directly to his view that intrinsic business value is the present value of future free cash flows, not a low accounting multiple ([Miller Value Partners, 2006 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/2006-q4-market-commentary/)). A 2022 Richer, Wiser, Happier interview page later described Miller as the biggest individual Amazon shareholder outside the Bezos family and said Amazon and Bitcoin together exceeded 80% of his personal investment portfolio at that point ([The Investor's Podcast, 2022](https://www.theinvestorspodcast.com/richer-wiser-happier/investing-legend-bill-miller-on-amazon-bitcoin-buffett/)).

This is not a single trade in the hedge-fund sense. It is a multi-decade compounder held across public funds, successor vehicles, and Miller's personal balance sheet. The public mutual-fund record confirms Amazon in Miller-managed portfolios during the late streak and crisis period, while the interview record supports the personal concentration claim. The exact first purchase date, complete share history, and final realized gain are not public.

### Thesis and How He Found It

The Amazon thesis was that reported margins understated normalized business value because the company was reinvesting in scale, selection, logistics, and customer relationships. Miller's 2006 letter argued that the market had embedded the wrong long-term expectation into Amazon's valuation, a classic version of his "what is discounted?" process. In the same letter, he rejected the growth-versus-value split and asked instead where the best value was, which is the philosophical opening that allowed him to own a company like Amazon while still calling himself a value investor ([Miller Value Partners, 2006 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/2006-q4-market-commentary/)).

The position also fit his older 1998 view that good portfolio construction lets winners run. In that letter, he contrasted the S&P 500's buy-and-hold, winner-retention behavior with the average active manager's habit of trimming winners too mechanically; Value Trust's lower turnover and willingness to let successful holdings grow became part of the method ([Miller Value Partners, 1998 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/1998-q4-market-commentary/)).

### Size and Structure

The structure was common equity in mutual funds and personal accounts. Exact fund-level Amazon weights need year-by-year portfolio reconstruction from annual reports. The personal concentration is better documented directionally than numerically: the 2022 interview page states that Amazon and Bitcoin together accounted for more than 80% of Miller's personal investment portfolio, and the transcript includes Miller saying his cost basis in Amazon was effectively zero after years of ownership ([The Investor's Podcast, 2022](https://www.theinvestorspodcast.com/richer-wiser-happier/investing-legend-bill-miller-on-amazon-bitcoin-buffett/)).

### Entry, Path, and Drawdown

Amazon's path was brutal enough to test the thesis repeatedly. The company passed through the dot-com crash, repeated episodes of margin skepticism, and later drawdowns in technology and growth stocks. The record does not expose the exact peak-to-trough drawdown for Miller's aggregate Amazon position, but the strategic point is clear: Miller's edge was not a precise bottom tick; it was his willingness to value the business on normalized long-term economics and stay with it when near-term reported margins looked poor.

### Exit and P&L

No complete exit record or security-level P&L is public. The trade was extraordinarily profitable by any reasonable mark-to-market comparison from the late 1990s or early 2000s to the 2020-2022 period, but a precise dollar gain would require Miller's internal account records and fund transaction ledgers. This file therefore does not assign an absolute P&L. The available evidence supports "best trade" status by documented personal concentration, longevity, and the central role of Amazon in explaining Miller's style.

### What It Teaches

Amazon teaches that Miller's value investing was a future-cash-flow discipline rather than a cheap-multiple discipline. The same trait that helped him own Amazon also later harmed him in financials and housing-linked names when normalized-value estimates were wrong. The lesson is powerful but not simple: long-duration contrarianism works only when the business value really is compounding underneath the controversy.

## 2. Bitcoin - The Asymmetric Personal Option

### Context and Dates

Bitcoin became Miller's late-career version of the misunderstood, hated, hard-to-value asset. By January 2021, in his 2020 Q4 market letter, Miller described Bitcoin as the best-performing asset category in 2020, said it was early in its adoption cycle, and compared it to "digital gold" with advantages over the metal ([Miller Value Partners, 2020 Q4](https://millervalue.com/bill-miller-4q-2020-market-letter/)). The 2022 Richer, Wiser, Happier page says he began buying Bitcoin at $200 and still held a very large personal allocation through Amazon and Bitcoin together ([The Investor's Podcast, 2022](https://www.theinvestorspodcast.com/richer-wiser-happier/investing-legend-bill-miller-on-amazon-bitcoin-buffett/)).

### Thesis and How He Found It

The thesis was not a conventional discounted-cash-flow thesis. Miller framed Bitcoin as an adoption option, a store-of-value experiment, and a hedge against fiat-money risk. In the 2020 Q4 letter, he argued that cash was being forced into a negative real-return role by Federal Reserve policy and that corporate balance-sheet adoption by companies such as Square, MassMutual, and MicroStrategy could matter if it broadened ([Miller Value Partners, 2020 Q4](https://millervalue.com/bill-miller-4q-2020-market-letter/)). In the 2021 Q2 letter, he was more guarded, calling Bitcoin an open question as an enduring store of value while still emphasizing the inflation and forecasting uncertainty surrounding markets ([Miller Value Partners, 2021 Q2](https://millervalue.com/bill-miller-2q-2021-market-letter/)).

### Size and Structure

The cleanest evidence is personal-capital exposure. The 2022 interview page reports that Amazon and Bitcoin together represented more than 80% of his personal investment portfolio. The page also says he began buying at $200. Those are interview-page claims and should be treated as single-source unless audio-checked and reconciled with other interviews. Patient Capital's current ADV permits flexible instruments, including Bitcoin ETFs in some contexts, but that does not by itself prove a specific Miller III fund-level Bitcoin allocation as of this task ([Patient Capital 2026 ADV](https://www.morganstanley.com/content/dam/msdotcom/en/wealth-investmentsolutions/pdfs/adv/Patient-ADV.pdf)).

### Entry, Path, and Drawdown

If the $200 early purchase reference is accurate, Bitcoin's mark-to-market path to more than $31,000 by Miller's 2020 Q4 letter represented roughly a 155x price increase on the earliest lots before later volatility. The path included severe drawdowns: the 2018 crypto winter, the 2021-2022 decline, and repeated regulatory and counterparty scares. Miller's 2022 interview was conducted during a painful selloff in technology and crypto assets, and the page records his focus on handling the emotional pressure of brutal drawdowns ([The Investor's Podcast, 2022](https://www.theinvestorspodcast.com/richer-wiser-happier/investing-legend-bill-miller-on-amazon-bitcoin-buffett/)).

### Exit and P&L

Exact average cost, realized sales, tax basis, and current holdings are not public. The trade may have been one of Miller's highest-return personal investments on early lots, but the only defensible statement is that early purchases at the cited $200 level compounded massively by 2020-2021 market prices. No absolute dollar profit should be asserted without account-level evidence.

### What It Teaches

Bitcoin shows both the reach and the danger of Miller's framework. He was willing to analyze an asset that many investors dismissed as uninvestable, ask what was already discounted, and size the upside asymmetry. But unlike Amazon or Google, Bitcoin has no business cash flows. The trade therefore belongs in the Canon as an example of probabilistic optionality, not as a transferable template for calling every volatile asset cheap.

## 3. Mid-1990s Technology Basket - Dell, AOL, and the Streak Engine

### Context and Dates

Miller's famous 15-calendar-year streak from 1991 through 2005 did not come from acting like a classic low price-to-book manager. His 2006 Q4 reflection says the key portfolio shift began in the mid-1990s, when he saw technology stocks trading at prices he considered cheap relative to their economics. He specifically cited Dell Computer at about five times earnings and Cisco at about 15 times earnings, and he said Value Trust bought a lot of technology in the mid-1990s ([Miller Value Partners, 2006 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/2006-q4-market-commentary/)).

The 1998 Q4 letter provides contemporaneous evidence of the position impact. Value Trust rose 48.04% in 1998 and Miller noted that Dell and AOL had become much larger relative to his portfolio than the largest S&P 500 position was to that index ([Miller Value Partners, 1998 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/1998-q4-market-commentary/)).

### Thesis and How He Found It

The thesis was that investors were misclassifying technology as unpredictable and therefore unfit for value analysis. Miller had been influenced by Brian Arthur's ideas about path dependence and lock-in, which made some technology market shares more durable than they looked. He compared high-return, high-growth technology companies against capital-intensive cyclicals and concluded that the tech names were the better value in the mid-1990s ([Miller Value Partners, 2006 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/2006-q4-market-commentary/)).

### Size and Structure

The structure was common stock in Legg Mason Value Trust. Exact weights by purchase date are not reconstructed here, but the 1998 letter's warning that Dell and AOL were outsized relative to the portfolio indicates meaningful concentration. The fund usually held a focused portfolio of roughly 35 to 40 stocks, with low turnover and no rigid sector weights, as Miller explained in the 1997 Q4 letter ([Miller Value Partners, 1997 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/1997-q4-market-commentary/)).

### Entry, Path, and Drawdown

The entry window was the mid-1990s. The path was excellent during 1998-1999 as technology became a market mania, then painful during the 2000-2002 bear market. Miller later wrote that he sold a lot of those names reasonably well in 1999 but did not sell all of them, so he did less well than some traditional value peers during the bear market that ended in March 2003 ([Miller Value Partners, 2006 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/2006-q4-market-commentary/)).

### Exit and P&L

Security-level P&L for Dell, AOL, Cisco, and related holdings is not public in a complete ledger. Fund-level evidence is strong: Value Trust's 1998 return was 48.04%, and the 2005 letter states that the fund outperformed the S&P 500 for 15 calendar years through 2005 ([Miller Value Partners, 1998 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/1998-q4-market-commentary/); [Miller Value Partners, 2005 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/515/)). The basket helped power the streak, but the exact contribution cannot be isolated without detailed holdings and transaction data.

### What It Teaches

This was Miller's methodological breakthrough. He did not win by discovering a new valuation formula. He won by refusing to let style labels stop him from comparing all available expected returns. The drawback is also visible: when a flexible investor owns the hot sector, the line between disciplined valuation and mania participation can blur.

Supporting source: [Patient Capital, A Look at Bill's Record](https://patientcapitalmanagement.com/articles/a_look_at_bills_record).

## 4. Google IPO - Buying "Expensive" Growth as Value

### Context and Dates

Google is the cleanest documented example of Miller buying an apparently expensive new-economy company as a value investment. In the 2005 Q4 shareholder note, he asked whether Google had been good value at the $85 IPO price and answered by pointing to its $436 price about a year and a half later. He then wrote that Value Trust owned Google at the IPO and still owned it at the time of the letter ([Miller Value Partners, 2005 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/515/)).

### Thesis and How He Found It

The thesis was that the IPO was misread as another internet-hype stock when the business had unusually strong economics and optionality. Miller used Google to explain variant perception: the market could have the public facts yet weight the magnitude or duration of the opportunity incorrectly. The Google example appears in the same letter that defines his process around expectations embedded in valuation ([Miller Value Partners, 2005 Q4](https://millervalue.com/letters/515/)).

### Size and Structure

The position was common equity in Value Trust. Exact position size at the IPO and at later dates is not reconstructed in this task. Miller's 2005 letter confirms ownership but does not provide purchase amount, percentage of fund assets, or shares held.

### Entry, Path, and Drawdown

The documented entry is the 2004 IPO at $85. By the 2005 Q4 letter, Miller cited a $436 price. That is a roughly 5.1x gross price increase from the IPO price before considering any subsequent trades, trims, or additions. The later path included the 2008 market decline, during which internet and technology holdings were not immune to fund-level stress, but Google itself was not among the primary detractors highlighted in the Value Trust crisis report.

### Exit and P&L

The exact exit and realized P&L are not public. The $85-to-$436 comparison establishes a large unrealized gain by late 2005 for IPO shares still held, but the fund's trade ledger is needed to compute absolute dollars, final multiple, and after-tax outcome. This file therefore records the price path as a documented mark-to-market gain, not a complete P&L.

### What It Teaches

Google shows Miller's core analytical move in compact form: he looked past the optical multiple and asked whether the market was underestimating the duration and magnitude of future free cash flows. It also shows why his approach could not be replicated by screens. The edge was judgment about business quality and expectations, not the existence of a low P/E ratio.

### Sources

Primary sources: [2005 Q4 Miller letter](https://millervalue.com/letters/515/), [2006 Q4 Miller letter](https://millervalue.com/letters/2006-q4-market-commentary/), [Legg Mason Value Trust 2008 N-CSR](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/357235/000119312508129538/dncsr.htm).

## 5. Post-Crisis Opportunity Trust Comeback - The Second-Act Trade

### Context and Dates

The post-crisis Opportunity Trust comeback is included as a "trade" only in the strategy-level sense. It was a long recovery campaign after Miller's worst period, not a single security. The drawdown was severe: the SEC-filed 2008 Opportunity Trust report shows the Primary Class down 65.49% for the year ended December 31, 2008, and the report disclosed average daily borrowings of $412.8 million under leveraging credit agreements during the year ([SEC N-CSR, Opportunity Trust 2008](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1096338/000119312509046308/dncsr.htm)).

By 2019, Institutional Investor reported that Miller Opportunity Trust ranked in the top 1% of Morningstar's midcap blend category for the three- and ten-year periods ended January 17, 2019, and had outperformed the S&P 500 by 2.97 percentage points per year over the prior decade ([Institutional Investor, 2019](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bswi2n30990nntbscc1s/corner-office/bill-miller-in-the-wilderness-and-loving-it)). Patient Capital's affiliated performance essay reports that the Opportunity Equity strategy had returned 7.35% annually net of fees from inception to June 30, 2015 versus 4.19% for the S&P 500 ([Patient Capital, A Look at Bill's Record](https://patientcapitalmanagement.com/articles/a_look_at_bills_record)).

### Thesis and How He Found It

The thesis was the same contrarian posture applied after institutional reputation damage: crisis prices, investor disgust, and forced selling could create unusually high expected returns if the assets survived. In a 2020 Ritholtz interview, Miller described the Opportunity fund's March 2009-to-2019 performance as helped by the same value-framework adjustments and psychological observations he had developed over prior decades ([Ritholtz, 2020](https://ritholtz.com/2020/07/transcript-bill-miller/)).

### Size and Structure

The structure was a public mutual fund and related strategy assets, not a single concentrated stock. The 2008 SEC report documents leverage risk and borrowings, while later successor-firm materials document the continuation of the Opportunity Equity strategy under Patient Capital after the May 2023 transaction, with Samantha McLemore continuing as lead manager and Miller remaining a minority owner/investor ([Patient Capital succession release](https://patientcapitalmanagement.com/articles/patient_capital_finalizes_bill_millers_succession_planning_deal)).

### Entry, Path, and Drawdown

The entry was effectively the 2008-2009 aftermath: a period when Miller's funds had been damaged, clients had redeemed, and media coverage had turned hostile. The path was psychologically hard because the same willingness to average down and tolerate controversy had just produced catastrophic losses in financials and housing-linked securities. The SEC Value Trust report for the year ended March 31, 2008 shows Primary Class down 23.9% versus the S&P 500 down 5.1%, with Bear Stearns and Countrywide Financial detracting a combined 480 basis points ([SEC N-CSR, Value Trust 2008](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/357235/000119312508129538/dncsr.htm)).

### Exit and P&L

This is fund-level and strategy-level evidence, not security-level P&L. The strongest independent-ish datapoint found in this run is Institutional Investor's Morningstar-based 2019 statement about top-1% category ranking and 2.97 percentage points per year of ten-year excess return. The affiliated Patient Capital essay gives longer strategy figures through June 30, 2015, but those should be reconciled with official performance reports before being used as final audited proof. Absolute dollars of profit are not reconstructed.

### What It Teaches

The comeback is important because it keeps Miller from being reduced either to "the streak" or to 2008. It shows that his process could recover when applied to hated assets after forced selling. It also shows the most uncomfortable lesson in his record: the investor's terminal wealth depends on surviving the path. A strategy that can rebound spectacularly may still be unsuitable for clients who cannot endure the drawdown.

## Cross-Trade Lessons

1. Miller's best trades came from valuation elasticity: he was willing to value a retailer, search engine, or software-enabled network by future economics rather than present optics.
2. Position behavior mattered as much as idea generation. His willingness to hold and sometimes add through volatility created both Amazon-scale wins and 2008-scale pain.
3. Fund-level greatness and client experience can diverge. A recovery after a 60% drawdown is impressive for a surviving capital base but cold comfort to a redeeming shareholder.
4. Bitcoin shows that Miller's late-career mind stayed open, but it also marks the edge of his framework because it lacks business cash flows.
5. The most replicable lesson is not "buy controversial assets"; it is "identify the specific expectation the market has mispriced, then keep checking whether the business evidence supports that variance."
