# Michael Burry - Key Writings

As of 2026-06-30T10:14:00Z, Michael Burry's written corpus is real but fragmented. He has not published a full investing book under his own name. The usable record is a chain of early internet stock writeups, reproduced Scion investor letters, a 2010 New York Times op-ed, the 2011 Vanderbilt lecture transcript, SEC-filed GameStop letters, official FCIC interview audio, and the 2025-2026 Cassandra Unchained Substack era. Treat original Burry pages and SEC filings as primary; treat HFA, Acquirer's Multiple, Saber Capital, GuruFocus, Scribd, Business Insider, and Barchart as carriers unless they quote or summarize a source that is otherwise inaccessible.

## Works By Burry

### 1. MSN MoneyCentral Strategy Lab / "Value Doc" archive, 2000-2001

**Central thesis.** The MSN corpus is the best compact statement of Burry before Scion became famous: contrarian value investing begins with price versus value, free cash flow, private-market value, balance-sheet survivability, and willingness to buy unpopular companies that institutional investors ignore. The archive's biography says he looked for out-of-favor industries and overlooked securities through free cash flow and private-market valuation ([MSN archive, 2000](https://gist.github.com/vwkd/d1b5839eb8c7d0182f6cd74bdbb65ab3)). It also shows a public-writing process: long stock analyses edited down for a retail audience, not sound bites ([MSN archive, 2000](https://gist.github.com/vwkd/d1b5839eb8c7d0182f6cd74bdbb65ab3)).

**Key ideas.** First, Burry's early edge was security-level work rather than market timing; his December 2001 Strategy Lab entry says his prior picks were up just over 23% while the S&P 500 and Nasdaq were sharply negative, but he still judged the work by absolute results and stock-picking quality rather than index applause ([MSN archive, 2001](https://gist.github.com/vwkd/d1b5839eb8c7d0182f6cd74bdbb65ab3)). Second, he accepted that a portfolio could look unlike benchmarks for long stretches. Third, he liked unpopular but analyzable businesses, especially where cash flow and enterprise value contradicted the market's disgust. Fourth, he did not reject technology or cyclicals categorically; price and value mattered more than labels. Fifth, he wrote for discipline: the public format forced him to explain the causal chain from facts to valuation. Sixth, he believed a manager must build a portfolio, not merely collect cheap names.

**Best sections to read first.** Start with the biography and strategy page, then the December 3, 2001 "Don't worry about indexes" journal entry. Then read several individual stock writeups to see how he moves from screenable cheapness to balance sheet, free cash flow, and company-specific risk. Because the archive is a GitHub gist preserving older MSN/Wayback material, exact dates and missing URLs should be verified when a claim depends on a specific article.

### 2. Scion Value Fund / Scion Capital investor letters, 2000-2006

**Central thesis.** The Scion letters are Burry's strongest investor-facing philosophy text: the job is to minimize permanent capital loss, exploit volatility rather than fear it, stay bottom-up even when the market looks dangerous, and align manager economics with client results. Several letter copies circulate as PDFs or reproduced excerpts; access is uneven, so cite the carriers honestly. Acquirer's Multiple carries a 2000-letter excerpt emphasizing downside avoidance and the arithmetic asymmetry of losses ([Acquirer's Multiple, 2023](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2023/09/michael-burry-the-difference-between-gained-and-lost-dollars-minimizing-downside-risk-is-key/)); another post carries a 2001-letter excerpt framing volatility as opportunity rather than risk ([Acquirer's Multiple, 2021](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2021/07/michael-burry-volatility-is-my-friend/)). Saber Capital notes that the original public-domain letter set is incomplete, while using the letters and MSN pieces to show Burry staying invested in bargains even while bearish on broad market valuations ([Saber Capital, 2015](https://sabercapitalmgt.com/michael-burry-focus-on-bargains-and-not-stock-market-valuations/)).

**Key ideas.** First, the central risk is permanent loss, not price volatility. Second, a cheap enough security can be safer after it becomes more volatile, because the investor gets repeated chances to buy below value. Third, broad-market pessimism does not automatically imply high cash; Burry can be bearish on indices and still long individual bargains. Fourth, the fund's structure mattered: the old letters discuss fees, expenses, and the burden of not charging asset-based fees when returns were not earned. Fifth, he was a concentrated opportunist, not a formulaic net-net buyer. Sixth, his communication style was candid but not always client-soothing; the later subprime conflict shows that brilliant letters do not guarantee mandate fit. Seventh, reproduced letters are still not audited statements, so all return claims need provenance labels.

**Best sections to read first.** Read the 2000 launch/first-period letter for downside-first arithmetic, the 2001 letters for volatility and portfolio construction, and any 2005-2006 material for the transition from equity value to mortgage credit. The best research upgrade would be to recover original investor PDFs with page images, not just excerpt carriers.

### 3. "I Saw the Crisis Coming. Why Didn't the Fed?", 2010

**Central thesis.** This op-ed is Burry's public post-crisis indictment: the housing bubble was visible in contemporaneous data, not an unforeseeable accident, and official institutions ignored or dismissed the evidence. GuruFocus preserves a visible mirror and identifies the piece as a New York Times op-ed from April 3, 2010 ([GuruFocus mirror, 2010](https://www.gurufocus.com/news/89252/michael-burry-i-saw-the-crisis-coming-why-didnt-the-fed)); Stansberry later summarized the op-ed's role in explaining Burry's public-data process and criticism of regulators ([Stansberry, 2026](https://stansberryresearch.com/stansberry-digest/the-100-data-set-that-exposed-the-last-financial-crisis)).

**Key ideas.** First, Burry rejected the "everybody missed it" defense. Second, he argued that securitization data, mortgage terms, and credit standards gave warning before the collapse. Third, he framed the trade as a research conclusion translated into credit default swaps, not a coin flip. Fourth, he emphasized counterparty and collateral mechanics: the right instrument still needed contracts and settlement discipline. Fifth, he criticized Federal Reserve and regulatory incentives, not only Wall Street greed. Sixth, the op-ed is a bridge between private letters and public policy argument: it shows Burry trying to explain why being early was not the same as being lucky.

**Best sections to read first.** Read the opening challenge to Greenspan, the middle sections explaining how Burry saw the mortgage market before 2007, and the closing policy critique. Use the mirror with caution; if the original NYT page is accessible in a future run, replace the mirror citation.

### 4. Vanderbilt Chancellor's Lecture transcript: "Missteps to Mayhem", 2011

**Central thesis.** The Vanderbilt speech is the best source-visible long-form Burry transcript. It turns the Big Short from a movie-like story into a mechanism: loose mortgage credit, interest-only loans, collateral deterioration, standardized CDS, failed fund-raising for a dedicated short vehicle, counterparty marks, AIG collateral vulnerability, and leadership failure. Vanderbilt's page records the April 5, 2011 talk as part of the Chancellor's Lecture Series ([Vanderbilt transcript, 2011](https://news.vanderbilt.edu/2011/04/13/michael-burry-transcript/)).

**Key ideas.** First, Burry describes public securitization filings as the route into the mortgage thesis. Second, he rejects the sideways-home-price assumption and explains why credit-dependent prices had to fall once peak mortgage products disappeared ([Vanderbilt transcript, 2011](https://news.vanderbilt.edu/2011/04/13/michael-burry-transcript/)). Third, he explains that he tried to set up Milton's Opus, a separate subprime-short vehicle, but could not sell the idea to investors. Fourth, he gives notional scale for the trade: about $1.8 billion in RMBS CDS and about $6.6 billion in housing-linked corporate credits ([Vanderbilt transcript, 2011](https://news.vanderbilt.edu/2011/04/13/michael-burry-transcript/)). Fifth, the AIG discussion shows his habit of reading legal and collateral details others ignored. Sixth, the speech is also a mistake document: he describes client pressure, side pockets, staff cuts, and trade liquidation risk.

**Best sections to read first.** Read the autobiographical opening only for context; the core investing value is the mortgage-underwriting section, the Milton's Opus and notional-exposure section, and the AIG/collateral section. For later tasks, pair the transcript with the FCIC audio and Scion letters to verify chronology.

### 5. GameStop Schedule 13D letters, 2019-2020

**Central thesis.** The GameStop letters show Burry's value process applied to governance: a despised equity can be attractive if cash, buybacks, share count reduction, board incentives, and short interest create a capital-allocation setup. The strongest source is Scion's SEC-filed Schedule 13D and exhibits ([SEC Schedule 13D, 2020](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1326380/000090514820000491/efc20-335_sc13d.htm)).

**Key ideas.** First, Burry framed buybacks at depressed prices as a direct route to per-share value creation. Second, he tied board compensation and tenure to shareholder alignment, not just stock cheapness. Third, he treated short interest as part of the setup but did not underwrite the later retail/gamma squeeze. Fourth, he used letters as pressure tools: the text is concise, operational, and board-facing. Fifth, the filing gives unusually clean position evidence compared with most Burry writings; the exhibits identify approximate ownership and transaction context. Sixth, the later miss matters: the letters captured value and governance, but not the full market-structure reflexivity of January 2021.

**Best sections to read first.** Read Exhibit C/D for buyback arithmetic and Exhibit E for board compensation and governance criticism; SEC line-items around the identity and transaction schedule should be read alongside the letters if using the position size.

### 6. FCIC staff audiotape and crisis archive, 2010

**Central thesis.** The FCIC material is not a polished essay, but it is official primary material for Burry's crisis-era account. Stanford's FCIC site identifies a staff audiotape of an interview with Michael Burry, and the archive directory lists a 97 MB May 18, 2010 Burry audio file ([FCIC interview page, 2010](https://fcic.law.stanford.edu/interviews/view/14); [FCIC audio directory, 2010](https://fcic-static.law.stanford.edu/cdn_media/fcic-audio/)).

**Key ideas.** First, it is a primary oral-history source and should rank above later retellings when transcribed. Second, it sits inside an official inquiry that can cross-reference dealers, ratings agencies, AIG, and mortgage-market participants. Third, because no transcript was source-visible in this run, it should not be quoted without timestamped listening. Fourth, it is the best future source for checking whether Lewis, Vanderbilt, and the op-ed compress or simplify any claim.

**Best sections to read first.** Future work should download or stream the May 18, 2010 audio, create timestamped notes, and compare Burry's account to the FCIC report's mortgage-CDS passages. Until then, cite it as an indexed primary source, not as proof of any exact wording.

### 7. Bloomberg TV transcript and crisis interviews, 2010

**Central thesis.** The Bloomberg interview transcript carrier shows Burry in post-crisis macro-policy mode: still a securities analyst, but now discussing Fed policy, real assets, gold, farmland, and the difficulty of speaking publicly after becoming a crisis character. The accessible copy is a Scribd carrier, not Bloomberg's own page, so treat it as useful but lower-provenance than Vanderbilt or SEC ([Scribd transcript carrier, 2010](https://www.scribd.com/doc/37453934/Michael-Burry-Bloomberg-Transcript)).

**Key ideas.** First, this is where Burry's reputation begins to shift from value-stock picker to public macro skeptic. Second, it helps explain later water/farmland/gold public narratives. Third, it shows why exact quote verification is hard: media transcripts circulate after original publisher pages disappear or become inaccessible. Fourth, it should be used as a lead source unless a Bloomberg original is recovered.

**Best sections to read first.** Read the sections on the housing bubble, Fed policy, and real assets. Avoid treating the transcript carrier as definitive for wording until checked against Bloomberg video or transcript archives.

### 8. Cassandra Unchained and post-Scion writing, 2025-2026

**Central thesis.** Cassandra Unchained is Burry's return to writing as the main product. The About page says the newsletter is his focus and offers analysis of stocks, markets, and bubbles through historical patterns ([Cassandra Unchained, 2026](https://michaeljburry.substack.com/about)). Burry's own December 2025 post says he sat for a roughly 40-minute Michael Lewis interview and had not intended to speak publicly again after 2012 ([Cassandra Unchained, 2025](https://michaeljburry.substack.com/p/michael-lewis-against-the-rules-podcast)). Business Insider reports him saying writing and analysis "go hand-in-hand," while linking the Substack launch to the closure/deregistration of Scion's outside-capital vehicle ([Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/why-big-short-michael-burry-blogging-writing-substack-investing-buffett-2025-11)).

**Key ideas.** First, the Substack marks a structural change: public analysis after Scion Asset Management's SEC adviser status was terminated on November 10, 2025 ([Sherwood, 2025](https://sherwood.news/markets/michael-burry-de-registers-his-hedge-fund-scion-asset-management-as-he-warns/); [Bloomberg Law, 2025](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/private-equity/michael-burrys-scion-hedge-fund-deregistered-sec-filing-shows)). Second, the first major arc is AI-bubble and accounting critique; Business Insider reports his Nvidia response focused on depreciation lives, chip obsolescence, and the difference between his claim and Nvidia's rebuttal ([Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/big-short-michael-burry-substack-nvidia-memo-depreciation-ai-bubble-2025-11)). Third, the Lululemon post shows he is still doing single-stock fundamental work, not merely crash-warning; Business Insider's report captures his argument about brand missteps, tangible book value, margin pressure, and long-run return assumptions ([Business Insider, 2026](https://www.businessinsider.com/big-short-michael-burry-substack-lululemon-atlas-shrugged-bag-stock-2026-6)). Fourth, the current writing is partly gated, so source-visible previews and reputable quoted summaries must be separated from full post claims. Fifth, Barchart's paid-reader review argues the new newsletter is a return to his pre-Big-Short writing roots, but it remains a secondary reader's view ([Barchart, 2025](https://www.barchart.com/story/news/36291929/i-paid-for-michael-burrys-new-400-substack-so-you-dont-have-to)).

**Best sections to read first.** Start with the About page and the Michael Lewis podcast note because they are visible and first-party. Then read visible trading-post previews and reputable summaries of the AI/depreciation and Lululemon pieces. Do not cite paid-only text unless the exact text is visible, archived, or carried by a reliable report.

## Best Works About Burry, Ranked

1. **Michael Lewis, *The Big Short* / Vanity Fair excerpt "Betting on the Blind Side."** Best for narrative and trade-process reconstruction. Vanity Fair describes the excerpt as Lewis charting Burry's trades and the reason for his obsession; the Longreads mirror identifies it as an 11,492-word excerpt from the book ([Vanity Fair archive, 2010](https://archive.vanityfair.com/article/2010/4/betting-on-the-blind-side); [Longreads, 2010](https://longreads.com/2010/03/03/betting-on-the-blind-side/)). Use it for process, investor conflict, and reported P&L, but remember it is a crafted narrative, not an audited ledger.

2. **Vanderbilt transcript and Vanderbilt contributor note.** Best institutional primary/biographical pairing. Vanderbilt supplies the long speech transcript and a short alumni bio confirming education, Stanford residency, Scion founding, and the Forbes-recognized finance website ([Vanderbilt contributor note, 2011](https://news.vanderbilt.edu/2011/09/06/contributors-for-the-summer-2011-issue/)). Use this before derivative biographies.

3. **Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission archive.** Best official primary context for the crisis. The Burry audio and the broader archive let future researchers compare his account with dealers, ratings firms, and regulators ([FCIC interview page, 2010](https://fcic.law.stanford.edu/interviews/view/14); [FCIC audio directory, 2010](https://fcic-static.law.stanford.edu/cdn_media/fcic-audio/)). Its limitation is usability: without a transcript, it is slow to cite.

4. **CBS / 60 Minutes coverage of Michael Lewis and the crisis trade.** Best accessible broadcast-era corroboration for the public version of Burry's story and the $725 million investor-profit figure already used in other Canon files. CBS frames Burry as early to the subprime problem and links the account to Lewis's reporting ([CBS News, 2010](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/author-michael-lewis-on-wall-sts-delusion/)). Use it as corroboration, not as the deepest source.

5. **Sherwood and Bloomberg Law on Scion deregistration.** Best current-status secondary sources for the 2025 transition from registered adviser to public writing. Both report the November 10, 2025 adviser-registration termination; Sherwood adds useful caveats about an unverified circulating investor letter and 13F option-notional confusion ([Sherwood, 2025](https://sherwood.news/markets/michael-burry-de-registers-his-hedge-fund-scion-asset-management-as-he-warns/); [Bloomberg Law, 2025](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/private-equity/michael-burrys-scion-hedge-fund-deregistered-sec-filing-shows)).

6. **Business Insider's 2025-2026 Burry/Substack coverage.** Best current interpreter of the post-Scion writing phase, especially where full Substack posts are gated. The writing-process article includes Burry's email comments on writing and Buffett/Munger influence; the AI and Lululemon pieces summarize current investment arguments with enough specificity to map themes ([Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/why-big-short-michael-burry-blogging-writing-substack-investing-buffett-2025-11); [Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/big-short-michael-burry-substack-nvidia-memo-depreciation-ai-bubble-2025-11); [Business Insider, 2026](https://www.businessinsider.com/big-short-michael-burry-substack-lululemon-atlas-shrugged-bag-stock-2026-6)). Use it as a carrier for gated claims, not a substitute for unlocked first-party text.

7. **Saber Capital, Acquirer's Multiple, and Barchart reading guides.** Best practical guides to the early letters and MSN materials. Saber is candid about incomplete original-letter access; Acquirer's Multiple preserves useful excerpts from the 2000-2001 letters; Barchart is useful for how current readers view Cassandra Unchained as a return to form ([Saber Capital, 2015](https://sabercapitalmgt.com/michael-burry-focus-on-bargains-and-not-stock-market-valuations/); [Acquirer's Multiple, 2021](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2021/07/michael-burry-volatility-is-my-friend/); [Barchart, 2025](https://www.barchart.com/story/news/36291929/i-paid-for-michael-burrys-new-400-substack-so-you-dont-have-to)). Their limitation is provenance: they are maps and excerpts, not the original archive.

## Attribution And Access Caveats

- **Original Scion letters remain the biggest gap.** Public carriers preserve enough to summarize themes, but a future run should recover page-image PDFs and record page references before relying on exact wording.
- **Do not cite movie dialogue as Burry.** The film is culturally important but not a source for his words or process.
- **Do not treat 13F tables as writings.** Use them for position evidence, not philosophy, unless paired with Burry letters or posts explaining the thesis.
- **Use Substack carefully.** Visible first-party text is primary; paid or JavaScript-hidden passages need archive captures, screenshots with provenance, or reliable secondary carriers.
- **Avoid quote aggregators.** Burry's corpus is already messy enough; unsourced aphorisms should be dropped.
