# Julian Robertson - Profile

As of 2026-06-24T09:53:00Z, Julian Hart Robertson Jr. is deceased. He died on August 23, 2022 at age 90, after a career that made Tiger Management one of the defining hedge fund firms of the late twentieth century ([UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/); [Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/)). The central caution for Canon readers is that Robertson's famous Tiger record belongs to a private hedge-fund complex that returned outside capital in 2000, while later Tiger Management activity includes family capital, seeding, public 13F reporting, and posthumous stewardship. Those later footprints are relevant to legacy and structure, but they are not a continuation of the original partnership return series ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/); [SEC 13F filing detail, 2022](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957422005008/0000919574-22-005008-index.html)).

## Life & Career Timeline

- **1932:** Julian Hart Robertson Jr. was born in Salisbury, North Carolina, on June 25, 1932 ([UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/)).
- **1955-1957:** Robertson graduated from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill in 1955, served two years as an officer in the U.S. Navy, and then moved to New York to join Kidder Peabody & Co. ([Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/); [UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/)).
- **1957-1978:** At Kidder Peabody he moved from sales trainee to CEO of Webster Management Corporation, Kidder's investment advisory subsidiary, giving him two decades of brokerage, research, and asset-management apprenticeship before Tiger ([Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/); [FIA Hall of Fame](https://www.fia.org/hall-fame/articles/julian-h-robertson-jr)).
- **1978-1979:** Robertson took a sabbatical in New Zealand with his family. Later accounts often treat this year as the break before he left Kidder and launched his own firm ([UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/)).
- **1980:** Robertson founded Tiger Management with outside capital usually reported around $8 million to $8.8 million, mostly from family, friends, and his own capital ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/); [Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/)).
- **1980s-1998:** Tiger became a dominant long/short hedge-fund complex. Robertson bought what he considered high-quality companies, sold short weaker or overvalued ones, and hired young analysts who later became a hedge-fund alumni network. By 1998, contemporaneous reporting said assets had reached roughly $21-$22 billion; family/foundation and FIA sources cite more than $23 billion ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/); [FIA Hall of Fame](https://www.fia.org/hall-fame/articles/julian-h-robertson-jr)).
- **1989:** Tiger Foundation was established by Robertson and Tiger employees to support poverty-fighting organizations in New York City. It survived Tiger's 2000 fund closure and became an important part of the Tiger network's civic identity ([Tiger Foundation](https://tigerfoundation.org/who-we-are)).
- **1996-1997:** Robertson and Josie Robertson founded the Robertson Foundation in 1996; the Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation was established in 1997 with an initial gift from Robertson ([Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/); [Robertson Foundation](https://www.robertsonfoundation.org/)).
- **1998-2000:** Tiger's strategy suffered in the market regime dominated by technology, internet, telecom, and momentum stocks. Washington Post reported that investors withdrew $7.7 billion after August 1998, that value holdings fell sharply, and that Robertson planned to distribute most of the $6.5 billion remaining in March 2000 ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/)).
- **2000:** Robertson closed Tiger's outside hedge funds and returned capital. The mirrored investor letter presents his own explanation: Tiger's long/short, quality-over-momentum approach had stopped working in an irrational market, and the remaining assets no longer justified managing external capital under those conditions ([TurtleTrader mirror](https://www.turtletrader.com/tiger-closing/)).
- **2000s-2010s:** After returning outside capital, Robertson increasingly used Tiger to manage family capital and seed outside managers. Columbia's Graham & Doddsville interview shows him still thinking actively about manager selection, long/short stocks, analyst quality, and where market "pitching" was weakest ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
- **2010:** The New York State Tax Appeals Tribunal held for Julian and Josephine Robertson in a New York City statutory-residency dispute for tax year 2000. The case turned on whether Robertson spent more than 183 days in New York City, not on investment misconduct ([New York Tax Appeals Tribunal, 2010](https://www.dta.ny.gov/pdf/archive/decisions/822004.dec.pdf)).
- **2022:** Robertson died on August 23, 2022. UNC, family-foundation, and obituary sources emphasize both the Tiger record and the philanthropic institutions that survived him ([UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/); [Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/)).
- **2024-2025:** Posthumous Tiger Management news centered on the continuing seeding/family-office platform, including reported litigation over Hound Partners' seeding economics and a broader "Tiger 3.0" evolution under later leadership. This is legacy/platform evidence, not evidence of Robertson's personal investing record ([Business Insider, 2024](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-tiger-mamangement-hound-partners-lawsuits-2024-11); [Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiger-management-since-julian-robertson-death-tiger-cubs-investing-2025-7)).

## Vehicles & Structure

**Tiger Management hedge funds.** The canonical Robertson vehicle was the Tiger Management hedge-fund complex, a private long/short partnership structure that could buy, short, use leverage, trade currencies, and take global positions. Washington Post's 2000 closeout article referred to seven funds being closed and the remaining $6.5 billion being distributed to investment partners ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/)). The investor-letter mirror says Tiger's gains from May 1980 through August 1998 were generated across stocks, currencies, derivatives, and futures, but the opened public record does not supply full audited partnership statements ([TurtleTrader mirror](https://www.turtletrader.com/tiger-closing/)).

**Analyst platform and "Tiger Cubs."** Tiger was also a talent machine. UNC describes Robertson as a mentor whose former analysts and managers became a major hedge-fund network, and Institutional Investor framed the "cubs" as both alumni and managers later seeded by Robertson ([UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/); [Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgdofw34j872g6v083k/portfolio/tiger-tiger-burning-bright)). Later task files should verify individual Tiger Cubs one by one rather than import long unsourced lists. The best-known names commonly include Lee Ainslie of Maverick, Stephen Mandel of Lone Pine, Andreas Halvorsen of Viking, Philippe Laffont of Coatue, John Griffin of Blue Ridge, and Chase Coleman of Tiger Global, but this A-profile uses that list only as orientation pending task-specific source checks.

**Post-2000 Tiger Management.** After returning outside capital, Tiger did not disappear. FIA's Hall of Fame page says Robertson maintained Tiger to manage his own investments and seed investment hedge funds ([FIA Hall of Fame](https://www.fia.org/hall-fame/articles/julian-h-robertson-jr)). SEC and 13F records show Tiger Management L.L.C. as a public filer after the original fund era; for example, its August 2022 13F filing identifies Tiger Management L.L.C. with CIK 0001027451 and a business address at 101 Park Avenue ([SEC 13F filing detail, 2022](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957422005008/0000919574-22-005008-index.html)). Aggregated 13F data show the later public-equity footprint became far smaller and should not be confused with the 1998 hedge-fund peak ([13f.info - Tiger Management L.L.C.](https://13f.info/manager/0001027451-tiger-management-l-l-c)).

**Adviser-registration footprint.** Tiger Management Advisors L.L.C. appears in the SEC/IAPD system under CRD 159252 and SEC number 801-72867; the current IAPD summary says the adviser is not currently registered and is not filing reports with the SEC or a state, while a 2017 Form ADV PDF is still available as a historical regulatory source ([IAPD summary](https://adviserinfo.sec.gov/firm/summary/159252); [Tiger Management Advisors Form ADV, 2017](https://reports.adviserinfo.sec.gov/reports/ADV/159252/PDF/159252.pdf)). The right interpretation is historical footprint, not a live AUM number for Robertson's original Tiger.

**Philanthropic vehicles.** Robertson's capital also lived on through philanthropy. Tiger Foundation was founded in 1989; the Robertson Foundation in 1996; the Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation in 1997; and the Robertson Scholars Leadership Program linked Duke and UNC through a major leadership-scholarship gift ([Tiger Foundation](https://tigerfoundation.org/who-we-are); [Robertson Foundation](https://www.robertsonfoundation.org/); [Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/); [Robertson Scholars](https://robertsonscholars.org/about-us/our-benefactor/)). These vehicles matter for the Canon because Robertson turned investment profits into durable institutions and because some post-2000 Tiger economics reportedly support foundation activity.

## Track Record Detail & Caveats

Robertson's headline record is spectacular and only partly transparent from public sources. The strongest contemporaneous open source in this run is Washington Post's March 30, 2000 report: it says Tiger began with $8.8 million, reached $21 billion, and generated nearly 32% after-fee returns for the 18 years beginning in 1980 ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/)). The mirrored Robertson letter gives a similar but not official-source claim: $8.8 million at May 1980 start, $21 billion by August 1998, and 31.7% after fees through that point ([TurtleTrader mirror](https://www.turtletrader.com/tiger-closing/)). Family and industry sources typically cite a peak above $23 billion, which may reflect capital definitions, timing, leverage, or different fund aggregates ([Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/); [FIA Hall of Fame](https://www.fia.org/hall-fame/articles/julian-h-robertson-jr)).

For the full life of the outside-capital funds, Institutional Investor reports a 25% net annualized return from 1980 to 2000 after the late drawdown ([Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgdofw34j872g6v083k/portfolio/tiger-tiger-burning-bright)). That lower full-period number is probably the cleaner headline for comparing Robertson with other long-lived hedge-fund investors, because it includes the 1998-2000 stress that ended the fund. The higher 31.7%-32% figure is still important, but it describes the run through August 1998, before the final decline and redemptions.

The late-period caveat is not cosmetic. Washington Post reported that investors withdrew $7.7 billion after August 1998 and that Robertson's value holdings were down about 30% in the new market regime ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/)). It also reported a large U.S. Airways holding and size-related liquidity limits. Robertson's own letter framed the issue as an irrational market in which business quality and valuation had been overwhelmed by technology, internet, telecom, and momentum psychology ([TurtleTrader mirror](https://www.turtletrader.com/tiger-closing/)). The episode is central to Robertson's profile: he was right about the technology bubble's excess, but he was too early and too big to keep outside capital through the interim.

No opened source in this run supplied audited monthly returns, volatility, gross exposure, leverage, benchmark-relative returns, fees, side-pocket treatment, fund-by-fund dispersion, or investor capital-account outcomes. Future C/D/H tasks should not treat any single CAGR as "verified" without partnership letters or audited statements. The responsible shorthand is: Tiger's public record is a reported elite hedge-fund record, strongly triangulated by contemporaneous press and later institutional sources, but not fully auditable from open web sources.

## Why Robertson Matters

Robertson matters first because he proved that a fundamental long/short equity fund could become an institutional-scale business without becoming a conventional mutual fund. Tiger's method was simple to state but hard to execute: own the best businesses the research team could identify, short the worst or most overvalued, and do the work globally. In Columbia's 2012 Graham & Doddsville interview, Robertson still described long/short stock selection as the best way to manage money, with the caveat that investors should look where competition is weakest rather than only where markets are most crowded ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).

Second, Tiger was a culture as much as a fund. Robertson's great institutional invention may have been the analyst environment: young, competitive, generalist, globally curious, and expected to defend ideas under pressure. UNC's obituary and Institutional Investor both emphasize the Tiger Cubs because the alumni and seed network became a compounding institution after the original funds closed ([UNC Kenan-Flagler](https://www.kenan-flagler.unc.edu/news/remembering-a-hedge-fund-pioneer/); [Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgdofw34j872g6v083k/portfolio/tiger-tiger-burning-bright)).

Third, Robertson is a clean case study in capacity. Tiger's demise did not come from fraud or a single hidden blowup in the opened sources; it came from a combination of market regime, position size, client redemptions, and the difficulty of moving billions through a strategy that depended on discriminating stock selection. A smaller manager might have waited longer. A less stubborn manager might have chased technology. Robertson instead chose to close and return outside capital ([Washington Post, 2000](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/)).

Fourth, Robertson expanded the meaning of "investment legacy." He did not merely keep managing a family account after 2000; he seeded other managers, embedded capital into philanthropy, and left durable education, poverty, environment, and medical-research institutions. That makes him a Canon investor whose influence is measured through returns, people, and institutions ([Tiger Foundation](https://tigerfoundation.org/who-we-are); [Robertson Scholars](https://robertsonscholars.org/about-us/our-benefactor/); [Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation](https://bjrff.org/about-us/)).

The negative lessons are just as important. Robertson's profile warns that being directionally right can still fail if timing, liquidity, leverage, investor patience, and position sizing are wrong. It also warns against romanticizing the Tiger model: its alumni network has produced great funds, but later Tiger-related firms have also suffered cycles, crowding, legal disputes, and high public scrutiny. Later tasks should therefore analyze the system, not just the legend.
