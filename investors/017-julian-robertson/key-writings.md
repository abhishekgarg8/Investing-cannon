# Julian Robertson - Key Writings

As of 2026-06-24T19:28:33Z. Julian Robertson was deceased as of this run, and no official Tiger Management archive of partnership letters was found. The source base is therefore thinner than for letter-writers such as Warren Buffett or Howard Marks: the best "works by" Robertson are a mirrored 2000 investor letter, long-form interviews, SEC filings, and philanthropy interviews. This file treats mirrors and transcript excerpts as useful but lower-confidence than official PDFs, SEC filings, court records, and contemporaneous newspaper coverage.

## Source Posture

Robertson did not leave a public corpus of annual letters, books, or memos comparable to Buffett, Munger, Marks, Bogle, or Fisher. The closest thing to a central text is the March 2000 Tiger Management closing letter, available in full through an [A Letter a Day mirror](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000), and corroborated in part by contemporaneous [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/) and [Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1) reporting. The best official interview source opened in this run is Columbia Business School's [Graham & Doddsville Issue XV](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf), which gives Robertson in his own voice on analysts, management quality, Apple/Google, shorts, capacity, and seeding.

The right way to read Robertson is therefore not as an author-theorist, but as a practitioner whose process appears in fragments: the closing letter explains when a great strategy becomes unfinanceable; the Columbia and Value Investor Insight interviews explain the stock-selection philosophy; the SEC filings show what active, concentrated ownership looked like in legal form; and the secondary profiles reconstruct the culture of Tiger and its alumni network. Any future Canon work should keep those categories separate.

## Works By Robertson / Tiger, Ranked

### 1. Tiger Management closing letter to investors, March 2000

**Source quality:** accessible full-text mirror, cross-checked against contemporaneous press. Use the [A Letter a Day mirror](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000) for the letter text; use the [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/) and [Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1) for independent contemporary confirmation of the closure, redemptions, remaining assets, and value-versus-tech framing.

**Central thesis:** Tiger's long/short value strategy had been enormously successful in rational markets, but by early 2000 the fund's combination of performance stress, investor withdrawals, enormous scale, and an irrational technology/momentum regime made continued outside-capital management imprudent.

**Key ideas:**

1. Robertson anchors the letter in stewardship, not bravado: Tiger started with $8.8 million in 1980 and had grown into a $21 billion organization before the final drawdown, but past compounding did not justify exposing remaining partners to a market he no longer understood.
2. The letter separates business quality from market popularity. Robertson still argues that companies earning high cash returns are good investments, even when the market temporarily rewards speculative stocks with little emphasis on earnings or price discipline.
3. It names the real killer as the combination of mark-to-market pain and redemptions. The market could remain irrational; limited partners did not have to remain patient.
4. It shows that "right but early" is not a full defense for a fund manager. A private fund has financing, liquidity, and client-confidence constraints that a personal account may not.
5. The closure decision is a rare example of process humility. Robertson does not present a heroic hold-the-line manifesto; he returns outside capital because the opportunity set and vehicle no longer fit each other.
6. The letter is also a primary source for the cultural myth of Tiger: buying the best and shorting the worst, trusting analyst work, and refusing to chase a mania solely to report short-term performance.

**Best passages to study:** the opening performance recap; the explanation of $7.7 billion in withdrawals; the contrast between value stocks and speculative technology; and the final fiduciary logic for closing the funds. The one-sentence takeaway is that a strategy can be intellectually sound but structurally impossible inside the wrong capital base.

**Caveat:** no official Tiger-hosted PDF or archive copy was found. Treat the mirror as a working text and keep the contemporaneous press nearby for verification.

### 2. Graham & Doddsville interview, Columbia Business School, Spring 2012

**Source quality:** official Columbia PDF, near-primary interview. Robertson appears in Issue XV under the theme "Looking for Competitive Spirit" in [Graham & Doddsville](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf).

**Central thesis:** Tiger's edge was people plus judgment. Robertson wanted analysts and managers who could find good management teams, assess real growth, compare long and short opportunities, and compete intensely without losing integrity.

**Key ideas:**

1. Management quality was the first filter. Robertson tells the students that if management looks unusually able and honest, the stock deserves serious work.
2. Growth mattered because good analysts could judge business trajectory. Robertson says his own concept of value moved from low absolute multiples toward relative valuation against likely growth.
3. The long/short method was a spread test: be long the best ideas and short the worst. A portfolio built this way should benefit from stock selection even if market direction is uncertain.
4. Analyst culture was apprenticeship by doing. The interview's stories of travel, fieldwork, and senior-junior pairing show why Tiger was a training machine, not merely a portfolio.
5. Shorting was harder by 2012 because hedge-fund competition, borrow costs, and market structure had changed; this is an important update to any romantic account of Tiger's 1980s and 1990s short book.
6. Manager seeding was an extension of the same people-judgment model. Competitiveness, honesty, and intellectual ability mattered more than a polished institutional wrapper.
7. Robertson's Apple/Google comments are useful not because the stock calls were timeless, but because they show how he weighed business model, leadership durability, cash, growth, and valuation in one conversation.

**Best sections to study:** the opening history of Tiger, the comments on hiring young analysts, the management-quality discussion, the analyst/seed-manager traits, and the capacity/competition comments. Read it before the quote file and the mental-models file; it is the cleanest single source for the operating system behind Tiger.

### 3. Value Investor Insight / SuperInvestor Insight excerpt, November 2006

**Source quality:** accessible PDF excerpt from Value Investor Insight/SuperInvestor Insight via [Tilson Funds](https://tilsonfunds.com/VII%26SIIexcerpts-11-06.pdf). It includes both Whitney Tilson's retrospective on Robertson versus Buffett and a Robertson interview beginning on the excerpted pages.

**Central thesis:** Robertson's definition of value evolved from low-multiple investing into growth-at-a-reasonable-price, and Tiger's supposedly low-quality 2000 portfolio deserves a more nuanced postmortem than the easy "he chased cheap junk" critique.

**Key ideas:**

1. The interviewer revisits an earlier criticism of Tiger's 2000 holdings and finds that a simple equal-weighted basket of disclosed Tiger names subsequently outperformed the comparable Berkshire basket and the S&P 500 in the examined period.
2. Robertson says value should be understood relative to expected growth, not only as a low P/E ratio. A faster-growing business at a higher multiple can be cheaper than a slow grower at a low multiple.
3. He emphasizes that good analysts earn their keep by judging growth rates and business position, not merely by sorting low multiples.
4. The piece explains why structure matters: Buffett's corporate vehicle could survive investor impatience in a way Tiger's limited-partnership capital could not.
5. Robertson discusses hedge funds as incentive-aligned partnerships, while also noting that the crowding of the industry made shorting and competition harder.
6. The Cleveland-Cliffs anecdote is a useful counterweight to the claim that Tiger was never activist; Robertson presents Tiger as willing to push governance change, but not in the fully public modern activist style.

**Best sections to study:** Tilson's "Here’s to You, Mr. Robertson" setup; the portfolio comparison table; the interview answer redefining value around growth; and the discussion of hedge-fund incentive alignment and shorting economics.

**Caveat:** this is an excerpted PDF, not a complete official archive of all Value Investor Insight material. It is still strong because the interview and tables are visible, dated, and attributable.

### 4. Financial Times interview excerpts, October 2009

**Source quality:** partial excerpt hosted by [MarketFolly](https://www.marketfolly.com/2009/10/julian-robertsons-interview-with.html), pointing back to FT video/interview material that was not fully opened in this run.

**Central thesis:** After closing outside funds, Robertson remained an active macro and equity thinker, but his process still looked like "find the manager, security, or business with the best edge" rather than buy a simple macro slogan.

**Key ideas:**

1. His gold comments distinguish between owning the metal and owning miners whose economics could improve as mining costs fall.
2. He was willing to express macro views through instruments such as rate/curve trades rather than only through common stocks.
3. The excerpt's long/short list shows a late-career mix of franchise-growth longs, macro shorts, and commodity views.
4. The piece also surfaces his long-running emphasis on avoiding large losses, though the exact phrase should be traced to an original venue before being quoted in future files.
5. It shows a post-2000 Robertson who was still publicly engaged but no longer bound by the same outside-capital redemption problem described in the 2000 letter.

**Best sections to study:** the gold-versus-gold-stocks exchange and the long/short list. Use this as a lead source for a later, better FT transcript hunt.

### 5. OneWire interview write-up, 2013

**Source quality:** Business Insider article summarizing a OneWire interview, with short direct quotations, at [Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-onewire-interview-2013-10).

**Central thesis:** Robertson's seeding model was a continuation of Tiger's hiring model: find smart, honest, competitive people, then give them room to compound.

**Key ideas:**

1. Talent selection is treated as the central skill, not an HR afterthought.
2. Robertson says the strongest hedge-fund managers are smart and honest, with competitiveness close behind.
3. He attributes Tiger Cubs' success partly to their own qualities rather than only to his mentorship.
4. The source is a compact way to connect Tiger's investing process to its post-2000 seeding platform.
5. It supplies a clean lead for future work on which Tiger Cubs were employees, which were seeded managers, and which were later descendants of descendants.

**Best sections to study:** the talent-selection paragraphs and the short quote on smart, honest, competitive managers.

### 6. Bloomberg / Business Insider interview excerpts, 2013-2014, plus Parable Insight 2016

**Source quality:** transcript-style excerpts and summaries, not complete official transcripts. Use the [2013 Business Insider Apple/Google transcript excerpt](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-criticizes-apple-2013-7), the [2014 Bloomberg panel write-up](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-bloomberg-panel-2014-9), and the [2016 Parable Insight note](https://parableinsight.com/2016/09/julian-robertson-talks-hedge-funds/) as secondary/near-primary fragments.

**Central thesis:** Late-career Robertson kept updating his views on competition, leadership, bubbles, and market structure; he did not reduce Tiger's philosophy to a fixed 1990s formula.

**Key ideas:**

1. The 2013 Apple/Google excerpt shows him changing his mind when he loses confidence in the durability of leadership and operating culture.
2. The same excerpt adds a compliance and reputation dimension: he says hedge funds generally try to stay within the rules, while acknowledging the SAC case as an industry concern.
3. The 2014 panel frames bond markets and equity flows as bubble risks, but still leaves room for great companies at reasonable prices.
4. Robertson says hedge funds had become harder because the competitor set was no longer bank trust departments and retail investors, but other hedge funds.
5. The 2016 note records his worry that low rates, short squeezes, and quantitative strategies made fundamental shorting more difficult.
6. Across the excerpts, his views on hedging are pragmatic: hedges matter most when markets go down, but a "hedge fund" label does not guarantee a fully hedged portfolio.

**Best sections to study:** the Apple/Google leadership reversal, the 2014 discussion of bond bubbles and hedge-fund competition, and the 2016 comments on short squeezes and quants.

**Caveat:** these are interview fragments. Do not use them as the only source for any major claim, and avoid quote-mining them beyond the visible text.

### 7. Philanthropy Roundtable interview, Fall 2016

**Source quality:** direct interview in [Philanthropy Magazine](https://www.philanthropyroundtable.org/magazine/fall-2016-interview-with-julian-robertson/).

**Central thesis:** Robertson applied the same people-and-results filter to philanthropy that he applied to investing: choose good managers, set expectations, measure outcomes, and encourage others to become responsible stewards.

**Key ideas:**

1. Tiger Foundation was designed to make employees responsible allocators of charitable capital, not just recipients of a corporate giving program.
2. Robertson explicitly connects investing and giving through management selection and expected results.
3. His comments on charter schools show an investment-style preference for measured outcomes and competition.
4. The interview clarifies why philanthropy is not a separate legacy footnote; it was part of Tiger's culture and later Tiger Cub network.
5. The source helps explain why posthumous Tiger Management and the Robertson family foundation appear together in later legal and business reporting.

**Best sections to study:** Tiger Foundation origins; the "good management" answer; and the passages on responsibility for gift outcomes.

### 8. Tiger Management / U.S. Airways Schedule 13D, August 1999

**Source quality:** primary SEC filing. Use the SEC [filing detail page](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/701345/000101144099000099/0001011440-99-000099-index.html) and [complete submission text](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/701345/000101144099000099/0001011440-99-000099.txt).

**Central thesis:** The filing shows Tiger as a concentrated, sometimes active owner, willing to disclose that market valuation did not reflect intrinsic value and that strategic alternatives could be explored to enhance shareholder value.

**Key ideas:**

1. Tiger, related entities, and Robertson disclosed beneficial ownership of 16,512,700 U.S. Airways shares, or 22.4% of the class, as of the filing.
2. The filing reports approximately $450.9 million of aggregate funds used to acquire the shares.
3. Item 4 states that Tiger believed the market valuation did not reflect the intrinsic value of the business.
4. The alternatives listed include merger, sale, recapitalization, open-market sale, or privately negotiated transactions.
5. The filing says Tiger supported current management and had no present plan to change the board, which makes it a softer form of activism than later proxy-fight activism.
6. As a writing, it is sterile legal prose, but it is invaluable evidence of how Tiger converted research conviction into ownership posture.

**Best sections to study:** Items 3, 4, and 5. Pair it with the D-file on mistakes because U.S. Airways later became a liquidity and concentration lesson, not merely an activism case study.

### 9. Charlie Rose interview, July 17, 1998

**Source quality:** official [Charlie Rose metadata page](https://charlierose.com/videos/1583) confirms the interview, date, and topic, but the transcript did not render in the opened page.

**Central thesis:** potentially important first-person source on Robertson during the Asian/Japanese crisis period.

**Key ideas recoverable in this run:** only metadata: Robertson, chairman of Tiger Management, discussed the Japanese economic crisis on July 17, 1998. Because no transcript text rendered, this should be treated as a high-priority future transcription target, not as evidence for specific claims.

**Best section to study:** transcript/video if a future agent can recover it through an archive, institutional subscription, or working video transcript.

## Best Works About Robertson / Tiger, Ranked

### 1. Daniel A. Strachman, *Julian Robertson: A Tiger in the Land of Bulls and Bears*

The best book-length secondary source identified in this run is Strachman's biography. The accessible [Wiley excerpt](https://catalogimages.wiley.com/images/db/pdf/0471323632.excerpt.pdf) opens with the copper trade and is unusually useful because it reconstructs Tiger's research workflow: reports, industry sources, field checks, conviction-building, and the discipline to stay with the trade until the Sumitomo/Hamanaka scandal exposed the imbalance. The excerpt is essential for the C-file on greatest trades and for understanding why "dirt-under-your-fingernails" research became part of the Tiger mythology. It is still a biography, not an audited trading record, so trade-size and P&L details should be cross-checked when possible.

### 2. Institutional Investor, "Tiger, Tiger, Burning Bright," 2010

This [Institutional Investor profile](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgdofw34j872g6v083k/portfolio/tiger-tiger-burning-bright) is the best compact secondary source for Tiger's full-period reputation, reporting a 25% net annualized 1980-2000 record, the $22 billion 1998 high, 70% asset shrinkage, and the scale of the Tiger Cubs legacy. It is less rich than Columbia on philosophy, but stronger for institutional memory and the alumni network.

### 3. Washington Post, "Robertson To Close Tiger Funds," March 30, 2000

The [Washington Post report](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2000/03/31/robertson-to-close-tiger-funds/bad674b9-96b1-4d95-9e42-bb361495abc7/) is indispensable because it was written at the event. It records the $6.5 billion remaining capital, $7.7 billion withdrawals, 16.5 million-share U.S. Airways stake, nearly 32% after-fee return through the first 18 years, and Robertson's market-rationality framing. It is the best external check against reading the closing letter too sympathetically.

### 4. Guardian, "Stumbling Tiger predicts hi-tech collapse," March 31, 2000

The [Guardian article](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1) corroborates the closing-letter themes while adding useful details: $16 billion value decline over 18 months, $7.7 billion withdrawn, a 22% U.S. Airways stake, and the plan to avoid dumping concentrated industrial holdings into the market. It also reports Tiger's 25% annual average over 20 years, making it a useful independent counterpart to Institutional Investor's later summary.

### 5. Institutional Investor obituary, 2022

Stephen Taub's [2022 obituary](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2bstms42qkeyqcqfe2r5s/culture/julian-robertson-jr-founding-father-of-the-modern-hedge-fund-industry-dies-at-age-90) is the best late-career institutional recap. It confirms death status, describes Robertson as a founding figure in the modern hedge-fund industry, reports 31.5% annualized flagship returns from 1980 to 2000, highlights the roughly 40% decline from end-1997 and 70% asset shrinkage, and quotes Tiger Cub testimony about his mentorship and competitive culture. Use it for legacy, not as a substitute for contemporaneous 2000 reporting.

### 6. Observer, "The Return of Julian Robertson," 2011

The [Observer piece](https://observer.com/2011/04/the-return-of-julian-robertson/) is not a deep investment source, but it is useful for reputational balance. It points back to BusinessWeek's critical 1996 profile and the subsequent libel suit/addendum. This is important because Robertson's story can easily become hagiographic. The piece reminds future agents to include temperament, internal culture, and public criticism alongside performance.

### 7. Business Insider and legal-current sources on Tiger after Robertson

The [2024 Business Insider Hound/Tiger litigation report](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-tiger-mamangement-hound-partners-lawsuits-2024-11), the 2025 New York court decision already mapped in the D-file, and the [2025 Business Insider Tiger 3.0 article](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiger-management-since-julian-robertson-death-tiger-cubs-investing-2025-7) are not key to Robertson's original writings, but they matter for current legal and platform context. They show that the post-2000 seeding model created long-lived economics, obligations, and governance questions that persisted after Robertson's death.

### 8. Institutional and foundation biographies

The UNC Kenan-Flagler obituary, Blanche & Julian Robertson Family Foundation bio, FIA Hall of Fame page, Tiger Foundation page, Robertson Foundation page, and Robertson Scholars page are best used for biographical and philanthropic facts. They are less analytical than the sources above, but they are useful guardrails for dates, education, philanthropy, and institutional legacy.

### 9. Practitioner blogs and quote roundups

MarketFolly, Parable Insight, Behind the Balance Sheet, The Investor's Podcast, 25iq, and similar sources can be useful lead generators. They should not carry the main evidentiary load unless they link to primary material or disclose a first-hand recollection that is labeled as such. In particular, quote roundups should be treated as attribution watchlists, not proof.

## Gaps And Source Risks

1. No official Tiger Management archive of investor letters, partnership reports, or internal research memos was found.
2. The March 2000 closing letter is accessible through mirrors and press corroboration, not through an original Tiger-hosted document.
3. The Charlie Rose 1998 interview is confirmed by metadata, but the transcript did not render; it should not be quoted until recovered.
4. FT, Opalesque, and some conference materials appear to exist only through partial excerpts or blocked pages in this run.
5. The full Strachman biography should be checked for chapter-level detail and page references before future files lean heavily on it.
6. Return figures remain reported, not independently audited from original Tiger statements: 31.5%-31.7% through different date ranges and 25% full-period net annualized figures should be date-labeled.
7. U.S. Airways primary filings are strong for ownership and purpose; they do not by themselves establish realized P&L.
8. Quote aggregators repeat several Robertson lines without original venues. Use the E-file's attribution watchlist before adding quotes to any future synthesis.

## Bottom Line

Robertson's key writings do not form a polished doctrine. They form a field record. The closing letter is the canonical text on strategy-versus-vehicle mismatch. The Columbia and Value Investor Insight interviews are the canonical texts on management quality, growth-adjusted value, analyst culture, and competitive talent. The U.S. Airways 13D shows Tiger's concentrated active-ownership posture in primary form. The best works about Robertson then explain what the first-person corpus cannot fully show: the training machine, the Tiger Cubs, the capacity problem, and the uncomfortable truth that even a brilliant strategy can fail its investors if liquidity, timing, and capital structure turn against it.
