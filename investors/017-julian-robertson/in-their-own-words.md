## Source posture and attribution rules

This file uses short, source-visible quote handles rather than long excerpts. Robertson left a thinner public corpus than Buffett, Soros, Marks, or Greenblatt: no official Tiger partnership-letter archive was located in this run, the 2000 closing letter is available through mirrors rather than a Tiger-hosted archive, and several television appearances survive as clips or article transcripts rather than full first-party transcripts. I therefore treat the 2012 Graham & Doddsville interview, the 2000 closing letter mirror cross-checked against contemporaneous press, and published interview transcripts as the core own-words record ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf); [A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000); [Guardian, 2000](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1)).

Robertson died on August 23, 2022, so "current" checks here focus on posthumous Tiger Management developments, not a living person's status. The most relevant legal update found in this run is the Hound Partners / Tiger Management seed-economics dispute: a September 29, 2025 New York decision granted Tiger's motion to dismiss Hound's declaratory-judgment claim and denied Hound's motion to dismiss Tiger's counterclaims; that dispute concerns a post-2000 seeding agreement, not Robertson-era fund performance ([Justia / New York Supreme Court, 2025](https://law.justia.com/cases/new-york/other-courts/2025/2025-ny-slip-op-33696-u.html); [Business Insider, 2024](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-tiger-mamangement-hound-partners-lawsuits-2024-11)).

## Quote index by theme

### Tiger's method: long, short, and where to fish

1. "50 best" - Robertson's test of the long book was not abstract cheapness; it was whether his best companies could beat the worst ones he could identify ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
2. "50 worst" - the short side was part of the same comparative ranking exercise, not a separate market-timing hobby ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
3. "go where" - his baseball analogy led to a practical mandate: find markets where competition is weak enough for stock-picking skill to matter ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
4. "bad management team" - his short template began with people and governance, then overvaluation and industry decline ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
5. "quality of people" - even in a valuation interview, the recurring first screen is management character and capability ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
6. "vigorous active market" - he thought the ideal hedge-fund environment was active enough for longs and shorts to work, without a one-way index surge overwhelming selection ([Business Insider / Bloomberg transcript, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-criticizes-apple-2013-7)).
7. "anchor windward" - in 2014 he still defended hedging as useful when asset-price bubbles made unhedged exposure fragile ([Business Insider / Bloomberg panel, 2014](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-bloomberg-panel-2014-9)).
8. "squeezing shorts" - by 2016 he saw crowding, quants, and short squeezes as making the old Tiger game harder ([Parable Insight / Bloomberg Surveillance notes, 2016](https://parableinsight.com/2016/09/julian-robertson-talks-hedge-funds/)).

### People, analysts, and manager selection

9. "good young people" - Robertson credited Tiger's rise partly to the decision to hire and develop unusually able young analysts ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
10. "decent and honest" - his first-management question was moral as well as analytical; community diligence was part of the research process ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
11. "psychological test" - the seeding machine tried to systematize judgment about character, not just read resumes and pitches ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
12. "smart" - in a OneWire interview, talent assessment starts with raw cognitive ability ([Business Insider / OneWire, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-onewire-interview-2013-10)).
13. "honest" - ability without integrity was not enough for Robertson's manager-selection template ([Business Insider / OneWire, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-onewire-interview-2013-10)).
14. "competitiveness" - he repeatedly framed investing talent through an athletic lens: the will to win mattered ([Business Insider / OneWire, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-onewire-interview-2013-10)).
15. "won't lose" - the phrase is terse, but it captures the temperament he admired: competitive people who hate losing often transfer that discipline across domains ([Business Insider / OneWire, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-onewire-interview-2013-10)).
16. "vicious competitor" - the Bloomberg transcript shows how literal his athletic filter could be when evaluating a Tiger Cub candidate ([Business Insider / Bloomberg transcript, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-criticizes-apple-2013-7)).
17. "too much talent" - late in life he worried that finance was absorbing people who might be more socially valuable in science or engineering ([Business Insider / Bloomberg transcript, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-criticizes-apple-2013-7)).

### Value, growth, and the 2000 closure

18. "real growth stocks" - Robertson described his own evolution away from mere low multiples once Tiger's analysts could underwrite earnings quality ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).
19. "stumbled badly" - the closing letter did not hide Tiger's late-period damage after August 1998 ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
20. "bear market" - Robertson framed the collapse as a value-stock bear market, not just a Tiger-specific embarrassment ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
21. "cash-on-cash returns" - even in retreat, he kept defending businesses with observable economic returns against pure momentum ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
22. "mouse clicks" - his critique of the technology bubble was aimed at a market that had stopped caring about earnings and price ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
23. "this, too, will pass" - he thought value would return, but not on a schedule he could safely underwrite for outside clients ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
24. "no advantage" - this was the key humility line: he had conviction about value, but no timing edge on when the regime would turn ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
25. "return all capital" - the conclusion was an investor-protection decision, even though the subsequent dot-com collapse partly vindicated his market view ([A Letter a Day closing-letter mirror, 2000/2023](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).
26. "pyramid destined" - contemporaneous press picked up the same bubble warning when reporting Tiger's closure ([Guardian, 2000](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1)).
27. "market which frankly" - the Guardian's account corroborates Robertson's admission that he did not understand the market he was being asked to fight ([Guardian, 2000](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1)).

### Later markets, technology, and macro caution

28. "better way" - by 2013, Robertson had cooled on Apple and preferred Google's leadership model ([Business Insider / Bloomberg transcript, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-criticizes-apple-2013-7)).
29. "competition is tougher" - he blamed weaker hedge-fund performance partly on industry success and crowding ([Business Insider / Bloomberg transcript, 2013](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-criticizes-apple-2013-7)).
30. "reasonable valued" - his 2014 Alibaba comment shows he was not anti-growth; price still had to be weighed against projected economics ([Business Insider / Bloomberg panel, 2014](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-bloomberg-panel-2014-9)).
31. "license to steal" - his old short-selling edge depended on an era before everyone knew the playbook ([Business Insider / Bloomberg panel, 2014](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-bloomberg-panel-2014-9)).
32. "came out of the blue" - he treated October 1987 as a reminder that even strong economies can suffer sudden market breaks ([Business Insider / Bloomberg panel, 2014](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-bloomberg-panel-2014-9)).
33. "quants" - by 2016, he treated quantitative crowding as one more reason fundamental shorts were harder ([Parable Insight / Bloomberg Surveillance notes, 2016](https://parableinsight.com/2016/09/julian-robertson-talks-hedge-funds/)).
34. "gold stocks" - in the 2009 FT interview excerpt, inflation worry translated into miners, not bullion, because business economics mattered even in macro hedges ([MarketFolly / FT interview excerpt, 2009](https://www.marketfolly.com/2009/10/julian-robertsons-interview-with.html)).
35. "psychological store" - his gold comments were skeptical of inert metal as an investment object, even when he shared inflation worries ([MarketFolly / FT interview excerpt, 2009](https://www.marketfolly.com/2009/10/julian-robertsons-interview-with.html)).

### Philanthropy and legacy

36. "enjoyed giving" - Robertson described philanthropy as continuous with his life, not a late-career conversion ([Philanthropy Roundtable, 2016](https://www.philanthropyroundtable.org/magazine/fall-2016-interview-with-julian-robertson/)).
37. "student body president" - his Robertson Scholars comments echo his investing filter: leadership and drive matter alongside grades ([Philanthropy Roundtable, 2016](https://www.philanthropyroundtable.org/magazine/fall-2016-interview-with-julian-robertson/)).
38. "leaders of tomorrow" - the scholarship program was explicitly aimed at leadership formation, not just financial aid ([Philanthropy Roundtable, 2016](https://www.philanthropyroundtable.org/magazine/fall-2016-interview-with-julian-robertson/)).
39. "not going to try" - on foundation succession, he rejected trying to control decisions from beyond the grave ([Philanthropy Roundtable, 2016](https://www.philanthropyroundtable.org/magazine/fall-2016-interview-with-julian-robertson/)).

## What the quotes reveal

Robertson's own words are less about a single formula than about a personnel system. He wanted great analysts, honest managers, competitive portfolio managers, and markets where those human advantages could still count. The long/short framework was the machine around that judgment: buy the best businesses led by capable people, short weak or overvalued companies, and keep looking for venues where the competition was not yet fully professionalized.

The 2000 letter adds the necessary humility. Robertson could be right that technology momentum was a bubble and still decide he had no timing advantage, no obligation to risk outside capital, and no easy way to fight a market he no longer understood. That combination - competitive conviction plus willingness to return capital when the game changed - is the central own-words lesson to carry into later Robertson synthesis work.
