# Julian Robertson - Greatest Trades

As of 2026-06-24T15:24:16Z, the public record supports one clearly documented Robertson "greatest trade" - Tiger's 1995-1996 copper short - and several important but less transparent situations. Tiger Management was a private hedge-fund complex, not a public mutual fund, so position-level P&L is usually reported by journalists, biographies, SEC snapshots, or Robertson interviews rather than by audited Tiger statements. Treat every dollar figure below as reported unless explicitly sourced to a regulatory filing.

## Evidence Caveat And Ranking

Robertson's trade file has to be more caveated than Buffett's or Lynch's. The original Tiger partnership letters and audited position ledgers were not found in opened public sources. The best trade-level evidence comes from Daniel Strachman's biography excerpt on copper, Robertson's 2000 closing letter, contemporaneous press around Tiger's closure, SEC 13F records after the outside-capital era, and later Robertson interviews. This file therefore ranks trades by a mix of direct P&L evidence, strategic importance, and source quality.

## 1. Short Copper During The Sumitomo/Hamanaka Squeeze, 1995-1996 - Best Documented Trade

**Context & dates.** In 1994-1996, copper prices looked too high to Tiger's commodity work despite weakening demand and normal mine production. The public catalyst arrived when Sumitomo disclosed losses tied to trader Yasuo Hamanaka, whose activity had helped distort the copper market. Strachman's excerpt says copper fell more than 30% by May 1996 and September futures traded around 87.80 cents by July ([Strachman/Wiley excerpt, 2004](https://catalogimages.wiley.com/images/db/pdf/0471323632.excerpt.pdf)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** Tiger's edge was old-fashioned field research: analysts spoke with producers, users, brokers, and market participants, then concluded that supply was exceeding demand while prices were being held up by something other than fundamentals. Robertson reportedly did not know the Sumitomo fraud in advance; the thesis was that copper was overvalued on normal supply-demand evidence, and the scandal later became the catalyst ([Strachman/Wiley excerpt, 2004](https://catalogimages.wiley.com/images/db/pdf/0471323632.excerpt.pdf)).

**Size & structure.** Strachman reports that Tiger had built more than $1 billion of copper exposure by June 1995. The exact instruments are not fully itemized in the excerpt, but the position was a short copper book using commodity-market exposure rather than a simple common-stock short ([Strachman/Wiley excerpt, 2004](https://catalogimages.wiley.com/images/db/pdf/0471323632.excerpt.pdf)).

**Entry, path, and drawdown.** The path was uncomfortable. Copper rose above $1.25 per pound in early 1995, squeezing many short sellers, while Tiger kept researching and added to the position as the story remained intact. The trade required holding through an adverse rally before Sumitomo's disclosure broke the market's support ([Strachman/Wiley excerpt, 2004](https://catalogimages.wiley.com/images/db/pdf/0471323632.excerpt.pdf)).

**Exit & P&L.** Strachman reports a one-day profit of about $300 million in late May 1996, probably the largest single profit day in Tiger's history. That figure is repeated in later podcast summaries, but it appears to trace back to Strachman, so it should be marked [single-source, biography-reported] rather than audited ([Strachman/Wiley excerpt, 2004](https://catalogimages.wiley.com/images/db/pdf/0471323632.excerpt.pdf); [The Investor's Podcast, 2025](https://www.theinvestorspodcast.com/episodes/the-rise-and-fall-of-julian-robertsons-tiger-fund/)).

**What it teaches.** This is the cleanest Robertson trade because it shows the full Tiger method: a simple story, field checks, willingness to be early, and adding when price action disagreed but facts did not. It also shows the danger: without Sumitomo's collapse, Tiger could have been squeezed for longer.

## 2. Short Subprime Credit Through CDS, 2007

**Context & dates.** After closing Tiger to outside investors in 2000, Robertson continued investing his own capital. Fortune's 2008 profile, quoted in Morgan Creek's 2015 letter, described Robertson owning credit default swaps on subprime debt as values fell in 2007 ([Morgan Creek, 2015](https://www.morgancreekcap.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/01/MCCM1Q2015MarketOutlook.pdf)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The trade reflected Robertson's broad macro concern that U.S. credit and housing risk were mispriced. Morgan Creek's excerpt says the subprime position was already paying off as subprime values plummeted, while the same Fortune material tied Robertson's 2007-2008 posture to a negative outlook on the U.S. economy and foreign financing of Treasuries ([Morgan Creek, 2015](https://www.morgancreekcap.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/01/MCCM1Q2015MarketOutlook.pdf)).

**Size & structure.** The open source identifies credit default swaps on subprime debt but does not provide notional exposure, counterparties, tranche details, premium paid, or collateral terms. Because this was personal/family capital after Tiger's outside funds closed, it should not be compared mechanically with Tiger's 1980-2000 partnership book.

**Entry, path, and drawdown.** The public excerpt does not provide entry dates or mark-to-market drawdown. The likely path was similar to other pre-crisis CDS shorts: negative carry before the market broke, then sharp gains as mortgage credit repriced. That path is an inference from instrument structure, not a Robertson-specific disclosed ledger.

**Exit & P&L.** Fortune, via Morgan Creek's excerpt, reported that Robertson earned 76.7% in 2007 managing his own money. The same excerpt says the result rivaled his best Tiger years. Because the figure came from returns provided by Robertson to Fortune and not an audited statement, mark it [self-reported / Fortune-reported] ([Morgan Creek, 2015](https://www.morgancreekcap.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/01/MCCM1Q2015MarketOutlook.pdf)).

**What it teaches.** The subprime trade shows Robertson still had a macro shorting instinct after Tiger: when the story was simple enough, he would use derivatives and not only common stocks. It also shows that his post-2000 record is partly personal-capital evidence, not limited-partner evidence.

## 3. Short Eurotunnel, Mid-1990s

**Context & dates.** A former sell-side analyst writing at Behind the Balance Sheet recounts pitching Tiger on Eurotunnel as an expensive equity likely to require more capital. The anecdote places the trade in the author's 1990s sell-side period, before Tiger's 2000 closure ([Behind the Balance Sheet, 2022](https://behindthebalancesheet.com/sundry/the-original-tiger-king/)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The thesis was balance-sheet and financing fragility: Eurotunnel's equity price did not reflect the need for a future injection of capital. The idea came through Tiger's broker/intelligence network, then James Lyall at Tiger was reportedly interested after a short call ([Behind the Balance Sheet, 2022](https://behindthebalancesheet.com/sundry/the-original-tiger-king/)).

**Size & structure.** The source says the analyst and Tiger sold about 3% of the company short and that the bet was "over $100m for sure." That is a practitioner memory, not a filing, so both the size and P&L are [single-source] ([Behind the Balance Sheet, 2022](https://behindthebalancesheet.com/sundry/the-original-tiger-king/)).

**Entry, path, and drawdown.** The open source does not supply entry price, borrow cost, interim squeeze, or exit date. It does say Tiger earned interest on the short-sale cash, which was more meaningful in that rate environment.

**Exit & P&L.** The source says Tiger made roughly 90% on the trade. No independent confirmation was found in this run, so the trade belongs below copper and subprime despite the high reported return ([Behind the Balance Sheet, 2022](https://behindthebalancesheet.com/sundry/the-original-tiger-king/)).

**What it teaches.** Eurotunnel is a classic Tiger short: a high-priced security, a flawed financing story, and fast conversion of outside research into a large position. It also shows the source problem with Robertson: some of the best trade anecdotes live in participant memory rather than primary Tiger documents.

## 4. Apple, Google, And The Post-2000 Quality-Growth Longs, 2009-2016

**Context & dates.** Robertson's 2012 Columbia interview said Apple and Google could continue to outperform, and a 2009 FT interview summary listed Google and Apple among his bullish long/short game answers ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf); [Market Folly / FT summary, 2009](https://www.marketfolly.com/2009/10/julian-robertsons-interview-with.html)). SEC 13F information tables show Tiger Management L.L.C. still held 409,310 Apple shares at December 31, 2015; Apple no longer appeared in the March 31, 2016 13F information table ([SEC 13F information table, Q4 2015](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957416011036/xslForm13F_X01/infotable.xml); [SEC 13F information table, Q1 2016](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957416013147/xslForm13F_X01/infotable.xml)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** Robertson's late-career shift was not cheap-stock nostalgia. In Columbia, he framed Apple and Google as exceptional businesses with strong products, growth, cash, and management quality. That matched the evolved Tiger philosophy: buy the best businesses when the market underestimates growth or quality ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).

**Size & structure.** Public 13F evidence is only a partial window because it omits shorts, derivatives, non-U.S. securities, and cash. The SEC Q2 2015 13F cover page shows Tiger Management L.L.C. reported $708.326 million of 13F holdings across 47 entries; the Q4 2015 information table later showed $775.181 million across 46 entries, including $43.084 million in Apple and $54.124 million in Alphabet share classes combined ([SEC 13F cover page, Q2 2015](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957415006266/xslForm13F_X01/primary_doc.xml); [SEC 13F information table, Q4 2015](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957416011036/xslForm13F_X01/infotable.xml)).

**Entry, path, and drawdown.** Entry dates and cost basis are not available from opened sources. The path was favorable for Apple and Alphabet over the 2009-2016 period, but any exact Tiger P&L would require the full quarterly 13F history, trades between filings, and any hedges.

**Exit & P&L.** By Q1 2016, Apple was absent from Tiger's 13F information table, while Alphabet Class C fell from 68,865 to 36,389 shares and Alphabet Class A remained 2,396 shares. No exact gain is public in the opened record, so this is a high-confidence thesis success but an unquantified P&L ([SEC 13F information table, Q4 2015](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957416011036/xslForm13F_X01/infotable.xml); [SEC 13F information table, Q1 2016](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1027451/000091957416013147/xslForm13F_X01/infotable.xml)).

**What it teaches.** Robertson was not permanently anti-technology; he was anti-price-insensitive technology. After the dot-com bubble, he could own dominant technology companies when business quality and valuation made sense.

## 5. WuXi PharmaTech Long, 2011-2015

**Context & dates.** WuXi PharmaTech appeared in Robertson/Tiger public discussions around 2011-2012, and Insider Monkey reported in 2012 that Tiger had pared both Google and WuXi. In 2015, WuXi agreed to a take-private transaction at $46 per ADS, or about $3.3 billion in aggregate cash consideration ([Insider Monkey, 2012](https://www.insidermonkey.com/blog/julian-robertson-eye-of-the-tiger-12879/); [WuXi press release, 2015](https://www.wuxiapptec.com/news/wuxi-news/3096)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** The fit with Robertson's framework is clear: a China-linked growth company in a market he believed had both frauds and fabulous stocks. Columbia's 2012 interview explicitly urged investors to go where the "pitching is the worst," naming Korea and China as fertile but risky markets ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf)).

**Size & structure.** The opened sources do not give Tiger's exact peak WuXi share count, cost basis, or hedge. Treat it as a public-equity long in the post-2000 Tiger Management L.L.C. portfolio, not an original Tiger hedge-fund-era position.

**Entry, path, and drawdown.** WuXi's path included normal China ADR and life-science outsourcing risk, then a take-private offer. The company press release says the $46 per ADS deal price represented a 16.5% premium to the April 29, 2015 NYSE close of $39.50 and 18.9%-20.1% premiums to 30- and 60-day VWAPs ([WuXi press release, 2015](https://www.wuxiapptec.com/news/wuxi-news/3096)). The SEC proxy confirms ADS holders would receive $46 less a $0.05 ADS cancellation fee if the merger closed ([SEC proxy, 2015](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1403132/000119312515340768/d86213dex99a1.htm)).

**Exit & P&L.** No opened source proves Tiger still held WuXi at the take-private close, so the deal should not be booked as realized Tiger P&L without 13F reconstruction. The better conclusion is that WuXi was an example of the kind of China growth long Robertson publicly liked, with a clear value-unlocking event later in 2015.

**What it teaches.** The trade shows Robertson's late-career willingness to fish in less efficient, non-U.S. growth markets while still requiring business quality. It also illustrates why 13F-only analysis can mislead: a visible name can be real, yet the actual entry and exit economics remain hidden.

## 6. Tiger Cubs And Seed Economics, 2000s-2020s - Not A Public Stock Trade, But A Major Capital-Allocation Win

**Context & dates.** After returning outside capital in 2000, Robertson used Tiger Management to seed and back external managers. Institutional Investor described the Tiger Cubs as a central part of his legacy, and Business Insider later reported that Tiger 2.0 became a prolific seeder after 2000 ([Institutional Investor, 2010](https://www.institutionalinvestor.com/article/2btgdofw34j872g6v083k/portfolio/tiger-tiger-burning-bright); [Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiger-management-since-julian-robertson-death-tiger-cubs-investing-2025-7)).

**Thesis & how they found it.** This was Robertson applying the same people edge to managers instead of stocks. Columbia's interview emphasized competitiveness and integrity as the variables he wanted in managers, and Business Insider reported that later Tiger still looks for competitive "winner DNA" with integrity ([Graham & Doddsville, 2012](https://business.columbia.edu/sites/default/files-efs/imce-uploads/Graham%20%26%20Doddsville%20-%20Issue%2015%20-%20Spring%202012.pdf); [Business Insider, 2025](https://www.businessinsider.com/tiger-management-since-julian-robertson-death-tiger-cubs-investing-2025-7)).

**Size & structure.** Structures varied by manager. The 2024 Hound litigation gives one concrete example: Business Insider reported that Hound was seeded with $15 million from Robertson in 2004 and later received another $8 million; the reworked agreement promised Tiger 12% of gross performance fees going forward ([Business Insider, 2024](https://www.businessinsider.com/julian-robertson-tiger-mamangement-hound-partners-lawsuits-2024-11)).

**Entry, path, and drawdown.** The path is multi-decade and mixed. It produced some famous firms, but seed economics also created contract, agency, and reputation risk, as the Hound dispute shows. Because many funds are private, exact Tiger seed-portfolio returns were not found.

**Exit & P&L.** No aggregate P&L is public in opened sources. This entry is included because it may be Robertson's largest post-2000 compounding engine, but it is not a clean public-markets trade and should be refreshed if Tiger seed economics become available.

**What it teaches.** Robertson's most portable edge may have been selecting people. For an individual investor, this is hard to copy directly, but it explains why Tiger's influence outlived Tiger's outside-capital funds.

## Trades Deliberately Not Ranked As Winners

**U.S. Airways.** Tiger's airline exposure was large enough to become a closure-era liquidity problem, not a greatest trade. Contemporary closure coverage reported that Robertson would isolate illiquid positions including a 22% U.S. Airways stake rather than dump them into the market ([Guardian, 2000](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1)).

**Short yen / macro currency book.** Tiger's yen exposure is also a mistake-file candidate, not a winner. This file leaves the exact yen reconstruction to the D-mistakes task because the best accessible evidence here points to late-period macro stress and liquidation pressure rather than a documented profitable trade ([Guardian, 2000](https://www.theguardian.com/money/2000/mar/31/personalfinancenews.business1)).

**Dot-com bubble underweight/shorts.** Robertson was ultimately right that the technology, internet, and telecom mania would collapse, but the outside-capital vehicle closed before Tiger could harvest the reversal. His 2000 letter says he could not justify subjecting investors to a market he did not understand, despite confidence that value would return ([Tiger Management closing letter mirror, 2000](https://aletteraday.substack.com/p/letter-78-julian-robertson-2000)).

## Cross-Trade Lessons

1. **The best Robertson trades began with a simple story.** Copper was over-supplied; Eurotunnel needed capital; subprime credit was too weak for the spread investors demanded; Apple/Google were better businesses than the valuation implied.
2. **Tiger's edge was evidence density.** The copper and Eurotunnel examples show Tiger converting field research and specialist networks into conviction.
3. **Robertson was willing to be early, but only if the story stayed intact.** Copper worked because the facts kept supporting the short despite adverse price action; the dot-com underweight failed as an outside-capital trade because the vehicle could not survive the timing mismatch.
4. **P&L transparency is the main unresolved problem.** Copper's $300 million day and Eurotunnel's 90% return are powerful, but they are not audited public figures. Future work should search for original Tiger letters and contemporaneous position reports before upgrading any figure from reported to verified.
5. **His greatest "trade" may have been people.** The Tiger Cub and seed network compounded Robertson's judgment beyond the life of the original fund, but it belongs in a separate mental-model and synthesis file because it is not a standard public-security trade.
