# Cross-investor themes (appended after each cohort)
Sections to grow over time: Edge taxonomy · Position sizing across the masters · Sell disciplines compared · Surviving drawdowns · Luck vs. skill · Temperament patterns · Where the greats disagree

## 2026-06-21 - Cohort 01: Buffett to Templeton

**Edge taxonomy:** The first cohort splits into six early edge families: quality/permanent-capital compounding (Buffett, Munger), statistical and special-situation value (Graham), broad public-equity GARP/scuttlebutt (Lynch), reflexive/concentrated macro (Soros, Druckenmiller), systematic small-edge infrastructure (Simons), and credit/distress/global-pessimism value (Marks, Klarman, Templeton). See [cohort-01](cohort-01.md).

**Position sizing across the masters:** Size is not a personality trait. It follows the unit of edge: single-business knowledge can justify concentration, broad cheapness needs baskets, credit and claims need downside-weighted sizing, macro needs explicit wrong-way loss budgets, and quant needs expected value after cost, impact, correlation, financing, and capacity.

**Sell disciplines compared:** The sell rule must fit thesis half-life. Buffett/Munger sell slowly when a durable business remains intact; Graham and Templeton prefer predefined value or time rules; Lynch sells when the company story/category changes; Soros/Druckenmiller reverse when the macro feedback loop breaks; Simons exits when forecast/risk decays; Marks/Klarman sell when forward risk-adjusted return or downside protection no longer compensates.

**Surviving drawdowns:** Anti-ruin is the shared skeleton. The cohort's strongest common warning is that insight is not enough if the vehicle cannot survive the path: margin debt, impatient clients, scale, product mismatch, policy delay, legal structure, and public reputation can all turn a correct idea into a bad investment.

**Luck vs. skill:** Skill shows up as process coherence before outcomes: rules, source discipline, vehicle design, sizing logic, sell criteria, and post-mortems. Luck shows up as era, country, data frontier, opportunity set, first successes, and the capital structure that lets a manager keep playing.

**Temperament patterns:** Patience is not one trait. Buffett/Munger patience is business ownership, Graham patience is rule-based reversion, Lynch patience is story monitoring, Klarman/Marks patience is optionality under stress, Templeton patience is global pessimism, Soros/Druckenmiller patience is waiting for the fat pitch but cutting fast, and Simons patience is institutional research discipline.

**Where the greats disagree:** Cohort 01's productive contradictions are concentration versus baskets, quality versus cheapness, human judgment versus systematic models, long holding periods versus fast reversal, public accessibility versus institutional privilege, and cash as optionality versus cash as opportunity cost.

## 2026-06-26 - Cohort 02: Fisher to Paul Tudor Jones

**Edge taxonomy:** Cohort 02 widens the Canon into eight additional edge families: qualitative growth/scuttlebutt (Fisher), causal macro/risk-balanced portfolio engineering (Dalio), endowment governance and patient value (Keynes), discretionary tape/trend speculation (Livermore), low-cost beta capture and fiduciary product design (Bogle), special-situation value plus quality-value formulas (Greenblatt), people-driven fundamental long/short equity (Robertson), variant-perception trading (Steinhardt), crisis credit/policy optionality (Tepper), and liquid convex macro trading (Jones). See [cohort-02](cohort-02.md).

**Position sizing across the masters:** Cohort 02 makes sizing a statement about thesis half-life and evidence quality. Fisher and Robertson can concentrate when qualitative evidence is unusually strong; Dalio diversifies by independent return driver; Keynes sizes around patient governance; Bogle largely refuses active sizing for ordinary investors; Greenblatt and Tepper size around structure, catalyst, downside, and capacity; Steinhardt and Jones require exit capacity before size; Livermore shows what happens when size outruns enforcement.

**Sell disciplines compared:** The cohort's sell rules split by clock speed. Fisher and Keynes sell slowly unless business or mandate evidence changes; Bogle sells mainly through rebalancing and life-cycle risk control; Greenblatt exits as value gaps close or catalysts fail; Robertson de-risks when path, shorts, or clients overpower fundamentals; Steinhardt re-underwrites constantly as consensus changes; Tepper sells when recovery rights or policy path deteriorate; Jones and Livermore cut or reverse when price invalidates the thesis.

**Surviving drawdowns:** The main survival lesson is structural: do not run a thesis in a vehicle that cannot bear its path. Tiger's late-1990s closure, Livermore's bankruptcies, Dalio's model misses, Jones's 1979 cotton loss and later Tudor reset, and Steinhardt's 1994 bond episode all show that being broadly right is insufficient if leverage, liquidity, client flows, timing, or legal constraints force the issue first.

**Luck vs. skill:** Skill appears as a well-matched system: Fisher's research protocol, Dalio's explicit models, Keynes's governance adaptation, Bogle's cost arithmetic, Greenblatt's document-driven event work, Robertson's analyst culture, Tepper's legal-capital-structure judgment, and Jones's risk-first trading. Luck appears in era, market structure, institutional access, central-bank or government behavior, technology/product cycles, and whether early wins created patient capital.

**Temperament patterns:** This cohort separates patience from inactivity. Fisher's patience is business-underwriting patience; Keynes's is governance patience; Bogle's is behavioral restraint; Greenblatt's is catalyst patience; Robertson's is fundamental conviction under client pressure; Tepper's is panic composure; Jones's is waiting for rare liquid asymmetry; Livermore is the warning that patience without enforced stops becomes hope.

**Where the greats disagree:** Cohort 02's productive contradictions are Fisher versus Bogle on active company research, Dalio versus Keynes on macro machines versus patient enterprise ownership, Livermore/Jones versus Bogle on trading versus abstention, Greenblatt/Tepper versus simple indexing on exploitable complexity, and Steinhardt versus Fisher on fast variant perception versus long-duration business compounding.

**Boundary risk:** Cohort 02 adds boundary control as a core Canon theme. Scuttlebutt needs information hygiene; old operator markets are not modern templates; variant perception can cross legal or cultural lines; macro platforms need compliance and governance as much as stop-loss discipline; and legal process can be the investment case itself in crisis credit. Future files should treat legal, cultural, environmental, and reputational controls as part of investment process, not postscript.

## 2026-06-30 - Cohort 03: Icahn to Burry

**Edge taxonomy:** Cohort 03 adds expression-driven edges: shareholder activism and governance optionality (Icahn), quantitative relationship arbitrage and Kelly sizing (Thorp), austere Graham-and-Dodd balance-sheet baskets (Schloss), flexible expectations-based public equity value (Miller), low-P/E plus dividend/growth mutual-fund value (Neff), endowment governance and external-manager access (Swensen), structural fixed-income alpha (Gross), commodity/country-cycle macro field research (Rogers), discretionary global macro platform risk (Kovner), and forensic primary-document contrarianism (Burry). See [cohort-03](cohort-03.md).

**Position sizing across the masters:** The cohort makes sizing a question of expression fit. Icahn sizes around the governance lever; Thorp around proven edge and fractional Kelly; Schloss around many uncertain cheap stocks; Miller around expectation gaps but with hidden-factor caution; Neff around public-fund low-expectations arithmetic; Swensen around institutional policy and liquidity; Gross around decomposed bond risks; Rogers around rare macro fat pitches; Kovner around loss-first scenario confirmation; Burry around document evidence plus instrument asymmetry.

**Sell disciplines compared:** Sell rules split by what is supposed to close the gap. Icahn exits after governance action or failed leverage; Thorp exits when the spread or plumbing fails; Schloss sells at conservative value; Miller sells when the expectations gap closes or factor exposure is revealed; Neff sells into recognition; Swensen rebalances through governance; Gross de-risks when fixed-income risk premia or wrapper flows no longer compensate; Rogers exits when timing or wrapper risk overwhelms the thesis; Kovner cuts when confused; Burry sells when value, catalyst, or mandate survival changes.

**Surviving drawdowns:** Cohort 03 shows that "correct" is not enough. Subprime nearly broke Burry's fund before paying, PIMCO's scale turned factor losses into flow crises, Yale's FY2009 drawdown tested illiquidity governance, Miller's 2007-2008 losses revealed common hidden drivers, Kovner's 1994 loss forced capital return, and Icahn's IEP episode showed that the activist's own financing can become the risk.

**Luck vs. skill:** Skill appears as repeatable expression design: Icahn's governance mechanics, Thorp's measured edge, Schloss's low-overhead discipline, Miller's expectation analysis, Neff's low-P/E underwriting, Swensen's institution-building, Gross's bond-risk decomposition, Rogers's field research, Kovner's scenario/risk process, and Burry's document work. Luck appears in era, market structure, legal access, data scarcity, interest-rate regimes, commodity cycles, manager access, dealer relationships, and early capital.

**Temperament patterns:** Cohort 03 separates patience from passivity. Schloss and Neff wait for low expectations to normalize; Swensen waits because the institution has a mission horizon; Gross waits only where yield/structure pays for risk; Rogers waits for rare macro alignment; Kovner waits for regime evidence and then cuts fast; Burry waits through social pain only if the instrument and mandate can survive; Icahn refuses to wait passively when governance can be forced.

**Where the greats disagree:** The productive contradictions are control versus anonymity, quantified proof versus field judgment, balance-sheet cheapness versus future optionality, diversification versus concentration, public accessibility versus institutional privilege, and forecast humility versus bold macro expression.

**Expression design:** This cohort adds a new master theme: an idea is not an investment until its expression is specified. Common stock, options, CDS, futures, bonds, limited partnerships, public mutual funds, endowments, commodity indexes, and activist filings each carry different path risks. Future Canon files should treat instrument, wrapper, client base, liquidity, legal boundary, and reporting burden as part of the thesis.
