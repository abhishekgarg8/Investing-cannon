# Cohort 01 Synthesis - Buffett to Templeton

As of 2026-06-21, this cohort covers the first ten completed investor folders: Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger, Benjamin Graham, Peter Lynch, George Soros, Stanley Druckenmiller, Jim Simons, Howard Marks, Seth Klarman, and John Templeton. The evidence base is the completed Canon work in those folders, especially the H-synthesis files and the G-mental-models files where available. One limitation matters: John Templeton's G-mental-models file was still a fresh claim and absent from `main` during this run, so Templeton is compared from his A-F outputs and H-synthesis rather than a completed G file ([Templeton synthesis](../investors/010-john-templeton/synthesis.md)).

## Executive Read

Cohort 01 does not produce one master style. It produces a hierarchy of durable investing problems. The first is **where the edge comes from**: price versus value for Graham, Buffett, Munger, Marks, Klarman, and Templeton; consumer/company-level scuttlebutt for Lynch; policy-regime instability for Soros and Druckenmiller; and statistical infrastructure for Simons. The second is **what vehicle lets the edge survive**: Berkshire's permanent capital and float, Baupost's private partnership, Oaktree's locked credit vehicles, Magellan's broad mutual-fund machinery, Quantum/Duquesne's flexible macro mandate, Renaissance's employee-aligned capacity cap, and Templeton Growth's unusually patient public-fund governance. The third is **what kills the model**: leverage, scale, forced clients, stale opportunity sets, attribution myths, and a failure to sell when the reason for owning has changed.

The strongest common principle is not contrarianism by itself. All ten investors require a reason why the current price is wrong and why the investor can hold, trade, or systematically exploit that error better than the marginal participant. Graham's cheap statistical baskets, Buffett and Munger's quality businesses, Marks and Klarman's distressed or complex claims, Lynch's researched growth stories, Soros and Druckenmiller's macro inflections, Simons's small-edge signals, and Templeton's global maximum-pessimism baskets are different answers to the same question: **what does the market underwrite badly, and why can this operator survive the path?** ([Graham synthesis](../investors/003-benjamin-graham/synthesis.md); [Buffett synthesis](../investors/001-warren-buffett/synthesis.md); [Simons synthesis](../investors/007-jim-simons/synthesis.md)).

The second common principle is anti-ruin. Even the boldest investors in the cohort built rules around survivability. Buffett holds enormous liquidity and treats reputation as a capital asset; Munger's concentration is conditioned on competence and no forced leverage; Graham's late method is diversified, rule-bound, and anti-margin; Marks and Klarman make client structure part of risk control; Simons caps capacity and models costs; and Druckenmiller's most repeated lesson is to cut fast when the evidence breaks ([Buffett mental models](../investors/001-warren-buffett/mental-models.md); [Munger mental models](../investors/002-charlie-munger/mental-models.md); [Druckenmiller mental models](../investors/006-stanley-druckenmiller/mental-models.md)).

## Convergent Principles

1. **Vehicle design is strategy.** Buffett is the cleanest example: Berkshire's float, tax deferral, reputation, operating earnings, and patient shareholders let him act in ways a redemption-prone fund could not ([Buffett synthesis](../investors/001-warren-buffett/synthesis.md)). But the principle repeats. Klarman's private client base enables illiquidity and cash optionality; Marks's Oaktree vehicles must match credit duration; Simons's Medallion edge depends on capacity limits and employee economics; Templeton's public mutual fund required governance willing to endure long stretches of underperformance ([Klarman synthesis](../investors/009-seth-klarman/synthesis.md); [Marks synthesis](../investors/008-howard-marks/synthesis.md); [Simons synthesis](../investors/007-jim-simons/synthesis.md); [Templeton synthesis](../investors/010-john-templeton/synthesis.md)).

2. **Price matters, but the unit of analysis differs.** Graham starts with balance sheet value and statistical cheapness; Buffett and Munger move toward business quality as the margin of safety; Lynch asks whether a company story, category, and valuation still cohere; Marks and Klarman price downside, illiquidity, and process risk; Soros and Druckenmiller price regime fragility and timing; Simons prices tiny forecast edges after costs; Templeton prices global pessimism across baskets ([Graham synthesis](../investors/003-benjamin-graham/synthesis.md); [Lynch synthesis](../investors/004-peter-lynch/synthesis.md); [Soros synthesis](../investors/005-george-soros/synthesis.md); [Templeton synthesis](../investors/010-john-templeton/synthesis.md)).

3. **The best investors convert temperament into rules.** "Patience" means different things: Buffett's years of inactivity, Graham's predefined sale targets, Lynch's tolerance for messy portfolios, Klarman's cash, Marks's cycle posture, Soros's willingness to press or reverse, and Druckenmiller's refusal to let ego defend a losing thesis. Temperament without a rule becomes personality; temperament with a rule becomes process.

4. **Scale is a tax on almost every edge.** Berkshire's size makes small bargains irrelevant; Magellan's scale required broad ownership; Baupost's flexibility faces opportunity-cost scrutiny at tens of billions; Oaktree's credit franchise must source large enough dislocations; Renaissance separated Medallion from larger outside products; and Druckenmiller returned outside capital when the mandate and emotional cost no longer fit ([Buffett synthesis](../investors/001-warren-buffett/synthesis.md); [Lynch synthesis](../investors/004-peter-lynch/synthesis.md); [Klarman synthesis](../investors/009-seth-klarman/synthesis.md); [Simons mental models](../investors/007-jim-simons/mental-models.md); [Druckenmiller synthesis](../investors/006-stanley-druckenmiller/synthesis.md)).

5. **Copying holdings is usually copying residue.** The cohort repeatedly warns against 13F imitation and guru mimicry. Buffett, Munger, Klarman, Soros, Druckenmiller, and Simons all have visible public artifacts that omit the real risk system: float, private claims, cash, shorts, derivatives, timing, tax structure, cost models, and intra-quarter changes. The transferable object is the decision architecture, not the filing.

## Genuine Disagreements

The cohort's most useful disagreements are not superficial style differences. They are incompatible operating answers that can both be right under the correct vehicle and regime.

**Concentration versus baskets.** Buffett, Munger, Soros, and Druckenmiller accept or seek concentration when evidence and structure justify it. Graham, Lynch, Templeton, Marks, Klarman, and Simons usually diversify at the instrument level, but for different reasons: statistical law of large numbers, liquidity, public-fund breadth, credit loss control, downside uncertainty, or model portfolio risk. Both camps got rich because the right question is not "concentrate or diversify?" It is "where is the edge measured?" A single dominant business insight may deserve size; a broad factor, distress pool, or small statistical edge needs a portfolio.

**Human judgment versus system discipline.** Buffett, Munger, Graham, Lynch, Marks, Klarman, Templeton, Soros, and Druckenmiller are judgment-led, though some use rules heavily. Simons is the outlier: normal sell decisions are model-driven, and discretionary override is reserved for risks the model may not encode. The contradiction is real. Human investors can see institutional, legal, behavioral, and narrative variables machines may miss; systematic investors can avoid ego, inconsistency, and untested story-fitting. Both got rich because each built controls around its own failure mode ([Simons mental models](../investors/007-jim-simons/mental-models.md); [Munger mental models](../investors/002-charlie-munger/mental-models.md)).

**Wait longer versus cut faster.** Buffett, Munger, Graham, Marks, Klarman, and Templeton generally want patience after purchase. Soros and Druckenmiller often want speed when the thesis breaks. Lynch sits in between: let improving stories run, but sell when the category thesis changes. The contradiction resolves through thesis type. A durable business or distressed claim may need time; a macro regime trade with policy/timing risk can become wrong very quickly.

**Quality versus cheapness.** Graham and Templeton preserve cheapness and diversification as core safeguards. Buffett and Munger show the adaptation: a higher-quality business can be safer than a statistically cheap but mediocre asset if the holding period is long. Klarman and Marks mediate the debate by asking whether downside protection, legal priority, or a catalyst compensates for uncertainty. This disagreement is central to the Canon because it prevents "value investing" from becoming one frozen recipe.

**Public accessibility versus institutional privilege.** Lynch and Templeton are the cohort's most public-fund accessible models. Graham's late basket rules are also unusually relevant to small investors. Buffett, Munger, Marks, Klarman, Soros, Druckenmiller, and Simons all contain structural privileges that most readers cannot copy: float, board access, private credit sourcing, locked capital, macro execution, family-office flexibility, scientific infrastructure, or employee-only economics.

## Sizing & Sell-Discipline Comparison

| Investor | Sizing logic | Sell / de-risk discipline |
|---|---|---|
| Warren Buffett | Concentrate when business, price, management, liquidity, and structure are understood; otherwise index or diversify. Berkshire-level cash and operating earnings make concentration survivable ([Buffett mental models](../investors/001-warren-buffett/mental-models.md)). | Controlled businesses are rarely sold; public securities can be trimmed or sold when thesis, moat, opportunity cost, or portfolio risk changes. Reputation/liability risk can force a broader rethink. |
| Charlie Munger | Concentrate only after competence, inversion, incentives, business quality, and survivability have been attacked from multiple angles ([Munger mental models](../investors/002-charlie-munger/mental-models.md)). | Sell or reduce when the competence claim is disproved, the business thesis weakens, leverage/liquidity changes, incentives deteriorate, or opportunity cost overwhelms tax/friction. |
| Benjamin Graham | Prefer diversified statistical baskets and special situations; no forced-selling leverage; size around group expectancy rather than single-name certainty ([Graham mental models](../investors/003-benjamin-graham/mental-models.md)). | Predefine exit before entry: profit objective, catalyst realization, or maximum holding period. Raise cash/bonds rather than lower standards when bargains disappear. |
| Peter Lynch | Broad active portfolio with starter positions, additions as facts improve, and category-aware sizing; tenbagger math allows many small errors ([Lynch mental models](../investors/004-peter-lynch/mental-models.md)). | Sell by category: growth runway maturing, cyclical peak recognized, turnaround thesis failing, valuation outrunning story, or original story no longer true. |
| George Soros | Small for hypotheses, medium when feedback loop is visible, large only with catalyst, liquidity, and explicit wrong-way loss budget ([Soros mental models](../investors/005-george-soros/mental-models.md)). | Exit when misconception is recognized, policy capacity changes, feedback channel stops, or timing risk exceeds holding capacity. |
| Stanley Druckenmiller | Large only when causal variable, timing, liquidity, downside, and portfolio matrix align; never size from desperation ([Druckenmiller mental models](../investors/006-stanley-druckenmiller/mental-models.md)). | Cut immediately when thesis breaks; let P&L interrogate the model; reduce mandate or return capital when scale damages the edge. |
| Jim Simons | Size by expected value net of costs, volatility, liquidity, impact, correlation, financing, and capacity; cap strategy to edge ([Simons mental models](../investors/007-jim-simons/mental-models.md)). | Let the model exit when forecast decays or risk contribution changes; override only for financing, counterparty, market-closure, regulatory, or survival risks. |
| Howard Marks | Controlled concentration inside specialized credit mandates; size by range of outcomes, downside severity, liquidity, fund life, and client fit ([Marks mental models](../investors/008-howard-marks/mental-models.md)). | Sell when forward risk-adjusted return worsens, success probability declines, thesis is less valid, or a superior opportunity exists; fund lives and withdrawals can force action. |
| Seth Klarman | Size by downside, liquidity, confidence, and substitution against existing risk; hold cash with a job; avoid recourse leverage ([Klarman mental models](../investors/009-seth-klarman/mental-models.md)). | Sell at conservative value, when thesis breaks, when a better opportunity demands capital, or when process/stakeholder risk becomes legitimacy risk. |
| John Templeton | Use diversified global or thematic baskets when pessimism is broad and single-name knowledge is imperfect; governance must tolerate long underperformance ([Templeton synthesis](../investors/010-john-templeton/synthesis.md)). | Sell when popularity changes the odds, price reaches fair value, or a broad reversal/lockup thesis is recognized; keep founder-era claims source-labeled until ledgers are found. |

## Who Contradicts Whom, And Why Both Got Rich

**Graham contradicts Buffett/Munger.** Graham's mature method says cheap, diversified, rules-based portfolios are safer than heroic business judgment. Buffett and Munger say business quality can itself be the margin of safety, and that rare high-quality assets deserve concentration. Both worked because Graham's method exploited neglect in small, ugly securities, while Buffett and Munger adapted value investing to scale, taxes, and long-duration business economics.

**Lynch contradicts Klarman.** Lynch embraced broad equity participation, everyday leads, and a willingness to own many growth stories. Klarman accepts inactivity, cash, private claims, and complex process assets. Both worked because Lynch's Magellan machine could harvest public-company growth and liquidity at scale, while Klarman's Baupost machine was built to exploit dislocation, complexity, and forced sellers.

**Soros/Druckenmiller contradict Buffett/Munger.** The macro traders size around regime breaks and reverse quickly; Buffett and Munger buy businesses they hope not to sell. Both worked because their holding periods and evidence types differ. A policy peg can break in a week; a consumer franchise can compound for decades. Applying the wrong sell rule to the wrong thesis is the error.

**Simons contradicts almost everyone.** The human investors use narrative, accounting, incentives, and judgment; Simons institutionalized systematic prediction and cost-aware portfolio machinery. Both sides got rich because markets are not one game. Some inefficiencies are legal, behavioral, balance-sheet, or institutional. Others are small, repeatable statistical edges available only to a research organization with data, execution, and capacity discipline.

**Templeton contradicts home-country quality concentration.** Templeton's edge came from widening the global search field and buying diversified pessimism. Buffett and Munger narrowed toward known quality. Both worked because each solved a different problem: Templeton fought parochialism and crisis extrapolation; Buffett/Munger fought false cheapness and mediocre economics.

## Transferable Patterns For The Canon

The first cohort argues for a practical canon built around **fit**. Edge must fit vehicle, vehicle must fit investor base, sizing must fit uncertainty, sell discipline must fit thesis half-life, and evidence must fit the source quality of the claim. No single investor can be copied whole. A reader can copy Buffett's owner mindset, Munger's inversion, Graham's margin of safety, Lynch's story verification, Soros's feedback-loop map, Druckenmiller's kill criteria, Simons's friction/capacity discipline, Marks's risk-temperature language, Klarman's downside case, and Templeton's global pessimism search. But copying any one of them without copying the structure that made the behavior survivable is how the lesson becomes a trap.

The cohort also gives the Canon its first taxonomy of failure. Value investors fail through stale cheapness, cash drag, and under-adaptation. Quality investors fail through overconfidence in durability. Macro investors fail through timing, leverage, and public-policy exposure. Quant investors fail through overfit, crowding, infrastructure, and product mismatch. Credit/distress investors fail through liquidity mismatch, process duration, and stakeholder under-underwriting. Public mutual-fund stock pickers fail through scale, investor behavior, and category drift.

Finally, the first cohort makes "luck versus skill" more precise. Skill appears in repeated process coherence before outcomes: written rules, vehicle design, risk limits, post-mortems, and adaptation. Luck appears in era, country, capital structure, opportunity set, survivorship, source visibility, and the first few successes that attract patient capital. The Canon should score both. Buffett without postwar U.S. capitalism, Simons without data/computing frontiers, Templeton without under-researched global markets, Graham without neglected filings, and Klarman without patient private capital would not be identical investors. Their skill was partly the ability to meet those conditions with a structure that could exploit them.
